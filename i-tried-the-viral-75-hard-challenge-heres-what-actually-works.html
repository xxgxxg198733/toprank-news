I Tried the Viral '75 Hard' Challenge. Here's What Actually Works

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 20, 2026 · 6 min read

Let me be real with you: I am not a fitness influencer. I'm a regular guy in his early 30s who sits at a desk too much, drinks more coffee than water, and has been 'starting next Monday' with his health goals since approximately 2019. But in April 2026, I decided to actually do something I'd been seeing all over my feed for years: the 75 Hard challenge. Created by entrepreneur Andy Frisella in 2019, it's a 75-day mental toughness program that has become basically a rite of passage in the fitness world. I figured if thousands of people could do it, so could I. Spoiler: I finished. Barely. And I have strong opinions about what's worth keeping and what's just trendy garbage.

For those who haven't encountered it, here's the deal. For 75 days straight, you must: (1) follow a diet of your choice with no cheat meals, (2) do two 45-minute workouts per day (one must be outside), (3) drink a gallon of water, (4) read 10 pages of a non-fiction book, and (5) take a progress photo. Miss any task? You start over from day one. No exceptions. It's brutal. It's designed to be brutal. Frisella calls it 'a 75-day mental transformation, not a fitness program.' And honestly? He's not wrong.

Week 1: The Honeymoon Phase

Day one was easy. I was motivated, excited, and full of that energy you get when you start something new. I woke up at 6 AM, did a 45-minute yoga class at home, drank half my gallon by noon, and read 10 pages of 'Atomic Habits' while eating a salad for lunch. Afternoon workout was a 45-minute run. I felt like a superhero. Day two was similar. Day three, I started getting annoyed at having to carry a gallon jug everywhere. Day four, I had a work dinner and had to explain to colleagues why I was drinking water instead of wine. Day five, I was sore. Day six, I was bored. Day seven, I almost quit.

The hardest part of the first week wasn't the workouts or the diet. It was the water. A gallon (3.8 liters) is a lot of liquid. I was peeing every 45 minutes. I woke up twice a night to pee. My skin felt weirdly bloated. I read online that this is common — your body adjusts after about two weeks. But those first seven days were a constant battle with my bladder. I started timing my water intake so I wouldn't have to pee during meetings. It was absurd.

Weeks 2-4: The Grind Sets In

By week two, I had a routine. Wake up, workout, work, second workout, read, sleep. The repetition was both comforting and maddening. The outdoor workout rule was harder than I expected. It rained for three straight days in week three. I did a 45-minute walk in the rain, soaked and miserable, thinking 'this is stupid.' But I did it. And that's sort of the point. The challenge forces you to do things when you don't want to. When it's inconvenient. When you have a million excuses.

The diet I chose was Mediterranean — lean proteins, vegetables, whole grains, healthy fats. No alcohol, no processed sugar, no fried food. The no alcohol part was eye-opening. I'm not a heavy drinker, but a glass of wine with dinner or a beer after work was a habit I didn't realize I had. By week three, I felt noticeably better. My sleep improved. My skin cleared up. I had more consistent energy throughout the day. I wasn't even tempted by the office donuts anymore.

Weeks 5-7: The Dark Middle

This is where most people quit. I read that the average dropout rate for 75 Hard is around 60%. I almost became one of them. Week five, I got sick. Nothing serious — a cold — but the rules say no exceptions. So I did two workouts while coughing and feeling like garbage. I did my outdoor walk in 40-degree weather because I was too congested to run. I cried a little. I'm not ashamed to admit it. It felt pointless. But I kept reminding myself that this is exactly what the challenge is testing: will you quit when things get hard?

Week six, I had a social event — a friend's birthday dinner. Everyone was drinking, eating pizza, having fun. I sat there with my water and a grilled chicken salad. I felt left out. I felt like I was missing out. But I also felt proud that I could say no. By week seven, the challenge had stopped feeling like a punishment and started feeling like a choice. I was choosing to do this. Nobody was forcing me. That shift in mindset was the real transformation.

Weeks 8-10: The Final Stretch

The last three weeks were anticlimactic in the best way. The routines had become habits. I didn't have to remind myself to drink water — I just did. The workouts were part of my day, like brushing my teeth. I'd finished four books ('Atomic Habits,' 'Deep Work,' 'Man's Search for Meaning,' and 'The Power of Now'). I'd lost about 12 pounds and visibly gained muscle definition. But the biggest change was mental. I felt more disciplined, more focused, less reactive. I could sit with discomfort without immediately trying to escape it. That's a skill I didn't know I was missing.

Day 75, I took my final progress photo. I looked different. Not just physically — I looked more confident. More grounded. I celebrated by going for a long hike with a friend and eating a burger (which was good, but honestly, not as amazing as I'd hyped it up to be). I felt weirdly sad it was over. The structure had become comforting.

What I'd Change (And What I'd Keep)

Okay, here's my honest assessment. 75 Hard is not for everyone. The rigid rules are designed for people who struggle with consistency. If you're already disciplined, you might find it tedious. If you're a perfectionist, the 'start over' rule could be psychologically damaging. I saw people on Reddit who failed on day 74 and were devastated. That's not healthy. The challenge should be about building habits, not punishing yourself for a slip-up.

What I'd keep: the two workouts (one outdoor, one indoor), the daily reading, and the no-cheat-meal rule (within reason). These genuinely transformed my productivity and well-being. What I'd change: the gallon of water. It's excessive. Most nutritionists recommend 2-3 liters for an adult male. The gallon thing is just macho posturing. Also, the progress photos are unnecessary for most people. They can trigger body dysmorphia. Take them if you want, but don't make them mandatory.

Would I recommend 75 Hard? Yes, but with caveats. If you're someone who struggles with follow-through, who makes excuses, who says 'I'll start Monday' — this might be exactly what you need. But if you're prone to anxiety, disordered eating, or all-or-nothing thinking, please skip it. There are gentler ways to build discipline. The challenge is a tool, not a solution. Use it if it fits. Leave it if it doesn't.

As for me? I'm not doing it again. But I'm keeping the habits. I still do two workouts most days. I still read before bed. I still drink more water than I used to. And when I feel like quitting something hard, I remember week five, walking in the rain, and I keep going. That's the gift the challenge gave me. It's not about the 75 days. It's about what you take with you after.
