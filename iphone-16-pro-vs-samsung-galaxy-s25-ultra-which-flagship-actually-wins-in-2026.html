iPhone 16 Pro vs. Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra: Which Flagship Actually Wins in 2026?

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 23, 2026 · 5 min read

It's July 2026, and the flagship phone wars are, frankly, exhausting. Every year Apple and Samsung release their "best phone ever" with incremental upgrades and marketing that could sell ice to penguins. But this year felt different. The iPhone 16 Pro and Galaxy S25 Ultra both launched with real changes — not just a new color and a slightly faster chip.

I've been using both phones as my daily drivers since April. I swapped SIMs, carried two devices, and took notes on everything. Battery life. Camera performance. Software quirks. Even the feel in my hand. Here's what I found.

Design and Build: One Is a Brick, One Is a Beauty

The iPhone 16 Pro is noticeably lighter than last year. Apple shaved off about 10 grams by using a new titanium alloy frame. It's 199 grams now, which feels just right. The edges are slightly more rounded, and the frosted glass back doesn't slip as much as before. It's a refinement of a design that's been around since the iPhone 12, but it works.

The S25 Ultra, on the other hand, is a rectangle. A sharp, angular, imposing rectangle. Samsung ditched the curved display entirely. The result is a phone that looks like a mini tablet. It's 219 grams — heavier than the iPhone — but the weight distribution is good. The flat screen makes it easier to use with an S Pen, which is still included in the box. That alone gives Samsung a win for note-takers.

Winner: iPhone 16 Pro for comfort. S25 Ultra for features.

Display: Samsung's OLED Still Reigns

Let's be real: Samsung makes the best smartphone displays in the world. The S25 Ultra has a 6.9-inch Dynamic AMOLED 2X with a 120Hz refresh rate and a peak brightness of 3,000 nits. It's stunning. Colors are vibrant without being cartoonish. HDR content looks incredible. The anti-reflective coating, introduced with the S24 Ultra, is back and improved — I could read this phone in direct sunlight without squinting.

The iPhone 16 Pro has a 6.3-inch LTPO OLED (6.9-inch on the Pro Max). Apple finally caught up with 3,000 nits peak brightness too, but the bezels are slightly thicker. The color accuracy is class-leading, as always, but it just doesn't pop like the Samsung. In everyday use, both are excellent. But if you watch a lot of HDR video, the Samsung wins.

Winner: S25 Ultra

Camera: A Split Decision

This is the big one. The iPhone 16 Pro has a 48MP main sensor, a 48MP ultrawide, and a 12MP 5x telephoto. The ultrawide upgrade is significant — it's much sharper than the 12MP sensor on the 15 Pro. Low-light shots are cleaner, with less noise. The 5x telephoto is good, but not great. Apple still relies heavily on computational photography to make up for the smaller sensor.

The S25 Ultra has a 200MP main sensor, a 50MP ultrawide, and two telephotos: a 50MP 5x and a 12MP 3x. The 200MP sensor is overkill for most people, but the 50MP ultrawide is genuinely useful for landscape and architecture shots. The 5x telephoto is sharper than Apple's. Samsung's AI processing has improved — skin tones are less saturated, and the "over-sharpened" look from previous years is mostly gone.

Here's the thing: for point-and-shoot, the iPhone is more consistent. Photos look natural, balanced, and true to life. The Samsung sometimes over-processes, especially in low light. But if you zoom in, crop, or edit, Samsung's higher resolution gives you more flexibility.

Winner: Tie. iPhone for consistency, Samsung for versatility.

Performance and Battery: Apple Silicon vs. Snapdragon

The iPhone 16 Pro runs on the A18 Pro chip, built on a 3nm process. It's fast. Really fast. Apps open instantly, games run at 60fps with max settings, and thermal throttling is minimal. The battery is 3,500mAh (smaller than Samsung's), but Apple's efficiency means it lasts a full day with moderate use. I got about 7-8 hours of screen-on time.

The S25 Ultra has the Snapdragon 8 Gen 4 (custom for Galaxy). It's also 3nm, and benchmarks show it's slightly ahead of the A18 Pro in multi-core performance. In real-world use, I couldn't tell the difference. Both are blazing fast. The Samsung has a 5,000mAh battery, and it shows — I consistently got 9-10 hours of screen-on time. That's a full day of heavy use and then some.

Winner: S25 Ultra for battery life. Tie for raw speed.

Software: iOS vs. One UI — It's Personal

iOS 20 (yes, we're at 20 now) is stable, smooth, and boring. The same app grid, the same Control Center, the same everything. Apple added some AI features — Siri can now summarize articles and generate images — but they're not revolutionary. The ecosystem is the real draw: iMessage, AirDrop, seamless integration with Mac and iPad.

Samsung's One UI 7 is based on Android 16. It's feature-rich, customizable, and surprisingly polished. The Galaxy AI features are more useful than Apple's: real-time call translation, AI photo editing that actually works, and smart suggestions based on your routine. Samsung has also committed to seven years of software updates, matching Apple's promise.

If you're in the Apple ecosystem, the iPhone is the obvious choice. If you want flexibility and features, Samsung wins.

Winner: Personal preference. Objectively, Samsung offers more features.

Price and Value

The iPhone 16 Pro starts at $1,099. The S25 Ultra starts at $1,199. That $100 difference is notable, but Samsung frequently has trade-in deals and discounts. I've seen the S25 Ultra drop to under $800 with a trade-in. Apple rarely discounts.

Both phones are expensive. But the Samsung gives you more hardware (S Pen, bigger battery, higher resolution cameras) for the price.

Winner: S25 Ultra (slightly, depending on deals)

The Verdict

If you asked me in April which phone I'd keep, I'd have said the iPhone. The consistency, the ecosystem, the familiar feel. But after three months, I'm leaning toward the S25 Ultra. The battery life alone is a game-changer. The display is better. The camera versatility is real. And One UI has become genuinely pleasant to use.

That said, if you're heavily invested in Apple's ecosystem — AirPods, Mac, iPad, Apple Watch — don't switch. The integration is still unmatched. But if you're open to either platform, the S25 Ultra is the better phone this year.

My pick: S25 Ultra by a narrow margin.
