I Tried the Samsung Galaxy Ring for a Week – Here's What Nobody Tells You

TR
By Rachel Greene · July 15, 2026 · 3 min read

The Ring That's Supposed to Change Everything

Last Monday, Samsung officially launched the Galaxy Ring at their Unpacked event, and I got my hands on one for a full week. The hype is real – this thing is tiny, light, and packed with sensors. But does it actually work? After seven days of wearing it alongside my Apple Watch Ultra, I have some answers. Let's get into it.

The Good: Sleep Tracking That Actually Works

The biggest selling point of the Galaxy Ring is sleep tracking. And I have to admit, it's impressive. The ring uses a combination of heart rate, skin temperature, and movement sensors to track your sleep stages. The results were spot on compared to my Apple Watch. But here's the thing: the ring is so lightweight (just 2.3 grams) that I forgot I was wearing it. No bulky strap, no uncomfortable clasp. I slept better because I wasn't thinking about the device. The morning report gives you a 'Sleep Score' from 0-100, and it actually helped me improve my habits. I started going to bed earlier because I wanted a higher score. It's a weird motivator but it works.

The Bad: Notifications Are a Pain

The ring can show notifications – but it's terrible at it. You get a small vibration on your finger, and then you have to look at your phone to see what it was. Half the time I missed it. The companion app, Samsung Health, is decent but not great. It takes about 10 seconds to sync, which feels slow when you're in a hurry. And if you have an iPhone? Forget it. The ring only works with Samsung phones, which is a huge limitation. I'm using a Galaxy S25 for this test, but most people don't have one.

The Ugly: Battery Life Is Overhyped

Samsung claims the ring lasts 7 days on a charge. In my testing, I got 5 days – and that's with sleep tracking on and notifications off. The charging case is tiny, which is nice, but you have to remember to charge it every few days. If you're the type of person who forgets to charge your phone, this ring will die on you. Also, the ring is water-resistant to 100 meters, but I wouldn't swim with it. The sensors are sensitive, and I'm paranoid about damage.

How It Compares to Oura and Whoop

The Oura Ring Gen 4 is the main competitor. I've worn an Oura for two years, and the Galaxy Ring beats it on sensors – Samsung has more advanced bio-sensors, including a new optical heart rate sensor that's more accurate during exercise. But Oura's app is much better. The insights are deeper, the UI is cleaner, and the community features are more engaging. Whoop is still the best for athletes, but the ring format is more convenient than a band. If you're a casual fitness user, the Galaxy Ring is a good choice. If you're serious about data, stick with Oura.

The Verdict: Should You Buy It?

If you have a Samsung phone and you want a lightweight sleep tracker, the Galaxy Ring is a solid buy at $399. But if you're on an iPhone or you need detailed workout metrics, skip it. The ring is a first-generation product, and it shows. The navigation is clunky, the app needs work, and the battery life is average. But the potential is huge. I can see a future where rings replace watches completely. Just not yet.
