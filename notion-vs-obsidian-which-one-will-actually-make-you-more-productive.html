Notion vs Obsidian: Which One Will Actually Make You More Productive?

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 20, 2026 · 6 min read

Let me start with a confession: I have a serious problem with productivity apps. I've tried them all — Todoist, Roam Research, Bear, Evernote (RIP to my 2014 self), even a weird one called Capacities that I found on Hacker News. But for the last six months, I've been doing a proper experiment. I used Notion for everything in Q1 2026, then switched entirely to Obsidian for Q2. And I mean everything. Work notes, personal journal, project management, even my grocery list. I wanted to know: which one actually makes you more productive, and which one just makes you feel productive?

Spoiler: the answer surprised me. But first, let's set the stage. Both Notion and Obsidian are incredibly powerful. Both have passionate communities. Both can do almost anything you want, if you're willing to learn. But they approach the problem of personal knowledge management from completely different angles. And depending on your brain type, one will feel like a superpower and the other like a chore.

The Setup: What Each App Does Best

Notion is the Swiss Army knife of productivity. It's a database, a wiki, a project manager, and a note-taker all rolled into one. You can build anything — a company's entire internal documentation, a habit tracker, a content calendar, a recipe book. The interface is beautiful. It's what I'd call a 'structured' tool. You decide the structure upfront (tables, databases, templates), and then fill in the content.

Obsidian, on the other hand, is a plain-text note-taking app built around the concept of linked thinking. It's based on local Markdown files — nothing is stored in the cloud by default. The killer feature is the graph view, which shows connections between your notes. It's inspired by the Zettelkasten method (German for 'slip box', a note-taking system used by a hyper-productive sociologist named Niklas Luhmann). Obsidian is unstructured chaos until you impose order. It's for people who think in networks, not hierarchies.

Where Notion Wins (And It's a Lot of Places)

Notion absolutely crushes it when you need to organize complex information with a team. If you're running a small business, a content team, or a project with multiple stakeholders, Notion is the clear winner. The database functionality is unmatched. You can create a 'Tasks' database linked to a 'Projects' database and a 'Team Members' database. Filter, sort, view as a calendar, a gallery, a board. It's flexible enough to handle almost any workflow.

I used Notion to plan a 3-week Europe trip (the one I wrote about in the previous article). I had a database for cities, one for accommodations, one for restaurants, one for activities. I linked them all. I could see my entire trip on a timeline view. It was beautiful, organized, and genuinely helpful. For collaborative work, Notion is hard to beat. Real-time editing, comments, permissions — it just works.

But here's the dark side. Notion can become a time sink. I spent hours tweaking templates, adjusting colors, and reorganizing databases. The app is so flexible that you can spend all your time building the perfect system instead of actually doing the work. It's the tool equivalent of spending 3 hours organizing your desk instead of writing the report. I fell into this trap hard. I had a 'Dashboard' page that I kept perfecting, but it never felt done. There was always one more thing to add.

Where Obsidian Wins (And Why It's Growing So Fast)

Obsidian is the opposite. It forces you to write first, organize later. The default is a simple text file. You link notes using double brackets [[like this]]. There's no fancy database upfront. You just write. And over time, connections emerge naturally. The graph view is not a gimmick — it genuinely helps you see patterns in your thinking. I've been using it for three months and I've already found connections between ideas I never would have noticed otherwise.

The biggest advantage of Obsidian is speed. It launches in under a second on my M2 MacBook Air. Searching through thousands of notes is instantaneous. There's no loading screen, no spinning wheel. It's all local. That matters more than I expected. When I want to capture a thought, I want it to be frictionless. Obsidian is frictionless. Notion feels like opening a heavy door.

Another huge win: Obsidian is future-proof. Because everything is stored as plain Markdown files on your computer, you're never locked in. If Obsidian shuts down tomorrow (unlikely, but possible), I still have all my notes in a format I can open with any text editor. Notion? Good luck exporting those complex databases. They've improved their export, but it's still messy. I've heard horror stories from people trying to leave Notion and losing formatting, linked databases, and media.

The 2026 Updates That Changed Everything

Both apps have had major updates this year. Notion finally added offline mode in a meaningful way — you can now edit pages without internet, and they sync when you reconnect. Hallelujah. This was my biggest complaint for years. They also improved their AI features (built on GPT-4), which can summarize notes, generate content, and suggest actions. I used it to draft meeting notes. It works, but it's not revolutionary.

Obsidian released version 1.8 in early 2026, which brought native tables (finally!) and a much-improved mobile app. The mobile app was always the weak link — slow, clunky, hard to navigate. The new version is still not as smooth as Notion's mobile app, but it's genuinely usable now. They also launched Obsidian Sync 2.0, which is faster and more reliable. I've been using it to sync between my laptop and phone. It works, though it costs $10/month (or you can use iCloud, which is free but slower).

The Verdict: Who Should Use Which?

After six months of real testing, here's my honest take. If you work in a team or need to manage complex projects with databases, use Notion. It's the better tool for structured collaboration. The learning curve is worth it. But be honest with yourself: are you actually building something complex, or are you just wanting to build a pretty system? If it's the latter, save yourself the time and pick something simpler.

If you're a solo creator, a writer, a student, or someone who thinks in connections rather than hierarchies, use Obsidian. It will feel weird for the first week. You'll miss drag-and-drop databases and pretty templates. But after a month, you'll wonder how you ever used anything else. The depth of thought it enables is real. I've written more in the last three months than in the previous year, and I credit Obsidian's frictionless design.

My personal setup now: Obsidian for personal notes, journaling, writing, and idea development. Notion for client projects and collaborative databases. They're not competitors in my workflow — they're complementary. But if I had to pick just one? Obsidian. Because at the end of the day, productivity is about thinking better, not organizing more. And Obsidian makes me think better.
