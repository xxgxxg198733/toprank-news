I Went to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Final in Mexico City — Here's What Happened

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 22, 2026 · 7 min read

I'm not a soccer fan. I mean, I watch the World Cup every four years like everyone else, but I couldn't name the starting lineup of any national team. So when my friend Diego called me in March and said, "I got tickets to the final in Mexico City. You're coming," I almost said no. I had work deadlines. I had a cat that needed feeding. I had a million reasons to stay home.

I said yes. And it was the best decision I've made all year.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup final was held at the Estadio Azteca in Mexico City on July 19, 2026 — the first time the final has been played in North America since 1994. The match was Argentina vs. Portugal, a clash of titans that had Messi (still playing at 39) facing off against Cristiano Ronaldo (41) in what everyone called a "farewell match" for both legends. Argentina won 3-2 in extra time, with Messi scoring a free kick in the 117th minute that I swear bent around the wall like it had a mind of its own. The stadium erupted. I cried. So did the guy next to me. So did Diego. It was pure, unfiltered joy.

But the match was only part of the story. The week I spent in Mexico City was the real adventure. Here's what I learned — the stuff the guidebooks don't tell you.

The City Is a Sensory Overload (In the Best Way)

I landed at Benito Juárez International Airport on a Tuesday afternoon, and the first thing I noticed was the air — thick with smog, exhaust, and the smell of street food. It's not pleasant, exactly, but it's alive. The second thing I noticed was the noise. A constant hum of traffic, music, shouting, and laughter. Mexico City is the loudest city I've ever visited, and I've been to Tokyo, Cairo, and New York. It's not a bug; it's a feature. The city vibrates with energy.

I stayed in a small Airbnb in the Condesa neighborhood, a leafy, art-deco district that felt like a refuge from the chaos. My host, a graphic designer named Elena, left me a map with hand-drawn annotations: "Best tacos al pastor — El Huequito (avoid the place on the corner, it's tourist trap)" and "Don't drink the tap water, even for brushing teeth" and "If a mariachi band approaches you in a restaurant, they will charge you $200 pesos per song. Say no unless you want to pay." Solid advice, all of it.

The Food Is the Best in the World (And I'm Not Exaggerating)

Before this trip, I thought I knew Mexican food. I was wrong. I knew Americanized Tex-Mex — burritos, nachos, chimichangas. Real Mexican food is something else entirely. I ate at a taco stand in the Mercado de San Juan that served tacos de lengua (beef tongue) that were so tender and flavorful, I almost cried. The tortillas were handmade, the salsa was smoky and spicy, and the whole thing cost $1.50. I ate three.

I tried tlacoyos — blue corn masa cakes stuffed with beans and cheese, topped with nopales (cactus) and salsa. I had mole poblano at a restaurant called Pujol, which is consistently ranked one of the best in the world. The mole had 30 ingredients and took three days to make. It was the most complex, layered dish I've ever eaten. I had churros from a street vendor in the Roma neighborhood that were crispy on the outside, soft on the inside, and dusted with cinnamon sugar. I ate so much that I had to buy new pants.

The World Cup itself brought a culinary carnival. Every block had a street vendor selling esquites (corn with mayo, cheese, and chili), elotes (same but on the cob), and tortas (sandwiches). There were pop-up stalls selling "tacos de canasta" — basket tacos that are steamed in a basket and filled with potatoes, beans, or chicharrón. They're cheap, filling, and perfect for eating while walking to the stadium.

The Fans Are Intense (But Mostly Friendly)

I've been to sporting events before — Super Bowls, NBA finals, college football games. Nothing prepared me for the energy of a World Cup final in Mexico. The Estadio Azteca holds 87,000 people, and every single one of them was screaming. The noise was physical — I could feel it in my chest. When Messi scored the winning goal, the sound was so loud that my ears rang for an hour afterward.

But here's what surprised me: despite the intensity, I never felt unsafe. Yes, there were rival fans shouting at each other. Yes, there were flares and smoke bombs. But I saw strangers hugging after the match, regardless of which team they supported. A group of Portuguese fans bought me a beer at a bar after the game, even though their team lost. "It's football," one of them said, shrugging. "Next time, maybe we win." That spirit of camaraderie is something I'll never forget.

That said, a few tips for anyone planning to attend a major event in Mexico City: Don't wear expensive jewelry or watches. Keep your phone in your front pocket. Use only official taxis or Uber (I had a friend who got scammed by an unlicensed cab driver). And learn a few phrases in Spanish — "gracias," "por favor," "dónde está el baño" — it goes a long way.

The Culture Is Ancient and Modern at the Same Time

Mexico City is built on top of Tenochtitlan, the Aztec capital. You can't escape history here. I visited the Templo Mayor, the ruins of the main Aztec temple, which sits right next to the Metropolitan Cathedral — a literal collision of indigenous and Spanish colonial worlds. The museum next to the ruins is extraordinary, with artifacts that tell the story of a civilization that was brutally destroyed. It's humbling and heartbreaking.

But the city isn't stuck in the past. The Roma and Condesa neighborhoods are full of contemporary art galleries, trendy coffee shops, and innovative restaurants. I spent an afternoon at the Museo Soumaya, a futuristic silver building that houses a massive collection of art, including works by Rodin, Dalí, and Van Gogh. It's free on Sundays. The contrast between the ancient and the modern is what makes Mexico City so unique — you can walk from a 700-year-old pyramid to a rooftop bar with craft cocktails in 15 minutes.

The Practical Stuff: What You Need to Know

If you're planning to visit Mexico City for a major event (or just because), here's the practical advice I wish I'd had:

Book early. I booked my Airbnb four months in advance, and prices for the World Cup week were already 3x normal rates. Flights from New York were $800 round-trip — I paid $1,200 because I waited until May.

The altitude is real. Mexico City is at 7,350 feet above sea level. The first two days, I got winded walking up a flight of stairs. Drink lots of water, avoid alcohol for the first 24 hours, and take it easy. I saw a guy pass out at the stadium because he didn't hydrate.

Use the Metro. Mexico City's subway system is cheap ($0.25 per ride) and extensive. It's crowded, but it's faster and cheaper than taxis. Just be aware of pickpockets — keep your valuables in a zippered bag.

Learn to love street food. It's safe (trust me, I ate it every day and never got sick), delicious, and cheap. But use common sense: go to stalls that are busy, where the food is cooked fresh, and where the vendor wears gloves.

Don't skip the museums. The National Museum of Anthropology is one of the best museums in the world. The Frida Kahlo Museum (Casa Azul) is a pilgrimage site for art lovers. The Palacio de Bellas Artes has incredible murals by Diego Rivera. Plan at least one museum day.

Would I Do It Again?

In a heartbeat. The World Cup final was the most exhilarating live sports experience I've ever had. But Mexico City itself was the real star. The food, the culture, the history, the people — it's a city that demands to be experienced, not just visited. I'm already planning a return trip for Day of the Dead (Día de los Muertos) in October. If you haven't been, add it to your bucket list. And if you're going for a big event, prepare to be overwhelmed — in the best possible way.

I'll never forget that moment in the 117th minute, when Messi curled the ball into the top corner and 87,000 people lost their minds. I was one of them. And for a few seconds, I understood what it means to be part of something bigger than yourself. That's not just travel. That's magic.
