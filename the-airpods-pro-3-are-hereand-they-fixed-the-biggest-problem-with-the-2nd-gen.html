The AirPods Pro 3 Are Here—And They Fixed the Biggest Problem with the 2nd Gen

TR
By Ryan Cooper · July 16, 2026 · 6 min read

Apple launched the AirPods Pro 3 on June 30, 2026, and I've had a pair glued to my ears ever since. I've been a fan of the AirPods line since the original Pros came out in 2019—they're not perfect, but they're the most convenient earbuds I've ever used. The 2nd gen, released in 2022, improved sound quality and added adaptive transparency mode, which was genuinely useful. But they had one glaring problem that drove me crazy: the fit.

I have weird ears. I've tried every size of silicone tip Apple provides, and none of them stay put. The Pros would start to slip out after about 20 minutes of walking, and forget about running—they'd fall out within a block. The AirPods Pro 3 promise to fix this with a completely redesigned shape and a new fit system. And you know what? They actually deliver.

The Fit Is Finally Right

The biggest change with the AirPods Pro 3 is the shape. Instead of the bulbous design that's been essentially the same since 2019, the new model has a more elongated stem and a slightly smaller, more angular body that sits deeper in your ear. Apple says they used 3D scans of thousands of ears to design the new shape. I don't know if that's true, but it works.

I wore them for a three-mile run yesterday, and they didn't budge. Not once. I even shook my head aggressively at the end to test them (yes, I looked ridiculous). They stayed put. The new ear tips come in four sizes, including an extra-small that finally works for people with smaller ear canals. The tips are also made of a new material that's grippier without being uncomfortable. It's a small change, but it makes a huge difference.

Noise Cancellation: A Noticeable Step Up

The AirPods Pro 2 already had excellent active noise cancellation—arguably the best in the true wireless category. The 3rd gen improves on it with a new H3 chip that Apple says processes noise-canceling algorithms 40% faster. In practice, this means the ANC is more aggressive at blocking low-frequency sounds like airplane engines, air conditioners, and the hum of a coffee shop.

I tested them on a flight from New York to Chicago last week. With the 2nd gen, I could still hear a faint drone from the engines. With the 3rd gen, that drone is almost completely gone. I could listen to a podcast at 50% volume without any interference. It's not quite Sony WF-1000XM5 level—those are still the kings of ANC—but it's close. For most people, the difference won't be noticeable unless you're in a very noisy environment.

Transparency mode is also improved. Apple calls it "Adaptive Transparency 2.0," and it now automatically reduces loud sounds like sirens or construction noise while keeping ambient sounds natural. It's seamless and uncanny. I walked through a construction zone and the jackhammer sound was muffled, but I could still hear a conversation happening nearby. It feels like magic, but it's just really good signal processing.

Sound Quality: Better, But Not a Revolution

The AirPods Pro 3 sound slightly better than the 2nd gen, but let's be honest—if you're buying AirPods for audiophile-level sound, you're buying the wrong product. The bass is a touch punchier, the mids are clearer, and the highs are less harsh. Apple says they've improved the driver and the acoustic chamber, and I believe them. But compared to something like the Sennheiser Momentum True Wireless 4 or even the Samsung Galaxy Buds3 Pro, the AirPods still sound a bit thin.

Where they excel is in integration with Apple's ecosystem. The new Personalized Spatial Audio with dynamic head tracking is genuinely impressive—it feels like the sound is coming from the room around you, not from your ears. When I watched the new season of The Bear on Apple TV+, the audio felt immersive in a way that regular stereo doesn't. If you listen to a lot of Dolby Atmos music on Apple Music, these are the best earbuds for the job.

Battery Life Gets a Decent Bump

Apple claims 6.5 hours of listening time with ANC on, up from 6 hours on the 2nd gen. In my testing, I got about 6 hours and 10 minutes before the low battery warning appeared. With the case, you get up to 30 hours total, which is the same as before. The case now supports MagSafe, Qi2, and USB-C charging. It also has a built-in speaker (like the AirPods Pro 2 case) and a lanyard loop, which I still think is silly but some people apparently love.

The case also got a useful upgrade: it's now IPX4 rated for water resistance, same as the earbuds themselves. No more worrying if you drop the case in a puddle.

New Features: Conversation Awareness and Gesture Controls

Two new software features caught my attention. Conversation Awareness pauses your music when you start talking to someone and resumes when you stop. It works surprisingly well—I tested it by muttering to myself while making coffee and the music dutifully stopped and started. The detection is fast enough that you don't miss the first syllable of your own sentence. It's a small quality-of-life improvement, but I've already come to rely on it.

The other new feature is gesture controls. You can now swipe up or down on the stem to adjust volume (that was already there), but you can also double-tap the side of the earbud to answer a call or activate Siri. It's more intuitive than squeezing the stem, which I always found awkward. You can also nod your head to answer a call or shake to decline, thanks to the new motion sensors. It feels gimmicky, but it works. I used it while my hands were full of groceries, and it felt like living in the future.

Should You Upgrade?

If you have the AirPods Pro 2, the answer is probably no—unless the fit issue is driving you crazy, or you're a heavy traveler who will appreciate the better ANC. The improvements are real but incremental. If you have the original AirPods Pro from 2019, or any older model, the upgrade is a no-brainer. The fit, sound, ANC, and features are all dramatically better.

If you're an Android user, skip these. The full experience requires an Apple device—you'll miss out on spatial audio, seamless pairing, and Siri integration. Get the Sony WF-1000XM5 or the Samsung Galaxy Buds3 Pro instead.

At $249, the AirPods Pro 3 are priced the same as the outgoing model. They're expensive, but they're the best true wireless earbuds for iPhone users, period. The fit improvement alone makes them worth it for me. After years of earbuds slowly creeping out of my ears, I finally have a pair that stays put. That's worth the price of admission.
