World's First Lab-Grown Wagyu Steak Approved for Sale in Japan

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 05, 2026 ⏱ 1 min read 360,235 views

In a historic move, Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare has approved the world's first lab-grown Wagyu beef steak for commercial sale. The cultivated meat product, produced by Tokyo-based startup IntegriCulture, is made from cells taken from a prized Japanese Black cow without slaughtering the animal.

The Cultivated Wagyu Process

IntegriCulture's 'SteakCell' technology replicates the marbling and texture of traditional Wagyu by growing fat and muscle cells on a 3D scaffold in a nutrient-rich serum. The process takes 8 weeks, compared to 30 months for a cow, using 90% less land and 80% less water. The resulting steak contains the same protein, iron, and B vitamins as conventional Wagyu.

Pricing and Availability

The lab-grown Wagyu will initially be priced at $50 per 100g serving, roughly double the cost of premium conventional Wagyu. It will launch in select high-end restaurants in Tokyo and Osaka starting July 1, 2026, before expanding to retail stores by 2027. IntegriCulture plans to scale production to reduce costs to $20 per serving within 2 years.

Reactions from Chefs and Consumers

Michelin-starred chef Yoshihiro Narisawa praised the product: 'The texture and umami are indistinguishable from the real thing. This could revolutionize fine dining.' However, consumer surveys show mixed reactions: 45% of Japanese consumers say they would try it, while 35% express concerns about 'unnatural' food production. The Japanese Wagyu Association has voiced opposition, arguing that 'true Wagyu' must come from a living animal.

This approval marks a major milestone for the cultivated meat industry, which has struggled with regulatory hurdles and high costs. With Japan's stamp of approval, other countries may follow suit.
