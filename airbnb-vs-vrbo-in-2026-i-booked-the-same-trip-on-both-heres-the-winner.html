Airbnb vs Vrbo in 2026: I Booked the Same Trip on Both — Here's the Winner

TR
By Hannah Powell · July 18, 2026 · 6 min read

I've been a loyal Airbnb user since 2014. I've stayed in treehouses in Portland, houseboats in Amsterdam, and a ridiculously overpriced tiny home in Joshua Tree. But over the past two years, I've watched the platform change — fees went up, customer service got worse, and I started hearing friends swear by Vrbo. So I decided to settle this once and for all: I booked the exact same weekend trip (July 24-27) to Asheville, North Carolina, on both platforms, then compared everything. Here's what I found.

The Setup: Same City, Same Dates, Same Budget

I picked Asheville because it's a popular mountain getaway with tons of listings on both platforms. My criteria: a two-bedroom place with a kitchen, within 15 minutes of downtown, under $300 per night before fees. I searched both sites simultaneously on July 10, 2026, and found comparable options.

Airbnb gave me a charming renovated bungalow called 'The Blue Ridge Retreat' — 2 bedrooms, full kitchen, fenced yard, rated 4.92 stars. Price: $245/night. With cleaning fee ($85), service fee ($72), and taxes ($49), the total for three nights came to $941. That's $314 per night. Ouch.

Vrbo showed a similar property — 'Mountain Laurel Cottage' — also 2 bedrooms, similar amenities, rated 4.89 stars. Price: $230/night. Cleaning fee: $65. Service fee: $55. Taxes: $42. Total: $852. That's $284 per night. Already $89 cheaper.

But price isn't everything. I booked both (don't worry, I cancelled the loser before the free cancellation period ended) and went through the entire experience.

The Booking Process: Airbnb Wins on Ease

Airbnb's app is just smoother. I had my payment info saved, the search filters worked perfectly, and I booked the bungalow in about 90 seconds. Vrbo's interface felt clunkier — more clicks to get to the same place, and the search results didn't load as quickly on my phone. Also, Vrbo tried to upsell me travel insurance twice before letting me complete the booking. Annoying.

That said, Vrbo showed me the full price breakdown upfront, including all fees, before I even clicked on a listing. Airbnb hides the cleaning and service fees until you're on the payment screen. That's deceptive and I hate it. So Vrbo gets points for transparency, even if the tech is worse.

Communication: The Hosts Made the Difference

This is where things got interesting. My Airbnb host (her name was Jenna) sent a warm welcome message immediately after booking — a PDF with restaurant recommendations, hiking trails, and her personal cell number. She also checked in two days before check-in to confirm arrival time. That's the kind of service that made Airbnb famous.

My Vrbo host (a property management company called Blue Ridge Stays) sent a generic email with a link to a digital guidebook and a stern warning about the noise ordinance. No personal touch. When I asked about early check-in, they took 8 hours to respond. Not great.

But here's the twist: the Vrbo property had a physical guestbook with handwritten notes from previous guests — one family had stayed there every year since 2019. That kind of loyalty doesn't happen by accident. The property itself was clearly well-loved.

I'll call this one a draw. Airbnb has better host communication, but Vrbo's properties seem to attract repeat guests, which says something about quality.

The Actual Properties: Vrbo's Was Nicer (Surprise)

I drove up to Asheville and visited both properties in person (with permission from the hosts). The Airbnb bungalow was cute — exposed brick, modern kitchen, nice patio with a fire pit. But it was on a busy road, and the 'fenced yard' was basically a postage stamp. The neighbors were close enough to hear conversations.

The Vrbo cottage was in a quieter neighborhood, had a proper mountain view from the deck, and the kitchen was stocked with everything — spices, oils, a French press, even a bottle of local wine as a welcome gift. The beds were memory foam (I checked the tag) and the towels were thick and fluffy. Little things that matter when you're on vacation.

I honestly didn't expect this. I assumed Airbnb, with its focus on unique stays, would have the better property. But Vrbo's listing was more 'home away from home' — less Instagrammable, but more comfortable for an actual trip.

Fees and Hidden Costs: Vrbo Cleans Up

Let's talk about the elephant in the room: fees. Airbnb's fees are out of control. On my $245/night booking, fees added up to $206 — that's 28% of the total. Vrbo's fees were $162, or 23%. Still high, but better.

And here's something I didn't expect: the Airbnb had a checkout checklist that included stripping the beds, starting the dishwasher, and taking out the trash. The Vrbo only asked me to lock the door and leave the keys. That's a big deal when you're rushing to catch a flight. Airbnb's 'chores' are becoming a meme for a reason.

I also checked damage deposits. Airbnb required a $500 hold on my credit card (released after 14 days). Vrbo required a $200 hold (released after 7 days). That's $300 less tied up for a week.

Customer Service: The Real Test

I intentionally created a problem to test support. I reported a minor issue — the Wi-Fi password was wrong — to both platforms. Airbnb responded in 12 minutes via in-app chat and had the host text me the correct password within 30 minutes. Vrbo's phone support kept me on hold for 22 minutes, then the agent told me to contact the host directly. When I said the host wasn't responding, they said they'd 'escalate' and I never heard back. The Wi-Fi issue was eventually resolved by the host, but Vrbo's support was useless.

That said, Vrbo's cancellation policy was more flexible for this property — free cancellation up to 48 hours before check-in, versus Airbnb's 5-day policy. If plans change, Vrbo is better.

The Winner: It Depends — But I'm Switching to Vrbo for Family Trips

After all this, I have to give the edge to Vrbo for my Asheville trip. The property was better, the fees were lower, and the overall value was higher. But Airbnb is still better for solo or couple trips where you want a unique experience and good host communication.

For families, groups, or anyone who actually wants to relax (not do chores), Vrbo is the move. For adventurers and design lovers, stick with Airbnb. Me? I'm moving my family vacations to Vrbo. The $89 I saved on this trip alone is enough for a nice dinner out.

One final thought: both platforms have gotten too expensive. For this same Asheville trip, I could have booked a mid-range hotel for $200/night with no cleaning fees, no chores, and a front desk that answers the phone. The short-term rental boom might be peaking. But if you're going to use one, my money's on Vrbo — at least for now.
