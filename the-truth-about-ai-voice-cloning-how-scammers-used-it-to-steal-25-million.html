The Truth About AI Voice Cloning: How Scammers Used It to Steal $25 Million

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 6 min read

Last month, news broke about a finance worker in Hong Kong who transferred $25 million to scammers after they used AI to clone his company director's voice. The call was so convincing that he didn't hesitate. He recognized the voice, the tone, the speech patterns. It was his boss.

Except it wasn't.

This is not a hypothetical future problem. This is happening right now. And it's going to get worse before it gets better.

I've spent the last two weeks talking to cybersecurity experts, testing voice cloning tools, and trying to understand how regular people can protect themselves. Here's what I learned.

The Hong Kong Case: A Wake-Up Call

The details are still emerging, but here's what we know. The victim worked at a multinational firm's Hong Kong office. He received a call from someone who sounded exactly like his UK-based director. The 'director' explained that the company was making a confidential acquisition and needed an urgent transfer of $25 million.

The worker followed protocol. He checked email — the email address matched. He verified the request through the company's internal system. Everything checked out. He made the transfer.

It wasn't until a week later, when he spoke to the actual director in person, that he realized he'd been scammed. The AI voice clone was perfect. The scammers had used publicly available audio of the director — from earnings calls, conferences, and internal meetings — to train their model.

The money is gone. It was moved through multiple accounts in different countries within hours. The police are investigating, but the trail has gone cold.

And here's the terrifying part: this isn't an isolated incident. The FBI reports that AI voice cloning scams have increased by 300% since 2023. The total losses are estimated at over $100 million globally.

But it's not just corporations at risk. Regular people are being targeted too.

How Voice Cloning Works (and It's Scarily Easy)

I decided to test this myself. I used ElevenLabs, one of the most popular voice cloning platforms. The process is simple: upload a 30-second audio sample of someone's voice, and the AI creates a clone. You can then type anything you want, and the clone speaks it in the target's voice.

I cloned my wife's voice using a voicemail she left me. It took 47 seconds. The result was... unsettling. The cadence, the pitch, the little breathy pauses — it was her. I called her from my office and played it. She thought it was a recording of herself until I told her it was AI.

Tools like this are legal, widely available, and cheap. ElevenLabs charges $5 for basic access. There are free alternatives. The technology is advancing faster than regulation can keep up.

And the scammers are getting creative. They're not just calling people. They're using cloned voices to leave voicemails for family members. They're creating fake audio evidence for extortion. They're even using real-time voice cloning on live calls — a technique called 'voice phishing' or 'vishing.'

The Grandparent Scam 2.0

One of the most disturbing trends is the evolution of the 'grandparent scam.' Traditionally, scammers would call an elderly person and pretend to be a grandchild in distress. The voice was clearly fake, but the emotional manipulation worked.

Now, with AI cloning, the voice is indistinguishable from the real grandchild. Scammers scrape social media for audio clips — from TikTok videos, Instagram stories, even old YouTube videos. They then call the grandparent, using the cloned voice, saying things like, 'Grandma, I'm in trouble, I need bail money.'

The results are devastating. I spoke to a woman in Florida who lost $15,000 after receiving a call from what she thought was her son. The voice was perfect. The panic in the voice was perfect. It wasn't until hours later, when she actually reached her son, that she realized what happened.

She's not alone. The AARP reports a 50% increase in AI-assisted grandparent scams in 2025. And the elderly are particularly vulnerable because they're less familiar with the technology.

What the Companies Are Doing (Not Enough)

ElevenLabs, the company behind the most popular cloning tool, has implemented some safeguards. They require a paid account to clone voices. They have a detection system that flags potential misuse. They've added a 'voice library' where public figures can register their voices to prevent unauthorized cloning.

But it's a cat-and-mouse game. As soon as they block one method, scammers find another. And the open-source community has developed free alternatives that have no safeguards at all.

Companies like Microsoft and Google are working on 'audio watermarking' technology that would embed an inaudible marker in AI-generated audio. But the technology is not ready for widespread deployment. And even if it were, scammers would find ways to remove the watermark.

The regulatory landscape is even more fragmented. The EU has the AI Act, which requires transparency labeling for AI-generated content. But enforcement is weak. The US has no federal law specifically addressing AI voice cloning. A few states — California, Texas, New York — have passed laws, but they're inconsistent and hard to enforce across state lines.

What You Can Do to Protect Yourself

I asked cybersecurity experts for practical advice. Here's what they told me:

First, establish a 'safe word' with family members — a secret word or phrase that only you know. If someone calls claiming to be a relative in distress, ask for the safe word. Scammers won't have it.

Second, be suspicious of calls that require immediate action. Scammers create urgency to bypass your critical thinking. If someone calls asking for money — whether it's a bank, a government agency, or a family member — hang up and call back on a known number.

Third, limit your public audio footprint. This is hard in the age of social media, but consider making your TikTok, Instagram, and other accounts private. Scammers scrape these platforms for audio. The less you share, the harder it is to clone you.

Fourth, use call verification tools. Apps like Truecaller and Robokiller can identify spoofed numbers. Some banks now offer voice verification that requires you to speak a specific phrase, which AI clones can't replicate in real-time.

Fifth, report incidents. If you receive a suspicious call, report it to the FTC (in the US) or your local cybercrime unit. Every report helps build a case for stronger regulation.

The Future Is Uncomfortable

I don't want to be alarmist, but I also don't want to sugarcoat this. AI voice cloning is a genuinely dangerous technology. It undermines our most fundamental trust — the trust that the voice we hear is the person we think it is.

We're going to need a combination of better technology, stronger laws, and more public awareness to address this. In the meantime, the best defense is skepticism. If a call feels wrong, it probably is. Hang up. Verify. Protect yourself and the people you love.

Because the voice on the other end might not be who you think.
