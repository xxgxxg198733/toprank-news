The Hidden Travel Gem You've Never Heard Of – Albania's Riviera in July 2026

TR
By Joshua Reed · July 15, 2026 · 3 min read

Why I Chose Albania Over Greece

Last week, I was supposed to go to Santorini. But the crowds on Instagram looked insane – I saw a video of people queuing for 45 minutes to take a photo at the blue domes. I canceled. A friend recommended Albania, specifically the Riviera along the Ionian Sea. I booked a flight to Tirana for $280 round trip from New York. Best decision I've made all year.

The Journey: Tirana to the Coast

Flying into Tirana is an experience. The airport is small but efficient. I rented a car for $25 a day – a Fiat 500 with manual transmission. The drive to the coast took about three hours. The roads are winding, the scenery is stunning – olive groves, mountains, and eventually, the turquoise sea. I stopped in the city of Vlora for lunch at a roadside taverna. I had grilled octopus, a Greek salad, and a beer for $12. The octopus was the best I've ever had. The owner's grandmother made it. You can't get that in Santorini.

The Beaches: Ksamil, Drymades, and the Secret Spot

Ksamil is the most famous beach town in Albania. The water is incredibly clear – Caribbean-level clear. The beach is free, and you rent a sunbed for $5. There are small islands you can swim to. But it's getting popular. I went on a Tuesday and it was 70% full. If you want solitude, head to Drymades Beach, an hour south. It's a pebble beach with fewer people. The water is even clearer. I found a hidden cove between two rocks that was completely empty. I swam for an hour and saw no one. That's the Albania experience.

The Food: Seafood Heaven

Albanian food is a mix of Mediterranean and Balkan flavors. Grilled fish, fresh vegetables, olive oil, and herbs. I ate at a restaurant called 'Taverna Deti' in the town of Himara. I had grilled sea bass, a plate of fried calamari, a bottle of local wine, and dessert – total bill: $18. The fish was caught that morning. The wine was a crisp white from a local vineyard. I ate there three times because it was so good. Don't miss the 'byrek' – a savory pastry filled with cheese or spinach. It's everywhere and costs $1.

The People: Warm and Welcoming

Albanians are incredibly friendly. I don't speak Albanian, but everyone under 40 speaks English. People smiled, helped me with directions, and invited me for coffee. I sat at a café in the town of Saranda and a local named Arben bought me a Turkish coffee. We talked for an hour about politics and family. He told me his grandfather fought in World War II. The hospitality is real.

The Practical Stuff: Costs, Safety, and Tips

Albania is cheap. I spent about $50 a day including accommodation, food, and activities. I stayed in a guesthouse for $30 a night – clean room, AC, and a balcony with sea view. The country is safe. I walked around at night and never felt threatened. The currency is the Albanian Lek, but most places accept euros. Bring cash though – smaller towns don't have ATMs. The driving is chaotic but not dangerous. Just honk and go.

Why You Should Go Now

Albania is still under the radar. But it won't stay that way. The government is building a new airport in Vlora, and budget airlines are adding routes. Ryanair started flying to Tirana this year. In five years, it'll be the next Croatia. Go now. The water is warm, the food is incredible, and you won't find a better value on the Mediterranean. I'm already planning my return trip.
