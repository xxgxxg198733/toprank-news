Is the Rabbit R1 Worth the Hype? I Tested the AI Gadget for a Week

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 23, 2026 · 5 min read

Remember when everyone lost their minds over the Rabbit R1 at CES 2024? It was this tiny orange gadget that promised to replace all your apps with a single AI assistant. Two years later, it finally launched in late June 2026, and I've been using it for a week. The short version: it's not all it was cracked up to be. But the long version is more interesting.

I'm a tech optimist. I love the idea of an AI agent that can book my flights, order my coffee, and answer my questions without me touching a screen. The Rabbit R1 has a 2.88-inch touchscreen, a scroll wheel, a camera, and a microphone — and it's meant to be used with your voice. You talk to it, and it does things for you. In theory, it's the ultimate productivity tool. In practice, it's like having a very eager intern who keeps messing up.

The Good: When It Works, It's Magic

On the first day, I asked the Rabbit R1 to order me a pizza from Domino's. It asked which toppings I wanted, confirmed the address, and placed the order. Thirty minutes later, a pepperoni pizza arrived at my door. I didn't touch my phone once. That moment was pure science fiction come to life.

It's also great for simple tasks. "Set a timer for 15 minutes." "What's the weather in Tokyo?" "Find me a Thai restaurant within walking distance." It handles these perfectly, and the voice recognition is surprisingly accurate — it understood my accent even when I was mumbling in the morning.

The scroll wheel is a nice touch. You can scroll through options without looking at the screen, which feels more natural than tapping. The screen itself is bright and responsive, though it's tiny compared to a phone. You're not meant to stare at it — you're meant to talk to it.

The Bad: The Hiccups Are Real

But here's the thing: the Rabbit R1 only works with about 20 services right now. It can order from Domino's, book Ubers, play Spotify, and call via Google Voice. That's it. It can't order from Grubhub, it can't send WhatsApp messages, it can't check your Gmail, and it can't control your smart home unless you have specific brands. The promise of "replace all your apps" is years away, if it ever comes.

Even with supported services, things go wrong. I asked it to play "Blinding Lights" by The Weeknd on Spotify, and it played a reggae cover by some random artist. I asked it to book an Uber to the airport, and it set the pickup location to the wrong side of my building. The customer support chatbot was not helpful — it gave generic responses like "We're sorry for the inconvenience. Please try again."

The worst part is the battery. The Rabbit R1 lasts about 4 hours with moderate use. That's not a typo — four hours. By 2 PM, it was dead, and I had to plug it in like a needy Tamagotchi. The official site says it gets 8 hours, but that's with the screen off and minimal interaction. In real life, it's half that.

How It Compares to Your Phone

Here's the uncomfortable truth: your phone can already do everything the Rabbit R1 does, and better. Siri and Google Assistant are more reliable, have access to more apps, and don't require a separate $199 device. The Rabbit R1's only advantage is that it's dedicated — you use it intentionally, without the distractions of social media and email. But that's a niche use case.

I also tested it against my iPhone with ChatGPT's voice mode. I asked both to "plan a weekend trip to San Francisco with flights, hotel, and restaurants." ChatGPT gave me a detailed itinerary with links. The Rabbit R1 said "I can't do that yet" and suggested I book each part separately. It's just not smart enough to handle complex, multi-step tasks.

And let's talk about the price. The Rabbit R1 costs $199, plus a $10/month subscription for the AI features. Over two years, that's $439. For that money, you could buy a mid-range Android phone that does everything better. Or you could spend $19 on an app like Tasker that automates your phone. The value proposition is weak.

Who Should Buy It?

Honestly? Almost no one. The Rabbit R1 is a beta product marketed as a finished device. It's fun to play with for a few days, but it's not ready for daily use. If you're a tech enthusiast who loves early adopter gadgets and has $200 to burn, go ahead — you'll enjoy the novelty. But if you're a normal person looking for a productivity tool, save your money.

The one group that might benefit is people who want to reduce phone screen time. The Rabbit R1 forces you to interact verbally, so you won't be scrolling Instagram while using it. But you could also just buy a dumb phone and a smartwatch for the same effect.

The Bigger Picture

The Rabbit R1 is a glimpse of a future that's not here yet. The idea of an AI agent that handles all your digital tasks is compelling, and I believe we'll get there in 5-10 years. But right now, the tech isn't reliable enough, the ecosystem isn't broad enough, and the battery isn't good enough. The Rabbit R1 is a proof of concept, not a product.

I admire what Rabbit Inc. tried to do, and I hope they keep iterating. But as of July 2026, the Rabbit R1 is a cool toy, not a phone killer. Stick with your phone for now, and check back in a few years.
