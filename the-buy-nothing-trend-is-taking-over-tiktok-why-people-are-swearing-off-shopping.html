The 'Buy Nothing' Trend Is Taking Over TikTok: Why People Are Swearing Off Shopping

TR
By Amanda Brooks · June 29, 2026 · 3 min read

What Is the 'Buy Nothing' Trend?

There's a new viral movement on TikTok and Instagram called 'Buy Nothing 2026.' It's exactly what it sounds like: people are challenging themselves to buy nothing new for a whole year — no clothes, no gadgets, no home decor. They only buy essentials like food, toiletries, and medicine. Everything else is borrowed, swapped, or thrifted. I thought it was just another internet fad, but then I saw the numbers. The hashtag has over 2 billion views. So I decided to look into it. I found three people who are doing it seriously, and their stories changed my mind.

Why This Is Happening Now

The trend started gaining traction in early 2026, partly as a reaction to inflation and the cost of living crisis. But it's also a backlash against overconsumption. We've all seen those TikTok videos of 'hauls' — people buying dozens of Shein items for $100. The Buy Nothing movement is the opposite. It's about being intentional. The founder, a 28-year-old from Portland named Sarah Chen, started it after she calculated she spent $15,000 on clothes in 2025. 'I had a panic attack looking at my credit card statement,' she told me. 'I realized I was buying stuff I didn't even like.'

Meet Alex: 6 Months Without Shopping

Alex, a 34-year-old teacher from Austin, started the challenge in January. She's now six months in. 'The first month was brutal,' she said. 'I had to uninstall all shopping apps from my phone. I realized I was buying things out of boredom.' She now borrows books from the library, swaps clothes with friends, and mends her own clothes. 'I've saved about $4,000 already. And I feel lighter — like I'm not drowning in stuff.' She admits it's hard: 'I almost broke when my favorite jeans ripped. But I patched them. Now they look cooler.'

Meet Jamie: The Family Challenge

Jamie, a 41-year-old father of two from Ohio, is doing the challenge with his whole family. 'We have three kids, and they always want new toys. We told them for 2026, we're only buying secondhand or making things ourselves.' They've had to get creative. 'We made a board game out of cardboard. The kids loved it more than anything from Target.' The hardest part for Jamie was not buying new tech. 'I wanted the new Nintendo Switch 2, but I'm waiting until next year.' He says the family is closer now. 'We do more things together instead of just consuming.'

Meet Priya: The Minimalist Fashionista

Priya, a 29-year-old fashion blogger from London, might seem like an unlikely participant. 'I made money reviewing clothes. But I felt fake — I was encouraging people to buy stuff I didn't need.' She started the challenge and now only thrifts. 'I found a vintage Burberry trench coat for $30. It's better quality than anything new I could buy.' She says her content has actually grown because people love the thrift hauls. 'I'm more creative now. I style things in different ways. It's more fun than unboxing plastic bags.'

The Challenges: It's Not Easy

Everyone I spoke to said the hardest part was social pressure. 'Friends invite you to go shopping,' Alex said. 'You feel left out.' There's also the issue of quality. Not everything you can get for free or cheap is good. 'I got a free couch, and it had bed bugs,' Jamie laughed. 'That was a nightmare.' But they all agreed it's worth it. 'The first few weeks are hard, then it becomes a habit,' Priya said.

Is It Sustainable?

The Buy Nothing trend might not be realistic for everyone long-term. Some things you just have to buy new — like underwear or car tires. But the philosophy behind it — buying less, sharing more — is a good one. I'm not going to do a full year, but I've started small. I'm not buying any new clothes until September. It's been three weeks, and I've saved $200. I don't miss it. Maybe try it for a month. What do you have to lose except your shopping habit?
