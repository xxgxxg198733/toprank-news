Why I'm Telling Everyone to Visit Porto Before It Gets Completely Overrun

TR
By Daniel Wilson · July 24, 2026 · 5 min read

I first went to Porto in 2018. I fell in love with it the way you fall in love with a city that doesn't try to impress you. It's messy, hilly, covered in tile work that's peeling in beautiful ways, and the smell of port wine and river salt follows you everywhere. Back then, it felt like a secret. People went to Lisbon. Porto was the underrated sibling.

That's changing fast. In June 2026, Porto airport saw a 40% increase in passenger traffic compared to June 2025. Hotels are booking months in advance. A new direct flight from New York just launched. The secret is out. But I just got back from 10 days there, and I can tell you: it's still worth going. You just need to know where to go and what to skip.

Why Now? Because It Won't Be Like This for Long

Porto is at a tipping point. It's still affordable compared to other European cities. A good meal costs €15-20. A glass of port at a riverfront bar is €4. An Uber across town is €6. But prices are rising. I talked to a local shopkeeper named Rui who's been selling sardines and canned fish for 30 years. He told me rent in his neighborhood has doubled since 2022. He's not sure he can stay.

The city is also getting crowded. The Ribeira district, the historic riverside area, is packed with selfie sticks and tour groups. In 2018, I could walk across the Dom Luís I Bridge without bumping into anyone. In 2026, it's shoulder-to-shoulder at sunset. If you go, go early in the morning. Or go somewhere else entirely.

What to Actually Do in Porto (Beyond the Obvious)

Everyone goes to the port wine lodges in Vila Nova de Gaia. And you should — Taylor's, Graham's, Sandeman — they're all worth a visit. But here's a tip: book the reserve tasting at Taylor's. It's €45 and you get to try ports from the 1980s. It'll ruin cheap port for you forever.

But the real magic of Porto is in the neighborhoods tourists skip.

**Bonfim** is a working-class area east of the center. It's not pretty. The buildings are grimy, the streets are narrow, and there's graffiti everywhere. But it has the best food in the city. There's a tiny restaurant called O Fado that serves a francesinha — a monstrous sandwich of ham, sausage, steak, and melted cheese, drowned in a tomato-beer sauce — that will change your life. It's €9. I ate there three times.

**Foz do Douro** is where the river meets the Atlantic. It's a 20-minute bus ride from the center. The seaside promenade is stunning. There's a lighthouse, rocky beaches, and a café called Praia da Luz where you can watch the waves while eating grilled sardines. I spent an entire afternoon there reading a book. It was the most peaceful day of my trip.

**Cedofeita** is the artsy neighborhood. Independent galleries, vintage stores, and a street art scene that's genuinely world-class. Look for the mural by Hazul — a Portuguese artist who paints these surreal, dreamlike figures. It's on Rua de Miguel Bombarda.

The Food Scene Is Insane Right Now

Porto's food reputation used to be based on seafood and port. That's still true, but the new generation of chefs is doing something special. I ate at a restaurant called Antiqvvm, which has a Michelin star. The tasting menu is €85. For a Michelin-starred meal, that's a steal. The chef, Vitor Matos, does things with cod I didn't think were possible. A cod loin with saffron and almond foam, served with a crispy skin that tasted like the sea itself.

But the best meal I had was at a no-name tasca in the Bolhão market. A woman named Dona Isabel was frying sardines in a tiny stall. No menu. No prices. She just pointed at the fish and held up three fingers. €3. I ate it standing up with bread and a paper cup of vinho verde. It was perfect.

Also, try the pastéis de nata from Manteigaria. Skip the famous ones from Fábrica da Nata — they're good, but Manteigaria's are creamier, more custardy, and they have a flakier crust. Fight me.

A Cautionary Tale: What Not to Do

Don't take a guided tour of the Livraria Lello. Yes, it's the bookstore that inspired Harry Potter. Yes, it's beautiful. But it's also a tourist trap of epic proportions. The line stretches around the block, tickets are €15, and inside it's so crowded you can't move. I peeked through the window and walked away. Not worth it.

Don't eat on Rua das Flores. The restaurants there are overpriced and mediocre. Tourist food. Walk two streets over and find somewhere locals eat.

Don't try to drive in Porto. The hills are insane, the streets are narrow, and parking is impossible. Use the metro and your legs. You'll be sore, but you'll see more.

Is Porto Safe? And Other Practical Questions

Yes, it's safe. I walked alone at night as a woman and never felt threatened. Pickpocketing happens in tourist areas, so keep your phone in your front pocket. But violent crime is rare.

The best time to visit is May, June, or September. July and August are hot and crowded. I was there in early July and it hit 35°C (95°F) one day. Not fun. September is perfect — warm days, cool nights, fewer tourists.

Learn a few words of Portuguese. Not Spanish — Portuguese. People appreciate the effort. "Obrigado" (thank you) and "bom dia" (good morning) go a long way.

My Final Pitch: Go Before It Changes

Porto is still wonderful. It's still affordable, still charming, still full of character. But the pressure is mounting. More tourists, higher prices, more Airbnb conversions. In five years, it might look like Barcelona or Venice — a beautiful shell of what it once was.

So go now. Book the flight. Eat the francesinha. Watch the sunset over the Douro from the top of the Clérigos Tower. Walk the backstreets of Bonfim and get lost. That's the real Porto. And it's waiting for you.

I'll be back next year. I hope it hasn't changed too much.
