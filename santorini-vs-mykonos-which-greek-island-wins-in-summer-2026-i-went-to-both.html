Santorini vs Mykonos: Which Greek Island Wins in Summer 2026? (I Went to Both)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I have a confession: I used to be Team Mykonos all the way. The parties, the beach clubs, the gorgeous chaos of it all. Santorini felt like a postcard — beautiful, but flat. Something you look at, not something you experience.

Then I went back this May, right before the summer crowds hit, and I completely changed my mind. Both islands are incredible. But for summer 2026, with the new travel trends, the overcrowding issues, and the way tourism has evolved post-pandemic, one island clearly wins.

Let me explain why.

The Crowd Problem Nobody's Talking About

Let's start with the elephant in the Aegean Sea: overtourism. Santorini has been struggling with this for years. In 2024, the island welcomed over 3.4 million visitors — and it has a permanent population of about 15,000. That's insane. The main town of Fira becomes a human river from 10 AM to sunset. Oia, famous for its sunset views, is so packed that locals have started putting up signs begging tourists to stay away from certain spots.

Mykonos isn't much better during peak season. But here's the key difference: Mykonos handles crowds better. The island is larger, the infrastructure is more spread out, and there are more beaches to absorb the masses. Santorini is essentially one caldera edge with limited space. You feel the crush more acutely.

For summer 2026, I'd recommend Mykonos if you want to actually enjoy your vacation without feeling like a sardine. But there's a catch — and it's a big one.

The Cost of Paradise

Both islands are expensive. But Mykonos has become genuinely outrageous. A basic hotel room in Mykonos Town this July? We're talking €400-600 a night for something mediocre. A cocktail at a beach club? €25-30. Dinner for two with a bottle of wine? Easily €200+. And the prices keep climbing every year.

Santorini is expensive too, but you get more value for your money. A room with a caldera view in Fira or Imerovigli will set you back €300-500 a night, but the experience is genuinely world-class. You're paying for one of the most iconic views on the planet. In Mykonos, you're paying for the privilege of being seen.

If budget is a concern — and let's be real, for most of us it is — Santorini wins by a narrow margin. But only if you book early and avoid the absolute peak of August.

Beaches: Mykonos Wins, But Not How You Think

Mykonos has objectively better beaches. Paradise Beach, Super Paradise, Elia, Agios Sostis — the list goes on. They're sandy, they're swimmable, and many have excellent beach clubs with loungers, food, and music.

Santorini's beaches are... fine. The famous Red Beach is small and gets packed. Perissa and Kamari have black sand, which is unique but not as comfortable. The water is clear, but the beaches lack the variety of Mykonos.

BUT — and this is important — the best beach experience in Santorini is actually the one nobody talks about: Vlychada. It's a volcanic beach with dramatic cliffs, far fewer crowds, and a weirdly lunar landscape. I spent an entire afternoon there last month and saw maybe 20 other people. In Mykonos, you can't escape people.

So if beaches are your priority, Mykonos wins. But if you want a beach day without the scene, Santorini has hidden gems.

The Food Scene: An Unexpected Upset

I've always thought Greek island food is good but not remarkable. This trip changed my mind. Santorini has quietly become a serious culinary destination. The volcanic soil produces incredible ingredients — the cherry tomatoes, the white eggplants, the capers. Restaurants like Metaxi Mas and Selene are doing genuinely innovative things with traditional recipes.

Mykonos, by contrast, has leaned hard into international cuisine and Instagram-ready plating. You can get excellent sushi and pasta, but finding authentic, high-quality Greek food requires effort. The best meal I had in Mykonos this trip was at Kiki's Tavern, a no-reservations spot hidden on a dirt road near Agios Sostis. It was incredible. But it took me three tries to get a table.

Santorini wins for food, and it's not particularly close. The focus on local ingredients, the tradition of wine-making (the Assyrtiko grapes are world-class), and the general attitude toward cooking all add up to a better eating experience.

The Nightlife Divide (This Will Surprise You)

Mykonos is famous for its nightlife. Cavo Paradiso, Scorpios, the beach clubs — they're legendary. And they deliver. If you want to dance until dawn with a bottle of vodka in hand, Mykonos is your place.

But I'm in my mid-30s now. My idea of a good night is dinner that lasts three hours, good wine, and a walk under the stars. And for that, Santorini is actually better. The caldera views at night, with all the lights twinkling from the cliffside towns, are genuinely magical. There are excellent cocktail bars — 2 Brothers in Imerovigli, PK Cocktail Bar in Fira — without the screaming bass and bottle service attitude.

If you're under 25 and want to party, Mykonos. If you're over 30 and want atmosphere, Santorini. There's no wrong answer, just different life stages.

The Final Verdict

After a decade of visiting both, I'm picking Santorini for summer 2026. Here's why: the travel trends are shifting. More people are seeking authentic experiences over Instagram backdrops. Santorini offers depth — wine tours, archaeological sites (Akrotiri is a must), hiking trails between Fira and Oia, cooking classes.

Mykonos offers a good time. Santorini offers a memorable experience.

But if you go to Santorini, do it right. Visit in late May or early September to avoid the worst crowds. Stay in Imerovigli or Firostefani instead of Oia. Rent an ATV to explore the less touristy parts. And for the love of Zeus, don't just take the same sunset photo everyone else has. Find your own view.

Because that's what travel should be about — finding your own view, not someone else's.
