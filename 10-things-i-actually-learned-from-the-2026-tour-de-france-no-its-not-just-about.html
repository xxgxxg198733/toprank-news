10 Things I Actually Learned From the 2026 Tour de France (No, It's Not Just About the Winner)

TR
By Joshua Reed · July 24, 2026 · 6 min read

The 2026 Tour de France ended last Sunday in Paris, and I've been recovering from sleep deprivation ever since. Three weeks of early mornings, dramatic mountain stages, and enough sports drama to fill a Netflix series. But here's the thing — if you only looked at the final podium, you'd miss everything that made this year special.

I've been following cycling obsessively since 2015, and this was one of the most unpredictable Tours I can remember. Jonas Vingegaard didn't defend his title. Tadej Pogačar didn't dominate like everyone expected. A 24-year-old Colombian nobody had on their radar almost won the whole thing. And somewhere in the Pyrenees, a roadside baker made the best croissant I've ever tasted through a screen.

So before we forget about July 2026, here are the 10 things that actually mattered — the moments, the people, and the lessons that went beyond the yellow jersey.

1. The Winner Wasn't Who Anyone Predicted — And That's Perfect

Juan Ayuso, a 23-year-old from Spain, won the 2026 Tour de France. If you had that in your office pool, you're either lying or you're a time traveler. Ayuso rides for UAE Team Emirates, and for the last two years, he's been riding in Pogačar's shadow. But when Pogačar crashed on Stage 12, everything changed.

I watched that stage live. The descent off the Col de la Loze is terrifying even on TV. Pogačar slid on a patch of gravel, went down hard, and fractured his collarbone. The cameras showed him sitting on the side of the road, helmet off, face in his hands. That was it for his Tour. And for a moment, the whole race felt empty.

But Ayuso didn't panic. He took the yellow jersey two days later and held it through the Alps. He's not a showy rider — no dramatic attacks, no wild celebrations. Just steady, smart racing. I think we're going to see a lot more of him.

2. The Colombian Kid Who Stole My Heart

His name is Santiago Buitrago. He's 24, from Bogotá, and he rides for Bahrain Victorious. Before this Tour, I knew he was good on climbs. After this Tour, I'm convinced he's going to win a Grand Tour within three years.

Buitrago finished second overall, just 47 seconds behind Ayuso. But that doesn't tell the story. On Stage 15, he attacked on the Col du Galibier with 70 kilometers to go and held off the chasing pack for almost an hour. I was literally standing up in my living room, yelling at my TV. My neighbors probably think I've lost my mind.

He lost the lead in the time trial on Stage 20 — time trials are his weakness, and he knows it. But here's what nobody's talking about: he gained 12 seconds on Ayuso in the final mountain stage after that. If the Tour had one more mountain stage, he might have won. Mark my words: Buitrago is the future.

3. The Crash That Changed Everything

I mentioned Pogačar's crash, but there were others. On Stage 5, a spectator stepped into the road taking a selfie and took out half the peloton. Six riders went down. Richard Carapaz dislocated his shoulder and was out of the race. The spectator got arrested, but honestly, that doesn't help Carapaz.

This keeps happening. In 2023, a woman with a sign caused a massive crash. In 2024, a dog ran onto the course. At some point, the Tour organizers need better barriers or stricter crowd control. It's not funny anymore. These are people's careers and bodies.

4. The Team Dynamics Were Wild

Jumbo-Visma (now Visma-Lease a Bike) came in as defending champions and just... fell apart. Internal tensions from last year's contract disputes seemed to carry over. Their lead rider, Wout van Aert, crashed on Stage 8 and never recovered. Sepp Kuss, the American who won the Vuelta in 2023, was supposed to be a co-leader but spent most of the race pulling for others. By Stage 14, it was clear they had no plan.

Compare that to UAE Team Emirates. When Pogačar crashed, they didn't panic. They shifted all support to Ayuso immediately. That's the mark of a well-run team — they had a backup plan and they executed it perfectly.

5. The Best Food I Saw (Yes, Through a Screen)

Every year, the helicopter shots of French villages make me want to book a flight. But this year, there was a bakery in Bourg-Saint-Maurice that kept getting featured. The owners, a couple named Marie and Jean-Paul, made these enormous apricot tarts. I looked them up — they have a 4.9 on Google Maps. I'm genuinely planning a trip around visiting them.

Also, the feed zones have gotten way more creative. Riders are grabbing mini quiches and slices of ham now, not just energy gels. It's making me hungry just writing this.

6. The Commentary Was Actually Good This Year

NBC's coverage has been hit-or-miss for years, but they brought in a new co-commentator for 2026: former pro rider Lizzie Deignan. She's fantastic. She explains tactics in plain English, calls out bad decisions without being mean, and she clearly loves the sport. I learned more from her in one stage than I did from years of watching.

Also, the British Eurosport team with Rob Hatch and Sean Kelly remains unbeatable. Those guys make a 6-hour stage feel like 20 minutes.

7. The Green Jersey Battle Was More Exciting Than the Yellow

While Ayuso was cruising to victory in yellow, the points classification was a genuine war. Mads Pedersen vs. Jasper Philipsen vs. Fabio Jakobsen. They traded wins almost every day. Pedersen ended up winning by just 11 points — the smallest margin since 2017. Every sprint stage felt like a final. I love when secondary competitions get this intense.

8. The Young Riders Are Insanely Talented

Six of the top 10 finishers in the general classification were under 25. That's unheard of. Cian Uijtdebroeks, 23, finished fourth. Carlos Rodríguez, 25, finished fifth. This generation of riders is faster, stronger, and smarter than ever. The old guard — Froome, Thomas, even Roglič — are being left behind. It's both exciting and a little sad to watch.

9. The Best Moment That Had Nothing to Do With Winning

On Stage 18, a rider named Nils Politt (German, rides for UAE) stopped to help a fan who had collapsed on the side of the road. He gave up his place in the peloton, waited for medical help, and then chased back for 40 kilometers. He finished 15 minutes behind the leaders, but the crowd gave him a standing ovation at the finish. That's the Tour de France I love — not the winners, but the humans.

10. What I'm Taking Into Next Year

The 2026 Tour taught me not to write off young riders, to expect the unexpected, and that the best stories aren't always on the podium. I'm already clearing my calendar for July 2027. I'll probably book that bakery trip too.

If you watched this year, tell me your favorite moment. If you didn't, start paying attention — this sport has more drama than any reality show, and it's all real.
