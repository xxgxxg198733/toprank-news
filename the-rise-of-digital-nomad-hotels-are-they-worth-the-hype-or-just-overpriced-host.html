The Rise of 'Digital Nomad Hotels': Are They Worth the Hype or Just Overpriced Hostels?

TR
By TopRank Team · June 15, 2026 · 5 min read

If you've been on Instagram in the past year, you've seen the photos: a sun-drenched pool, a laptop open on a bamboo table, a coconut drink in hand, and the caption 'just another day at the office.' These are the 'digital nomad hotels'—properties designed specifically for remote workers, with coworking spaces, high-speed internet, and community events baked into the experience.

Brands like Selina, Roam, Outsite, and a new crop of independents are betting that the remote work revolution isn't going away. According to a report from the Global Co-working Alliance last month, the number of dedicated 'work-from-anywhere' accommodations has increased by 340% since 2022. It's a booming industry.

But are these places actually good for working, or are they just expensive hostels with better marketing? To find out, I spent the month of May living in three different digital nomad hotels: Outsite in Lisbon, Selina in Medellin, and a smaller independent called Workbase in Tbilisi, Georgia. I worked my normal job (I'm a freelance writer, so I need decent Wi-Fi and quiet space) and tested everything from internet speed to social atmosphere to whether I could actually get work done.

Outsite Lisbon: The Gold Standard

Outsite in Lisbon is the most polished of the three. It's a converted apartment building in the Alcantara neighborhood, about 15 minutes from the city center. The coworking space is on the ground floor with floor-to-ceiling windows, ergonomic chairs, and monitor rentals (€5 per day). The internet speed was 200 Mbps down, 50 Mbps up—fast enough for video calls and large file uploads.

The rooms range from private studios (€1,200 per month) to shared dorms (€450 per month). I got a private room with a kitchenette, which cost €950. It was clean, modern, and had good blackout curtains. The bed was comfortable.

What sets Outsite apart is the community aspect. They have a calendar of events: Monday night dinners (€15), Wednesday yoga sessions (free), Friday coworking brunches. I'm not usually a 'group activities' person, but I went to the Monday dinner and ended up talking to a graphic designer from Toronto and a startup founder from Singapore. We're still in a WhatsApp group.

The downside? It's expensive for Lisbon. You could rent a normal apartment for €700 per month and get a coworking membership for €100. Outsite is charging a premium for the convenience and community. Whether that's worth it depends on how much you value those things.

Selina Medellin: Hit or Miss

Selina is the biggest player in this space, with 50+ locations worldwide. I went to their Medellin property in the Laureles neighborhood, which is popular with remote workers. The Selina brand is more 'hostel meets hotel' than dedicated work space. The coworking area is open to the public (locals use it too), which creates a nice mix but also means it can get crowded and noisy.

Internet speeds varied wildly. In the coworking area, I measured 150 Mbps. In my room (a private suite at $800 per month), it dropped to 30 Mbps. That's fine for emails and browsing, but I had to go to the coworking area for video calls. The room also had thin walls—I could hear my neighbor's Zoom meetings clearly.

The social scene is more party-oriented than Outsite. There's a bar on the rooftop that plays music until late. If you're looking to work during the day and socialize at night, this is great. If you need sleep at 10 PM, not so much.

I also had an issue with the booking system. I booked for two weeks, then wanted to extend by another week. The front desk told me there was no availability, but I could see empty rooms on their website. It turned out they were holding rooms for longer-term guests. That felt like poor management.

Workbase Tbilisi: The Surprising Winner

Georgia isn't the first place that comes to mind for digital nomads, but Tbilisi has become a hub thanks to its low cost of living, generous visa policies, and fast internet. Workbase is a small operation—just 12 rooms—in the Vera neighborhood. It's run by a couple, Nino and Davit, who are former remote workers themselves.

The coworking space is small but well-designed: six desks with monitors, a meeting room for two, and a quiet corner with couches. The internet was 250 Mbps consistently. The rooms are basic but comfortable—I paid $550 per month for a studio with a small kitchen.

What made Workbase special was the attention to detail. Nino left a welcome note with restaurant recommendations. The kitchen had a stock of tea, coffee, and basic spices. There was a weekly family dinner where Nino cooked Georgian food (khachapuri, khinkali). It felt like staying with friends, not at a hotel.

The downside: Tbilisi is not for everyone. The infrastructure is developing—power outages happen, some streets are unpaved, and navigation is confusing. But if you're adventurous, it's an incredible value.

The Verdict: Good for Some, Not for All

So, are digital nomad hotels worth it? It depends on your personality and work style.

They're great for: Extraverts who want built-in social circles. People who travel alone and don't want to eat dinner by themselves every night. Anyone who values convenience over cost. Remote workers who need reliable internet and a dedicated workspace without the hassle of finding and furnishing an apartment.

They're not great for: Introverts who need quiet and solitude. Budget travelers who can get better value elsewhere. People who need absolute silence for deep focus work (the coworking areas are rarely completely quiet). Anyone who doesn't like structured community events.

For me, Outsite was the best experience because it hit the sweet spot between community and privacy. I met people when I wanted to, but I could retreat to my room when I needed to work. Workbase was a close second because of the genuine warmth of the owners. Selina was fine but felt too much like a hostel—not enough focus on the 'work' part of 'work from anywhere.'

If you're thinking about trying this lifestyle, my advice is to start with a one-week stay at a place like Outsite. See if you like the vibe. Don't commit to a full month until you know what you're getting into. And always, always check the internet speed before you book—ask for a recent speed test result. That's the one thing that can make or break the experience.

The digital nomad hotel trend isn't going away. But like any trend, it's worth approaching with realistic expectations. These places are a tool, not a magic solution. Used well, they can make remote work feel less lonely and more adventurous. Used poorly, they're just overpriced dorms with a marketing budget. Choose wisely.
