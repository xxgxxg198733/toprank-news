Costa Rica's New 'Digital Nomad Coast' Is a Game Changer for Remote Workers

TR
By Emily Watson · July 18, 2026 · 6 min read

I've been working remotely since 2019, and I've tried the whole digital nomad thing in Mexico, Portugal, and Thailand. But when Costa Rica announced its 'Digital Nomad Coast' initiative in April 2026 — a designated zone along the Pacific coast with tax incentives, coworking spaces, and streamlined visas — I was skeptical. Another country trying to cash in on the remote work trend? But then I actually went. I spent three weeks in June exploring the area from Jacó down to Manuel Antonio, and I'm genuinely impressed. Here's what it's actually like.

What Is the 'Digital Nomad Coast' Exactly?

First, some context. Costa Rica already had a digital nomad visa (rentista visa) that required proof of $2,500 monthly income and a bunch of paperwork. The new program, which went live on April 1, 2026, is different. It's not a visa per se — it's a designated region along the Pacific coast (about 50 miles of coastline) where the government has partnered with local businesses to create a remote-worker-friendly ecosystem. The benefits include: no income tax on foreign-earned income for the first two years, discounted coworking memberships at 15 partner spaces, priority internet installation (within 48 hours, they claim), and a simplified entry process that's basically 'show up with a passport and proof of remote work.'

I talked to Maria Fernandez, the tourism board's director of digital initiatives, who told me the goal is to attract 20,000 remote workers by the end of 2027. 'We realized that our regular visa process was scaring people away,' she said. 'So we created a frictionless entry point for people who want to work from paradise.'

Did it work? Mostly, yes. But there are catches.

The Good: Internet That Actually Works

My biggest fear about working from Costa Rica was the internet. I'd heard horror stories from friends who'd tried it in 2022 — spotty connections, power outages, and download speeds that made dial-up look fast. But the Digital Nomad Coast has invested heavily in fiber optic infrastructure. The coworking space I used in Jacó, called 'The Nest,' had gigabit fiber — I ran speed tests and got 850 Mbps down, 450 Mbps up. That's better than my apartment in Chicago.

I also tested internet at three different Airbnbs, all of which advertised 'dedicated remote work connections.' Two were excellent (200+ Mbps), one was mediocre (30 Mbps, fine for email but not video calls). The key is to book places that specifically mention the Digital Nomad Coast program — they've been vetted. Standalone rentals outside the zone are a gamble.

I worked from a beachfront café in Manuel Antonio one afternoon and had a Zoom call with 15 people without any lag. That would have been unthinkable five years ago.

The Bad: Cost of Living Isn't as Cheap as You Think

Let's talk money. Costa Rica has a reputation as an affordable destination, but the Digital Nomad Coast is not cheap. My three-week trip cost me about $2,800, including flights from the US ($450 round trip), accommodation ($1,200 for a nice one-bedroom Airbnb in Jacó), food ($800, eating out most meals), and coworking ($200 for a monthly pass). That's about $133 per day, which is doable but not cheap. For comparison, I spent about $100 per day in Portugal last year and $80 per day in Thailand.

Grocery prices are particularly painful. A gallon of milk costs about $6. A dozen eggs: $4. Imported goods like peanut butter or cheese can be double what you'd pay in the US. If you're on a tight budget, you'll need to cook local — which means rice, beans, plantains, and fresh fish. That's not a bad thing, but it requires adjustment.

Also, the 'tax-free' benefit only applies to income earned from foreign sources. If you're a freelancer with US clients, great. If you're working for a Costa Rican company? You'll pay their 15-30% income tax. That's not a problem for most digital nomads, but worth knowing.

The Ugly: The Monkeys Will Steal Your Lunch

This is not a joke. On my second day in Manuel Antonio, I was working on my laptop at an outdoor table when a troop of capuchin monkeys descended from the trees. One of them grabbed my sandwich (a $12 tuna melt from a nearby café) and was gone before I could react. The locals just laughed. 'Welcome to Costa Rica,' said the barista.

Beyond the wildlife, there's the heat and humidity. I'm from Chicago, so I'm used to seasons. Costa Rica is hot — like, 85-90 degrees with 80% humidity every single day. Working from a non-air-conditioned space is miserable. Most coworking spaces have AC, but many Airbnbs rely on fans. If you're heat-sensitive, pay extra for AC.

And the bugs. Oh, the bugs. Mosquitoes are relentless, especially around sunrise and sunset. I went through two bottles of DEET in three weeks. One friend got dengue fever last year (though the risk is low in the dry season). Bring repellent and don't skip it.

The Community Vibe Is Real

One thing that surprised me: the community. I've been to digital nomad hotspots in Lisbon and Chiang Mai, and they can feel isolating — everyone in their own bubble. But the Digital Nomad Coast seems to have cracked the code. The coworking spaces organize weekly events: beach volleyball, surf lessons, hiking trips, and 'work from the beach' days where they set up a mobile office on the sand.

I met a software engineer from Berlin, a graphic designer from Melbourne, and a YouTuber from Texas — all in my first week. There's a WhatsApp group with 1,200 members that shares tips on everything from the best SIM cards to which surf spots are safe for beginners. It felt like a community, not just a collection of remote workers.

The locals are also genuinely welcoming. Costa Rica (or 'Ticos,' as they call themselves) is known for the 'pura vida' mentality — a chill, friendly attitude that's infectious. I had a conversation with a surf instructor who told me, 'We want you here. We know you bring money and respect our home. Just don't try to turn us into America.' Fair enough.

Should You Go? My Honest Take

If you're a digital nomad looking for a new base, the Digital Nomad Coast is absolutely worth considering. The internet is better than I expected, the community is strong, and the natural beauty is unmatched — I worked with a view of the Pacific Ocean every day, which never got old. But it's not cheap, the heat is real, and you'll need to plan carefully around accommodation and budgeting.

I'd recommend it for: people who can afford $3,000-4,000 per month, who love nature and outdoor activities, and who are okay with a slower pace of life. I'd caution against it for: people on a tight budget, who need air conditioning 24/7, or who can't handle bugs and humidity.

Would I go back? Yes, absolutely. I'm already planning a three-month stay next winter. But I'll pack more DEET, less peanut butter, and a faster reflex for protecting my lunch from monkeys.
