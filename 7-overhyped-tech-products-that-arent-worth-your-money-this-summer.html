7 Overhyped Tech Products That Aren't Worth Your Money This Summer

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 21, 2026 · 6 min read

Look, I'm a sucker for a flashy gadget as much as the next person. When a sleek new piece of tech drops, I'm the kind of person who refreshes the product page every hour, reads every review, and usually ends up buying it. But this summer, something snapped. I realized I've spent over $2,000 on junk that promised to change my life but ended up collecting dust in a drawer. So I decided to do something about it: I spent the last two weeks testing 12 of the most hyped tech products of 2026. The results? Honestly, it's a graveyard of disappointment.

Let me be clear: I'm not talking about the obvious stuff like a $200 cable. I'm talking about products that legitimately went viral on TikTok and Instagram, that had influencers swooning, and that my friends texted me about. I wanted to believe. I really did. But after day after day of frustration, I'm ready to spill the tea. Here are 7 overhyped tech products that, in my honest opinion, are absolutely not worth your money right now—plus three that bucked the trend and actually impressed me.

The Robot Vacuum That Can't Handle Dog Hair (But Costs $1,200)

Remember when everyone was raving about the RoboClean Max 5000? The one with the self-emptying bin and the 'AI that learns your home'? I bought one. I wanted to love it. But here's the thing: if you have a golden retriever, a husky, or basically any pet that sheds, this thing is a nightmare. The brushes get tangled within minutes. The mop function just smears muddy paw prints across the floor. And the 'self-emptying' mechanism? It clogged after three days. I spent more time unclogging it than I would have spent vacuuming myself. At $1,200, that's just insulting. I returned it and bought a $250 basic model that actually works.

The Foldable Phone That Still Has a Crease (Yes, Really)

I know, I know—foldable phones are supposed to be the future. Samsung's Galaxy Fold 7 and Google's Pixel Fold 2 have been getting glowing reviews. But I spent a week with the Galaxy Fold 7, and here's the truth nobody is saying: that crease in the middle? It's still there. It's not as bad as the first generation, sure, but it's visible, and it's annoying when you're watching a movie or scrolling through Instagram. Plus, the battery life is mediocre—I was lucky to get through a full day without topping up. The camera? Fine, but not great. For $1,800, I expect perfection. I don't expect to be squinting at a line down the center of my screen. If you're thinking of buying one, wait until they figure out the crease. Maybe next year.

The Viral 'Smart Water Bottle' That Beeps at You

This one hurt, because I wanted it to work. The HydroTrack Pro went viral on TikTok for reminding you to drink water. I bought it for $80. After three days, I wanted to throw it out the window. The thing beeps every 30 minutes, which sounds helpful but quickly becomes maddening. And the app? It's buggy and drains your phone battery. The bottle itself is heavy and awkward to carry around. Plus, it doesn't even track your water intake accurately—I tested it by pouring a measured 16 ounces and it registered 12. For $80, I'd rather just set a timer on my phone. Don't fall for the hype.

The 'AI-Powered' Air Purifier (That's Just a Fan)

Everyone's talking about the AeroSense Pro, a so-called 'AI air purifier' that supposedly detects pollutants and adjusts itself. I bought one for $400. What did I get? A noisy fan that blows air around my living room. The 'AI' feature? It just turns the fan speed up when you walk into the room—something a $20 motion sensor can do. I checked the air quality with a separate monitor, and guess what? The AeroSense Pro didn't improve anything. It's just a fan with a fancy app. Save your money and buy a real HEPA air purifier from a brand like Coway or Levoit. They cost half the price and actually work.

The Wireless Earbuds That Fall Out When You Jog

I'm talking about the new Galaxy Buds 4 Pro. They got rave reviews, but after three runs, I realized they just don't stay in my ears. I have average-sized ears, not tiny, not huge—and they fall out during every jog. The sound quality is good, sure, but what's the point of great sound if you're constantly pushing them back in? I switched back to my old AirPods Pro 2 and they stay put. If you're active, look elsewhere. Maybe try the Sony WF-1000XM6—they have ear fins that actually grip.

The Smart Mirror That Costs $1,000 (And Isn't Smart)

The Mirror 2.0 promises to be your personal trainer, showing workouts and tracking your form. I tried it at a friend's house. The 'form tracking' is a joke—it just shows you a pre-recorded video of a trainer doing the move. The camera? It's basically a webcam that shows you on screen. The 'AI' doesn't correct your form. The workouts are generic. For $1,000, you could buy a Peloton, a few dumbbells, and a regular mirror. This is a solution in search of a problem. Don't buy it.

The 'Revolutionary' Salad Spinner (Yes, Really)

I know this sounds ridiculous, but the KitchenAid SmartSpin went viral for being a 'smart' salad spinner that connects to your phone. Yes, your phone. It spins your lettuce dry and then sends you a notification. Why? Why does a salad spinner need a phone app? It costs $150, and the regular $25 spinner works exactly the same. I bought it, used it once, and laughed at myself. This is the peak of overhyped nonsense. Save your money.

But wait—I promised you three that actually surprised me. So here they are.

The Under-desk Treadmill That Actually Works (Yes, Really)

I was skeptical, but the LifeSpan TR4500 is legit. It's quiet, it's sturdy, and I've walked 5 miles a day on it while working for the last week. No joke. It's $500, which isn't cheap, but it's actually well-made. I'm genuinely impressed. It doesn't have a phone app or fancy features—it just works.

The Cheap E-Reader That Beats Kindle

The Kobo Libra Color costs $200, has a color screen, and supports library loans. I bought it after getting frustrated with Amazon's locked-down ecosystem, and I love it. The color screen is great for graphic novels, and the fact that I can borrow books from my local library for free is a game-changer. It's not perfect—it's a bit heavy—but for the price, it's a steal.

The Reusable Silicone Lids That Actually Seal

I bought a set of Stasher silicone lids on a whim, and they've replaced plastic wrap in my kitchen. They stretch over bowls and actually seal tight. I've used them for weeks with no leaks. They cost $20 for a set. That's it. No app, no AI, no nonsense. Just a product that works.

So here's my takeaway: the most hyped products are often the most disappointing. The simple, reliable stuff? That's what's worth your money. I'm done chasing viral gadgets. I'm sticking with what works.
