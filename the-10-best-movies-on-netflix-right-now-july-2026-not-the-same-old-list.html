The 10 Best Movies on Netflix Right Now (July 2026, Not the Same Old List)

TR
By Daniel Wilson · July 19, 2026 · 5 min read

Netflix's algorithm is broken. You know the feeling – you spend 20 minutes scrolling, add three things to your list, and end up rewatching The Office for the hundredth time. I'm guilty of it too. But I decided to break the cycle. Over the past 30 days, I watched every major new Netflix release (yes, all 22 of them) plus a few hidden gems. Some were awful. Some were decent. These 10 are genuinely worth your time in July 2026.

I'm not including obvious classics like Squid Game or Stranger Things – you already know about those. This list is about what's fresh, what's underrated, and what's actually entertaining right now.

1. The Baker's Daughter (Original, Released June 2026)

This is the surprise hit of the summer. A French drama about a young woman who inherits her grandmother's bakery in a small Provençal village. Sounds like every other food movie, right? But the twist is that she's a climate scientist who lost her job, and the bakery becomes a metaphor for traditional knowledge versus modern science. The acting is incredible – Léa Seydoux gives a career-best performance. The bread-making scenes are so detailed you can almost smell it. It's slow, emotional, and utterly beautiful. 98% on Rotten Tomatoes, and deservedly so.

2. Neon Nights (Action Thriller, July 2026)

If you want pure adrenaline, this is it. A cyberpunk-ish thriller set in a near-future Tokyo where AI-powered street gangs control the black market. The lead is John Boyega, playing a disgraced detective who goes underground. The action sequences are mind-blowing – there's a chase through neon-lit Shibuya that's the best I've seen since Mad Max: Fury Road. The plot is thin (AI bad, family good), but you don't watch this for the story. You watch it for the visuals and the fights. It's loud, dumb, and absolutely fun.

3. The Last Library (Documentary, May 2026)

This documentary snuck up on me. It follows the librarians of the New York Public Library as they battle budget cuts, book bans, and a city that's forgetting the value of public spaces. It's not political – it's human. There's a scene where a librarian helps a homeless teenager apply for college that made me tear up. In an era of TikTok and streaming, this film reminds you why physical libraries matter. It's streaming now, and it's essential viewing for anyone who loves books.

4. Echoes of the Forest (Animated, June 2026)

Netflix's animation division has been hit or miss, but this is a hit. It's a Japanese-inspired fantasy about a girl who can communicate with animals. The animation is Studio Ghibli-level gorgeous – hand-drawn backgrounds, fluid character movements. The voice cast includes Awkwafina and Ken Watanabe. The story is simple but heartfelt: about loss, friendship, and protecting nature. My 8-year-old niece loved it. I loved it too. It's a family film that doesn't talk down to kids.

5. Concrete Jungle (Crime Drama, April 2026)

This is the best crime series Netflix has made since Narcos. It's a 6-episode limited series set in 1970s Rio de Janeiro, following a journalist investigating police corruption. The lead is Wagner Moura (Pablo Escobar himself), who also directs. The show is gritty, violent, and morally complex. It doesn't glorify anyone – the cops are corrupt, the criminals are human, and the journalist is flawed. The soundtrack is killer (samba and funk from the era). Binged it in one weekend. No regrets.

6. Skyward Bound (Sci-Fi, July 2026)

Netflix dropped this sci-fi film with almost no marketing, and it's a gem. It's about a group of astronauts who discover a wormhole near Jupiter – but instead of exploring it, they have to decide whether to tell Earth's governments. The tension is psychological, not explosive. It's like Arrival meets Gravity. The lead, Jessie Buckley, gives a haunting performance. The ending left me thinking for days. If you like smart sci-fi, don't miss this.

7. Spice Route (Reality Competition, June 2026)

I'm not usually a reality TV person, but this cooking competition is different. Contestants travel to different countries each episode and must recreate local dishes using only ingredients from that region. The twist? They have to learn from locals who don't speak English. The show is part travelogue, part cooking challenge, part cultural exchange. It's genuinely educational and surprisingly tense. The host is Padma Lakshmi, who brings warmth and expertise. Season 1 just finished, and it's already renewed for season 2.

8. Whispers in the Dark (Horror, May 2026)

Horror fans, this one's for you. It's a Spanish-language film about a family moving into a rural house where the walls seem to whisper. No jump scares, no gore – just slow, creeping dread. The sound design is incredible – you'll find yourself holding your breath. It's the directorial debut of Carla Simón, and it's already being called one of the best horror films of the decade. 92% on Rotten Tomatoes. Watch it with the lights off and the volume up.

9. The Great Escape (Comedy, July 2026)

Sometimes you just need to laugh. This is a buddy comedy about two retirees (played by Steve Martin and Martin Short) who fake their own deaths to escape a nursing home and go on a road trip. It's silly, heartfelt, and packed with jokes. Martin and Short have incredible chemistry – they've been friends for decades, and it shows. It's not Oscar material, but it's the funniest thing I've seen this year. Perfect for a bad day.

10. Lost in Translation (Remastered, June 2026)

Okay, this is technically a re-release, but Netflix just added a 4K remaster of Sofia Coppola's 2003 classic. If you've never seen it, now's your chance. If you have, the remaster makes Tokyo look even more dreamlike. It's still one of the most beautiful films about loneliness and connection. Scarlett Johansson and Bill Murray are mesmerizing. It's streaming now, and it's a reminder of why physical media and remasters matter – this film deserves to be seen in the highest quality possible.

Honorable Mentions

I also want to shout out Dark Waters (a gripping documentary about water pollution in Michigan) and The Chef's Choice (a competition show that's fun but not as good as Spice Route). Avoid Supernova 2026 – it's a disaster movie that's more disaster than movie.

Netflix has a lot of garbage, but these 10 prove they still make great content. My advice? Skip the algorithm. Use this list. And maybe cancel your plans for the weekend – you've got some watching to do.
