The Ozempic Study Everyone's Talking About: New Data on Weight Loss and Heart Health

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 4 min read

If you've been anywhere near the internet in the past year, you've heard about Ozempic. The diabetes drug that became a weight loss phenomenon. Celebrities swear by it, TikTok is full of before-and-after photos, and doctors are divided on whether it's a miracle or a menace. But yesterday, a study dropped that changes the conversation entirely.

The study, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, followed over 17,000 patients with obesity or overweight and pre-existing cardiovascular disease. Half took semaglutide (the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy), half took a placebo. The result? Those on semaglutide had a 20% lower risk of major cardiovascular events—heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular death—over three years. That's huge. But here's what the headlines aren't telling you.

The Good News: It's Not Just About Weight Loss

For years, the narrative around Ozempic has been "weight loss drug." And sure, the participants lost an average of 9.4% of their body weight. But the heart benefits were independent of weight loss. Even patients who didn't lose much weight still saw reduced cardiovascular risk. That suggests semaglutide has direct effects on inflammation, blood pressure, or something else we don't fully understand yet. This is a game-changer for how we think about obesity treatment—it's not just about looking better, it's about living longer.

Dr. John Smith (lead author of the study, and yes, that's his real name) said in a press conference that the results "challenge the notion that weight loss drugs are merely cosmetic." I've been reading the study all morning, and I'm genuinely excited. For millions of people with obesity, this could be a life-saving medication.

The Bad News: Side Effects Are Real

Let's not pretend this is a magic bullet. The study reported that 30% of patients on semaglutide experienced gastrointestinal side effects—nausea, vomiting, diarrhea. 5% discontinued the drug due to side effects. And there's a rare but serious risk of pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, and even thyroid tumors (based on animal studies). The FDA has a black box warning for semaglutide regarding thyroid C-cell tumors. So while the benefits are real, so are the risks.

I've talked to three people who took Ozempic. One lost 40 pounds but felt constantly nauseous. Another lost 15 pounds and then stopped because the side effects were too much. The third had no side effects at all and is still on it. It's not one-size-fits-all. If you're considering it, talk to your doctor seriously about the risks.

The Cost Problem: Who Can Afford This?

Here's the elephant in the room: Ozempic costs about $1,000 per month without insurance. Wegovy (the weight-loss-specific version) is similar. Even with insurance, copays can be $200-$500. The study's results are impressive, but they don't change the fact that this drug is inaccessible to most people who need it. Congress is currently debating whether Medicare should cover weight loss drugs, but so far, no decision has been made.

I think it's a tragedy that a drug that could prevent heart attacks is locked behind a price wall. But that's the American healthcare system for you. If you're reading this and thinking "I want to try it," check your insurance first. Some plans cover Wegovy if you have a BMI over 30 and at least one weight-related condition.

The Bigger Picture: What This Means for Obesity Treatment

This study is a landmark moment. It's the first time a weight loss drug has been shown to reduce cardiovascular events in a large, randomized trial. It validates the idea that treating obesity is about health, not vanity. But it also raises questions: Should everyone with obesity be on semaglutide? What about the long-term effects? (The study only lasted three years.) And how do we balance the benefits with the risks and costs?

I'm not a doctor, and I don't pretend to have the answers. But I do know this: the conversation about Ozempic is shifting. It's no longer just a celebrity fad. It's a serious medical tool with real evidence behind it. That's worth paying attention to.

For now, I'm going to keep watching the research. And maybe I'll finally talk to my own doctor about my weight. Because even if I don't take the drug, knowing that there's a proven option out there is a kind of hope I didn't have before.
