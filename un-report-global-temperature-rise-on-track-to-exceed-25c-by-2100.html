UN Report: Global Temperature Rise on Track to Exceed 2.5°C by 2100

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 250,440 views

A stark new report from the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), released today, June 4, 2026, concludes that the world is on a trajectory to warm by 2.5°C to 2.9°C by the end of the century, far exceeding the Paris Agreement's goal of limiting warming to 1.5°C. The report, titled 'Emissions Gap Report 2026,' calls for 'unprecedented' global action to avert catastrophic climate impacts.

The Findings

The report finds that global greenhouse gas emissions hit a new record high of 58.1 gigatons of CO2 equivalent in 2025, a 1.3% increase from 2024. Despite the expansion of renewable energy, fossil fuel use continues to grow, particularly in the transportation and industrial sectors. The analysis of 193 countries' Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) shows that even if all current pledges are fully implemented, warming would still reach at least 2.5°C. 'We are playing with fire,' said Inger Andersen, UNEP's Executive Director. 'Every fraction of a degree matters, and we are running out of time.'

Regional Disparities

The report highlights stark inequalities in emissions. The top 10% of global emitters, primarily in North America, Europe, and Australia, are responsible for nearly half of all carbon pollution. Meanwhile, the poorest 50% of the world's population contributes just 10% of emissions. 'Climate justice is at the core of this crisis,' said Andersen. 'The most vulnerable are paying the price for the excesses of the wealthy.' The report also notes that extreme weather events, including deadly heatwaves in India and Pakistan this spring, are already becoming more severe and frequent.

Urgent Recommendations

To get back on track, the UNEP recommends that countries must collectively cut emissions by 42% by 2030 and 57% by 2035 compared to 2025 levels. This would require a 'rapid and deep' decarbonization of energy systems, including a tripling of renewable energy capacity and a halving of deforestation by 2030. The report also calls for a phase-out of fossil fuel subsidies, which reached an all-time high of $1.3 trillion in 2025. 'The tools exist—we have the technology and the know-how,' said Andersen. 'What we lack is the political will. The next two years are the most critical in human history.'
