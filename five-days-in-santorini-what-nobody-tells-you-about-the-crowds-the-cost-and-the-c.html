Five Days in Santorini: What Nobody Tells You About the Crowds, the Cost, and the Caldera

TR
By Hannah Powell · July 22, 2026 · 5 min read

I've wanted to go to Santorini for years. You know the photos: whitewashed buildings with blue domes, sunsets that look like they're painted by a god, and infinity pools overlooking the caldera. It's the kind of place that feels like a dream. And honestly, parts of it are. But after spending five days there in July 2026, I learned a few things that Instagram doesn't show you. Let me break it down.

First, the good: Santorini is gorgeous. The caldera views are real. The sunsets are genuinely breathtaking. The food is incredible — think fresh seafood, fava beans, and tomato fritters. But the crowds? They're insane. During peak season, Oia (the famous sunset town) is so packed you can barely walk. I'm not exaggerating. I counted 12 cruise ships in the harbor one day, each dumping thousands of tourists at once. The main streets become human rivers. It's overwhelming.

Day 1: Arrival and the Oia Sunset Madness

I flew into Santorini's airport from Athens — a quick 45-minute flight. The airport is tiny, but it's efficient. I rented a car (highly recommended — more on that later) and drove to my hotel in Imerovigli, a quieter village north of Fira. Imerovigli is often called the "balcony of Santorini" because of its views. I stayed at a small boutique hotel called Anastasia Princess, which was expensive ($450/night) but totally worth it for the caldera view from my room.

That evening, I made the mistake of going to Oia for sunset. I'd read the warnings, but I thought I'd be fine. I wasn't. The streets were shoulder-to-shoulder. People were jostling for spots on the castle ruins. I ended up at a rooftop bar called Kastro Oia, which had a minimum spend of $60 per person. The sunset was beautiful — orange and pink over the caldera — but the experience was stressful. If I could do it again, I'd watch sunset from Imerovigli or a quieter spot. Oia is not worth the hassle.

Day 2: Exploring Fira and the Caldera Trail

Day two, I hiked the Caldera Trail from Fira to Oia. It's a 10-kilometer path along the edge of the caldera, with views of the volcano and the sea. It took about three hours at a moderate pace. The trail is well-marked and passes through several villages. It's beautiful, but it's not easy — there are steep sections and uneven steps. Bring water and good shoes. The best part was walking through Skaros Rock, a rocky outcrop with ruins of a Venetian castle. It's less crowded than Oia and offers stunning views.

In Fira, I visited the Museum of Prehistoric Thira, which houses artifacts from the ancient city of Akrotiri (destroyed by the volcano around 1600 BC). It's small but fascinating. The admission is €6. Worth it if you're into history. For lunch, I ate at a taverna called Pelican Kipos, which has a garden setting and serves excellent moussaka. The service was slow, but the food was worth the wait.

Day 3: Boat Trip to the Volcano and Hot Springs

I booked a half-day boat trip to Nea Kameni, the volcanic island in the middle of the caldera. The tour cost €50 and included a hike to the crater, a stop at the hot springs, and a visit to the island of Thirassia. The hike to the crater is short but steep — about 20 minutes up a rocky path. The crater is active, and you can see steam rising from the vents. It's a surreal experience.

The hot springs were disappointing. They're not hot — more like lukewarm, and the water is murky with sulfur. Plus, you have to swim from the boat to a shallow area, which is cold. I'd skip the hot springs and spend more time on Thirassia, which has a charming village with empty streets and great views of the caldera. I ate lunch at a taverna called To Psaraki, which had the best grilled octopus I've ever had. No crowds, just good food and a view.

Day 4: Akrotiri and the Red Beach

Day four, I visited the ancient ruins of Akrotiri. It's often called the "Minoan Pompeii" because it was buried in volcanic ash. The site is covered by a modern roof that protects the ruins. It's well-preserved — you can see multi-story buildings, frescoes, and drainage systems. The admission is €12. It's a must-see for history buffs.

Nearby is the Red Beach, famous for its red volcanic sand. The beach is accessible via a short hike from the parking lot. The sand is more like pebbles, and the water is clear. But it was packed — people everywhere. I stayed for 20 minutes and left. If you want a less crowded beach, try Vlychada Beach, which has white cliffs and a lunar-like landscape. It's a 15-minute drive from Akrotiri and much quieter.

Day 5: Food, Wine, and Departure

My last day was all about food and wine. Santorini is famous for its Assyrtiko wine, a crisp white that pairs perfectly with seafood. I visited Domaine Sigalas, one of the island's best wineries. The tasting was €25 and included five wines. The views from the terrace are gorgeous — overlooking the caldera with the vines in the foreground. I bought a bottle of their Vinsanto (a sweet dessert wine) to take home.

For lunch, I had a final meal at a restaurant in Pyrgos called Metaxi Mas. It's off the beaten path, with no caldera views, but the food is incredible. I had fava (a creamy split pea purée), tomato fritters, and grilled lamb chops. The total was €35, which is reasonable for Santorini. After lunch, I drove to the airport and flew home, exhausted but happy.

The Bottom Line: Is Santorini Worth It?

Yes, but with caveats. Santorini is beautiful, but it's expensive and crowded. A typical dinner for two with wine can easily run €100. Hotels are pricey — expect to pay at least €200/night for something decent. But if you plan carefully, you can avoid the worst of the crowds. Visit in shoulder season (May, June, or September). Rent a car to explore the less touristy villages. Skip Oia sunset in favor of a quieter spot. And don't forget to try the wine.

Santorini is a dream destination, but it's also a real place with real problems. Go in with your eyes open, and you'll have an amazing time. Just don't expect it to be empty.
