I Ate at the World's First AI-Run Restaurant. It Was Weird (But Kind of Great)

TR
By Matthew Anderson · July 20, 2026 · 5 min read

Let me set the scene. It's 7 PM on a Tuesday in San Francisco's SoMa district. I'm standing outside a restaurant called 'Automata' that opened on June 15, 2026. The sign says 'Fully AI-Powered Kitchen' and 'No Human Chefs.' I'd read about it in Wired and thought it was probably a gimmick — something like those robot sushi places in Japan that are fun for five minutes but don't actually produce good food. But I was curious. And hungry. So I walked in.

The space is minimalist — white walls, concrete floors, an open kitchen with robotic arms moving in precise, mechanical choreography. There are no flames. No shouting. No clanging pans. Just the hum of machinery and the occasional hiss of steam. A human hostess (the only human I'd interact with) seated me and handed me a tablet with the menu. The menu changes daily based on what the AI 'decides' to cook, using inventory and seasonal availability. That night, it was a four-course tasting menu with a wine pairing. Price: $85. Which, for a tasting menu in SF, is actually reasonable.

How It Works: The Tech Behind the Kitchen

I asked to speak with the head chef. They laughed. There isn't one. The kitchen is run by a system called 'ChefGPT,' developed by a startup called Dish Intelligence. It uses a combination of computer vision, robotic manipulators, and a large language model trained on 50,000 recipes from Michelin-starred restaurants. The robot arms can chop, stir, sear, plate, and even taste using a spectrometer that analyzes chemical compounds. The AI decides the plating, adjusts seasoning in real-time, and learns from each dish it produces.

Here's where it gets wild: the AI doesn't just repeat recipes. It improvises. The menu said 'Pan-seared scallops with fennel puree and saffron foam.' I watched the robot assemble it — the scallops were placed with millimeter precision, the foam was piped in a perfect circle, and the fennel puree was swirled into a pattern that looked almost artistic. It was eerie. But also impressive.

The Food: Did It Actually Taste Good?

Let's get to the important question. I'm a pretty serious home cook. I've spent years learning techniques, understanding flavor combinations, and developing a palate. I was ready to hate this. I was prepared to say 'see, robots can't replicate human intuition.' But I have to be honest: the first course — a chilled corn soup with smoked paprika oil — was genuinely excellent. The texture was velvety. The smokiness was balanced. The sweetness of the corn was pronounced. I closed my eyes and couldn't tell it wasn't made by a human.

The scallops were cooked perfectly — golden crust, translucent center, not a hint of rubberiness. The fennel puree was smooth and aromatic. The saffron foam was light and floral. Second course: a duck breast with cherry gastrique and roasted vegetables. The duck skin was crispy. The meat was medium-rare. The gastrique had that perfect sweet-sour balance. I was starting to get annoyed at how good it was.

Dessert was a dark chocolate tart with salted caramel and olive oil. The crust was flaky. The chocolate was rich but not cloying. The olive oil added a peppery finish. It was, objectively, a very good dessert. I found myself scraping the plate clean. Then I felt conflicted. Was I supposed to enjoy food made by a machine? Was this a betrayal of something fundamental about cooking?

The Weird Part: What's Missing

Here's the thing. The food was technically excellent. But it lacked something I can only describe as 'soul.' I know that sounds pretentious, but hear me out. Great food tells a story. It reflects the cook's personality, their history, their mood that day. A human chef might decide to add a little extra spice because they're feeling bold, or plate something messy because they want it to feel rustic. The AI doesn't have that. It optimizes for perfection, but perfection isn't always what makes food memorable.

I also missed the theater of a kitchen. At a good restaurant, I like watching the chefs work. The chaos, the collaboration, the occasional curse when something burns. Automata is silent. The robots move with cold precision. It's impressive, but it's not warm. I had a conversation with the hostess about this. She said they get that feedback a lot. 'People either love it or find it unsettling,' she told me. 'There's no in-between.'

The Bigger Picture: Is This the Future?

Automata isn't alone. Similar AI-powered kitchens are opening in Tokyo, London, and Dubai. The economics are compelling: no sick days, no labor disputes, no training costs, consistent quality. The restaurant industry is facing a labor crisis — cooks are burned out, underpaid, and leaving in droves. AI kitchens could solve that. But at what cost?

I talked to a friend who's a sous chef at a Michelin-starred restaurant in New York. She was horrified. 'Cooking is an art,' she said. 'It's about human connection. You're not going to get that from a machine.' I see her point. But I also see the appeal for restaurant owners. And honestly, for diners who just want a reliably excellent meal without the price tag of a celebrity chef.

I don't think AI will replace all chefs. But I do think it will carve out a niche — fast-casual, upscale fast food, hotel buffets, maybe even some fine dining experiences where the novelty itself is the draw. Automata is booked solid for the next two months. People are curious. And the food is good. So maybe there's room for both. Maybe we can have robot-made scallops on Tuesday and a human-cooked pasta on Thursday. The future isn't all or nothing.

As I left, I grabbed a card. It said 'Every plate is a perfect plate.' I don't know if I want every plate to be perfect. Sometimes I want the imperfect one made by someone who cares. But I'm glad Automata exists. It made me think. And it made me a really, really good duck breast.
