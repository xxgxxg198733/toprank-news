The Top 10 AI Tools I Actually Use in 2026 (And Which Ones Are Overhyped)

TR
By Sarah Mitchell · July 22, 2026 · 5 min read

I'll be honest: I'm tired of AI hype. Every week there's a new tool that promises to revolutionise your workflow, and 90% of them are just wrappers around ChatGPT with a nicer UI. But some actually deliver. After spending the last six months testing over 30 AI tools for my work as a writer and consultant, I've narrowed it down to the ones I actually open every day.

This isn't a list of the 'best' AI tools in some abstract sense. It's a list of tools that have changed how I work — for real. Some are obvious (yes, ChatGPT is on here), but there are a few surprises that most people haven't heard of. Let's get into it.

1. ChatGPT (GPT-5) — Still the King, But It Has Competition

Look, ChatGPT is still the default for a reason. GPT-5, which launched in late 2025, is genuinely impressive. The reasoning is better, the hallucinations are rarer, and the new 'deep research' mode can write 5,000-word reports that actually cite sources correctly. I use it for first drafts, brainstorming, and summarising long documents. It's not perfect — sometimes it's too confident about wrong answers — but it's my go-to.

Overhyped? Sort of. Most people don't need $200/month Pro tier. The free version with GPT-4o is good enough for 80% of tasks.

2. Claude 4 (Anthropic) — Better for Long-Form Writing

I switched to Claude for my actual writing. The new Claude 4 model has a 200K token context window — that's like 150 pages of text. I paste entire articles into it and ask for inline edits, and it handles them without losing track. The tone is more natural, less robotic than ChatGPT. For creative work, it's my secret weapon.

Not overhyped. It's genuinely underrated by mainstream users.

3. Perplexity Pro — The Search Engine That Actually Works

Google Search is dying — the results are full of spam and AI-generated garbage. Perplexity Pro (now with GPT-4 integration) gives me direct answers with citations from real sources. I use it for research constantly. The 'collections' feature lets me save searches by topic, which is great for ongoing projects.

One caveat: it's not great for local search (find me a pizza place nearby). But for research? It's replaced Google for me entirely.

4. Otter.ai — The Meeting Transcriber That Saves My Sanity

I have too many meetings. Otter.ai joins my Zoom calls, records them, and generates transcripts with speaker labels. It also creates AI summaries — bullet points of action items. I've stopped taking notes entirely. The accuracy is excellent, even with accents and technical jargon.

Overhyped? No. It's a simple tool that does one thing perfectly.

5. Midjourney V7 — Still the Best Image Generator

DALL-E 4 is good, Stable Diffusion 4 is open-source, but Midjourney V7 is still the king of aesthetics. The new 'style reference' feature lets you upload an image and generate new ones in the same visual style. I used it to create a consistent look for a presentation deck, and it saved me hours of design work. The Discord interface is still annoying, but the results are worth it.

Overhyped? If you're not doing visual work, skip it. But for creators, it's essential.

6. Notion AI — The Best AI for Project Management

Notion has been my note-taking app for years. The AI add-on ($10/month) is surprisingly good. It can write meeting notes, generate project plans, and even translate documents. I used it to draft a 20-page proposal in an hour — something that would have taken me two days. The killer feature is the Q&A: you can ask 'What's the status of Project X?' and it searches all your notes for the answer.

Not overhyped. It makes an already great app even better.

7. Suno AI — Music Generation That's Actually Fun

Okay, this one is more for fun than productivity. Suno AI generates original songs from text prompts. I typed 'upbeat indie folk song about a cat who thinks he's a dog' and got a genuinely catchy tune with lyrics, vocals, and instrumentation. It's not going to win a Grammy, but for making silly songs for friends or generating background music for videos, it's incredible.

Overhyped? For serious musicians, yes. For everyone else, it's a blast.

8. Grammarly Premium — Still Worth It in 2026

I know, I know — Grammarly is old news. But the 2026 version with AI writing suggestions is genuinely good. It catches nuance, suggests better phrasing, and even helps with tone (e.g., 'this sounds aggressive, try this instead'). I use it for all my professional emails and client communications. The free version is fine, but Premium's tone detection is worth the $12/month.

Not overhyped. It's a tool that's quietly improved over years.

9. Copy.ai — Best for Marketing Copy

I hate writing ad copy. Copy.ai makes it painless. I type 'write 10 Facebook ad variants for a new organic coffee brand targeting millennials' and it spits out usable options in 30 seconds. The new 'brand voice' feature lets you save your writing style and apply it consistently. I've used it for email campaigns, landing pages, and even YouTube descriptions.

Overhyped? It's not a full replacement for a copywriter, but it's a great assistant.

10. GitHub Copilot — Indispensable for Developers

I'm not a developer, but I dabble in Python for data analysis. Copilot is like having a senior dev looking over your shoulder. It suggests completions, writes boilerplate, and even debugs code. My developer friends swear by it. The new 'agent mode' can build entire small apps from a description. It's wild.

Not overhyped. It's genuinely transformative for coding.

So there's my list. The AI tools that have earned a permanent spot in my workflow. The key takeaway? Don't chase hype. Find tools that solve a specific problem you actually have. For me, that's research, writing, meetings, and creative work. Yours might be different. But these 10? They're the real deal in 2026.
