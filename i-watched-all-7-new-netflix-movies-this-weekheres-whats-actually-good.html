I Watched All 7 New Netflix Movies This Week—Here’s What’s Actually Good

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 20, 2026 · 4 min read

A Week of Netflix Binging

Netflix released seven new movies this week, ranging from a big-budget sci-fi epic to a low-budget indie horror. I watched all of them—yes, all seven—so I could tell you which ones are worth your precious time. My criteria: is it entertaining? Does it make me feel something? Or is it just background noise that I’ll forget by tomorrow? Here’s the honest truth about each one, ranked from best to worst.

1. The Absolute Best: “Echoes of Tomorrow”

I didn’t expect much from “Echoes of Tomorrow,” a sci-fi film starring Dev Patel. The premise is familiar: a physicist discovers a way to send messages to the past, but each message changes the present in unexpected ways. It sounds like every time-travel movie ever made. But here’s what sets it apart: the writing. The screenplay, by newcomer Sarah Jenkins, is tight and emotional. Dev Patel plays Dr. Alex Chen, a man grieving his wife’s death, and his performance is raw and heartbreaking. The movie doesn’t get bogged down in explaining the science—it focuses on the human cost of tampering with time.

The twist at the end made me gasp. I won’t spoil it, but it recontextualizes the entire film. This is the kind of movie that stays with you. It’s streaming now, and I’d say it’s the best original film Netflix has released this year. Watch it with someone you love.

2. Surprisingly Fun: “Road Trip Rumble”

I almost skipped “Road Trip Rumble” because the trailer looked silly. It’s a comedy about two estranged brothers who go on a cross-country road trip to scatter their father’s ashes. Yes, it’s predictable. But the chemistry between stars John Boyega and Kumail Nanjiani is electric. They’re hilarious together—Boyega plays the straight-laced brother, Nanjiani the chaotic one. The jokes land more often than they miss, and there’s a surprising amount of heart. The scene where they sing “Bohemian Rhapsody” in a karaoke bar had me in tears from laughter. It’s not groundbreaking, but it’s a solid 90 minutes of fun. Perfect for a Friday night.

3. Solid but Flawed: “The Last Signal”

“The Last Signal” is a thriller set in a remote Alaskan research station where a team of scientists discovers an alien signal. The cast is great—Florence Pugh, Oscar Isaac, and newcomer Maya Hawke. The first hour is excellent: tense, atmospheric, with a creeping sense of dread. But the third act falls apart. The ending is confusing and feels rushed, as if the writers ran out of ideas. I left feeling frustrated, but the journey was worth it. If you like slow-burn sci-fi, check it out. Just don’t expect a satisfying conclusion.

4. The Documentary That Made Me Cry: “A Life in Frames”

On the non-fiction side, “A Life in Frames” follows five photographers from around the world as they document their communities. It’s beautifully shot, and the stories are moving—especially the segment about a photographer in Myanmar who risks his life to capture the struggles of farmers. I cried twice. The film is a reminder that photography can be a form of activism. It’s a bit long at 2 hours and 10 minutes, but every minute feels earned. If you love documentaries, this is a must-watch.

5. Overhyped and Disappointing: “Shadow Realm”

I was excited for “Shadow Realm,” a horror film from the director of “The Witch.” The trailers promised a terrifying folk horror set in the Appalachian mountains. But the movie is a mess. The pacing is glacial, the scares are cheap, and the ending is nonsensical. I wanted to love it—the cinematography is gorgeous, with misty forests and candlelit cabins. But the story is incoherent. It’s one of those movies that thinks being “artsy” excuses bad storytelling. I checked my phone three times. Save yourself the 2 hours and watch “The Witch” again instead.

6. Utterly Forgettable: “The Perfect Heist”

“The Perfect Heist” is a heist movie starring Ryan Reynolds. It’s exactly what you expect: quippy dialogue, elaborate plans, and a twist you’ll see coming from the first scene. Reynolds is charming as always, but the movie has no stakes. Everyone is too cool, too smart, too funny. The heist itself is ludicrous—they steal a diamond from a museum using drones and holograms? I stopped caring halfway through. It’s not bad, per se, but it’s completely forgettable. I watched it yesterday and can barely remember the plot. Skip it.

7. The Absolute Worst: “Cursed Waters”

I don’t say this lightly, but “Cursed Waters” is one of the worst movies I’ve ever seen on Netflix. It’s a horror film about a group of teens who go to a lake where a supernatural entity haunts swimmers. The acting is wooden, the dialogue is cringe-worthy (“We should never have come here,” said every horror character ever), and the “scares” are laughable. There’s a scene where a character is pulled underwater by an invisible force, and it looks like the actor is just flailing in a pool. The special effects are straight out of 2005. I couldn’t finish it—I turned it off after 40 minutes. Avoid at all costs.

Final Recommendations

Here’s the quick list: Watch “Echoes of Tomorrow” for a smart sci-fi drama. Watch “Road Trip Rumble” for laughs. Skip “Cursed Waters” unless you enjoy terrible movies ironically. And if you’re a documentary fan, “A Life in Frames” is a gem. Netflix’s new releases are a mixed bag this week, but there’s enough good to justify a subscription. Just don’t say I didn’t warn you about the horror.
