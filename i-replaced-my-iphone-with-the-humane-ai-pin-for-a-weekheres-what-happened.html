I Replaced My iPhone with the Humane AI Pin for a Week—Here’s What Happened

TR
By Daniel Wilson · July 20, 2026 · 5 min read

The Hype Around Screenless Computing

When Humane launched the AI Pin in late 2024, it felt like science fiction. A small device you clip to your shirt that projects a display onto your palm, powered by AI? No phone needed. I was skeptical, but also intrigued. Could this really replace my iPhone? I decided to find out. For one week, I left my phone at home and used only the Humane AI Pin for calls, messages, navigation, and information. Here’s how it went—spoiler: it was both frustrating and fascinating.

Day 1: The First Hour Is Magical

The AI Pin comes in a sleek box that feels more like jewelry than tech. You charge it via a magnetic dock, then clip it to your shirt using a magnet that’s surprisingly strong. Setting up takes about 10 minutes: connect to Wi-Fi, pair with your Humane account, and you’re off. The device talks to you through a speaker or a pair of bone-conduction earbuds (sold separately).

For the first hour, I was amazed. I asked, “What’s the weather like today?” and the Pin answered instantly: “It’s 72 degrees in San Francisco, partly cloudy.” I asked for the nearest coffee shop, and a small green dot appeared on my palm, guiding me with a map projected via a laser. It felt like the future. But then I tried to text my wife.

The Texting Nightmare

You can send texts via voice. You say, “Tell my wife I’m running late,” and the Pin transcribes your message and sends it. In theory, it’s great. In practice, it misheard me three times. “I’m running late” became “I’m running lake,” then “I’m running bait.” By the third attempt, I was shouting, and the Pin said, “I can’t process that right now.” My wife later told me she got a message that said, “I am running bait.” I had to borrow a stranger’s phone to explain.

Voice recognition is better on a phone because you can see the transcription and edit it. With the Pin, it’s one-shot, and if it fails, you start over. That’s a fundamental flaw for anyone who texts regularly.

Navigation: Good, But Not Great

I used the Pin for navigation a few times. You say, “Navigate to the nearest grocery store,” and a small green line appears on your palm. It’s minimalist—just a line and a distance readout. No street names, no landmarks. I found myself looking at my palm while walking, which felt weird and slightly dangerous. I nearly walked into a pole.

The real problem is that the display is hard to see in bright sunlight. The laser projection works best indoors or in shade. Outdoors, it’s washed out. And there’s no audio navigation unless you use the earbuds, which I forgot to bring. I ended up using my memory and guessing. It worked sometimes, but I wouldn’t trust it in a new city.

Battery Life: The Pin Dies Faster Than You Think

Humane claims the AI Pin lasts about 4 hours of active use. In reality, I got about 3 hours before it died. The device runs hot too—after 20 minutes of talking to it, the clip felt warm against my shirt. The battery pack (a separate magnetic booster) adds another 4 hours, but it’s bulky and looks like a pager strapped to your chest. I charged the Pin twice a day, which was annoying.

Compare that to my iPhone, which lasts all day. The Pin is clearly not designed for heavy users. It’s more of a “digital assistant for short interactions” than a full replacement.

The Camera: Surprisingly Good Photos

One area where the Pin shines is the camera. You can take photos by tapping the device twice. The 13MP sensor captures sharp, well-exposed images that look better than I expected. The AI automatically crops and adjusts colors. I took a photo of a sunset, and it looked like something from a Pixel phone. The built-in AI can also describe scenes for visually impaired users, which is a nice touch.

But there’s no viewfinder. You point the camera roughly where you think the subject is, and hope for the best. I missed several shots because the framing was off. It’s like shooting with a disposable camera—fun, but not reliable.

AI Features: The Best Part (and the Worst)

The Pin’s AI is powered by GPT-4 and a custom model from Humane. It can answer questions, summarize articles, and translate languages. I asked it, “What’s the capital of Mongolia?” and it answered “Ulaanbaatar” instantly. I asked for a recipe for chicken tikka masala, and it read me the ingredients. These moments felt magical.

But the AI also hallucinates. I asked, “What’s the latest news about the Lakers?” and it told me they won a game that never happened. When I corrected it, the Pin said, “I apologize for the error,” but offered no source. That’s a problem if you rely on it for accurate information. Also, there’s no screen to fact-check—you just have to trust it.

Humane says they’re updating the AI weekly, but during my week, I encountered a few obvious errors. It’s a beta product, and it shows.

Should You Buy It? Not Yet

The Humane AI Pin costs $699 plus a $24 monthly subscription (includes cellular data, AI access, and cloud storage). That’s expensive for a device that can’t reliably send a text. I wanted to love it—the concept of a screenless, always-on AI assistant is compelling. But in practice, it’s too buggy, too limited, and too dependent on perfect conditions.

Who is this for? Maybe early adopters who love gadgets for the sake of innovation. Or people who want to reduce screen time and don’t mind sacrificing functionality. But for me, I’m sticking with my iPhone for now. The AI Pin is a fascinating glimpse of the future, but it’s not ready for the present. I’ll check back in a year.
