10 Must-Watch Movies on Netflix, HBO, and Amazon This July 2026

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 20, 2026 · 4 min read

I Spent a Week Binge-Watching So You Don't Have To

Let's be real: scrolling through streaming services has become a part-time job. I swear, half my evenings are spent staring at covers, reading synopses, and then giving up to rewatch The Office. But not this week. I carved out some serious couch time (yes, my back hurts) and dug through Netflix, HBO Max, and Amazon Prime to find the 10 movies actually worth your time right now. It's July 2026, and the summer blockbusters are hitting hard, but so are some quiet little films that'll sneak up on you.

1. The Last Signal (Netflix) – The Year's Smartest Sci-Fi

I went into this one with low expectations, honestly. Another Netflix sci-fi? But director Amma Asante has crafted something special. It's about a team of astronomers who detect a signal from a dying star—and then things get weird. No space battles, just pure tension. The performances are killer, especially by Dev Patel, who plays a guilt-ridden physicist. I was on the edge of my seat. It's currently sitting at 94% on Rotten Tomatoes, and for once, I agree with the critics.

2. Midnight in Marrakech (Amazon Prime) – Pure Escapism

Look, sometimes you just need a movie that looks like a vacation. This heist film set in Marrakech is gorgeous. The colors, the markets, the rooftops—it's a visual feast. But it's also genuinely funny. The chemistry between the two leads (Florence Pugh and John Boyega) is electric. It's not deep, but it's the most fun I've had with a movie this year. Perfect for a Friday night when your brain is fried.

3. The Architect's Apprentice (HBO Max) – A Quiet Masterpiece

This is the film nobody's talking about, and that's a crime. It's a slow-burn drama set in 16th-century Istanbul, about a young boy who becomes the apprentice to the great architect Sinan. The attention to detail is insane—the mosques, the tiles, the light. It's meditative. I had to pause it twice just to breathe. It won the Palme d'Or at Cannes last month, and it deserves every award. Bring tissues.

4. Circuit Breaker (Netflix) – Action That Actually Works

I'm picky about action movies. Most of them are just noise. But this one, directed by Chad Stahelski (yes, the John Wick guy), is different. It's about a former Olympic gymnast who becomes a bike messenger in a near-future city. The chase scenes are choreographed like dance. I rewound the first sequence three times. It's lean, mean, and under 100 minutes. Thank you, Chad.

5. The Dinner Party (Amazon Prime) – You Will Argue About This Ending

This is the movie that'll split your friend group. It's a psychological thriller about six old friends who reunite for dinner—and then secrets come out. The whole thing is shot in one house, and it's suffocating in the best way. The ending is ambiguous, and I've seen people fighting about it online. I loved it. My friend hated it. That's the kind of movie I respect.

6. Echoes of Youth (HBO Max) – The Documentary That Broke Me

I don't cry easily at movies, but this documentary had me sobbing. It follows three teenagers in rural Appalachia as they prepare for a statewide poetry slam. It's about poverty, ambition, and the power of words. One kid recites a poem about his mother, and I had to pause. It's on HBO Max and it's essential viewing. Seriously, just watch it.

7. Neon Nights (Netflix) – The Surprise Hit of the Summer

Here's a fun one: a neon-noir set in 1980s Tokyo, about a jazz trumpeter who gets mixed up with the Yakuza. It's stylish as hell, with a killer soundtrack. The lead, a newcomer named Aoi Nakamura, is magnetic. It's not perfect—the plot gets a little messy in the second act—but the vibe is undeniable. I've already watched it twice.

8. The Garden of Whispers (Amazon Prime) – For When You Need to Slow Down

This is an Iranian film about an elderly woman who tends a secret garden in Tehran. There's almost no dialogue. Just images. It's the most peaceful movie I've seen in years. If you're feeling anxious or overwhelmed, put this on. It's like a deep breath.

9. Roadside (HBO Max) – A Road Trip Movie That Feels Real

Most road trip movies are fake. The conversations are too witty, the landscapes too perfect. This one, starring Steven Yeun and Awkwafina, feels like a real drive across the American Southwest. They argue about stupid things. They get lost. They eat bad gas station food. It's warm and funny and sad. I loved every minute.

10. The Fall of the House of Usher (Netflix) – Yes, It's a Movie Now

Mike Flanagan's adaptation of Poe's story got turned into a film version this year, and it's terrifying. Not jump-scare terrifying—existential terrifying. The sound design will crawl under your skin. I watched it alone at midnight and regretted it. But I couldn't stop. It's a masterclass in atmosphere.

My Honest Takeaway

I went into this thinking I'd find maybe three or four good movies. Instead, I found ten that I'd happily watch again. Streaming is overwhelming, but if you pick carefully, there's gold in there. Start with The Architect's Apprentice. Then watch The Dinner Party and text me your take on the ending. I bet you'll be on one side or the other.
