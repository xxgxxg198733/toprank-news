Mission: Impossible – The Final Reckoning Review: Tom Cruise Did It Again (and I'm Exhausted)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 22, 2026 · 3 min read

I walked into the theater for Mission: Impossible – The Final Reckoning with high expectations. The last one, Dead Reckoning Part One, was a blast—a globe-trotting spy thriller with a train sequence that made my palms sweat. But I also had doubts. Can Tom Cruise, at 63, still pull off the insane stunts? Has the franchise run out of steam? And is this actually the final film, or just another Hollywood lie?

I saw it on Thursday night at a packed IMAX screening in San Francisco. The audience clapped at the end. I clapped too. But my feelings are complicated. Let me break it down.

The Stunt: Cruise Jumps a Motorcycle Off a Cliff (Again)

You've seen the trailer. Cruise rides a motorcycle off a cliff in Norway, base jumps, and then skydives into a moving train. It's real—no CGI. He did it 130 times for the perfect take. The sequence is breathtaking. The camera follows him in real time, and you feel the wind and the speed. It's the best action set piece of 2026 so far. But here's the thing: it's the opening scene. After that, the movie slows down for 45 minutes of exposition. I felt like I'd already peaked.

The Plot: Espionage Nonsense (But the Good Kind)

The story picks up right after Dead Reckoning Part One. Ethan Hunt (Cruise) is still chasing the Entity (an evil AI) that threatens global security. This time, he's joined by his old team: Luther (Ving Rhames), Benji (Simon Pegg), and Ilsa (Rebecca Ferguson, who's back despite her apparent death in the last film—no spoilers, but it's handled well). The villain is Gabriel (Esai Morales), a former MI6 agent with a personal grudge. The plot involves a nuclear submarine, a secret base in the Arctic, and a MacGuffin called the "Key of Chronos" that can control the Entity. It's convoluted and silly, but the movie doesn't take itself too seriously. The dialogue is sharp, and there are genuine laughs—Benji's panic in a tight spot is comedy gold.

The Action: Nonstop, But Sometimes Exhausting

The action is relentless. There's a car chase through Rome (a homage to The Italian Job), a fistfight on top of a moving plane, and a third-act underwater sequence that rivals Mission: Impossible – Rogue Nation's underwater scene. The choreography is stunning. Cruise does his own stunts, and you can feel the commitment. But the editing is frenetic—some cuts are too fast, making it hard to follow the geography of the fights. I wished the director, Christopher McQuarrie, had let some shots breathe. The John Wick movies have longer takes, and they're more immersive.

The Performances: Cruise Carries the Weight

Tom Cruise is the heart of this franchise, and he gives his all. There's a scene where he runs through a market in Dubai (yes, he runs again), and his face shows exhaustion and determination. It's not just a stunt—it's acting. Rebecca Ferguson is underused but brilliant in her limited screen time. Simon Pegg provides comic relief, and Ving Rhames brings gravitas. Hayley Atwell returns as Grace, a thief turned ally, and she holds her own. The real standout is Esai Morales—his Gabriel is cold, calculating, and genuinely threatening. The final confrontation between Ethan and Gabriel is tense and emotional, with a twist I didn't see coming.

The Verdict: A Solid Ending, But Not the Best

So, is The Final Reckoning the best Mission: Impossible movie? No. That honor still belongs to Fallout (2018), which had a tighter script, better pacing, and a more iconic villain (Henry Cavill's John Lark). But it's a worthy conclusion. The emotional beats land—Ethan's sacrifice, his team's loyalty, the idea that one person can make a difference. The final scene is a gut punch that ties back to the first film. I won't spoil it, but it made me tear up.

Is it actually the final film? Probably not. Cruise has hinted at more, and Paramount will want to milk the franchise. But if this is the end, it's a good one. Go see it in IMAX. You won't regret it. Just be prepared to be exhausted.
