Santorini in 2026: How to Beat the Crowds and See the Real Island

TR
By Emily Watson · July 23, 2026 · 7 min read

I'll be honest: I almost didn't go to Santorini this year. Every Instagram post shows the same thing — a sea of people crammed into Oia, all trying to get that sunset photo with the blue domes. I read that over 2 million tourists visit each year, and with cruise ships dropping off thousands daily, it sounded like a nightmare. But my girlfriend had been dreaming of it for years, so we booked a 10-day trip in early July 2026. And you know what? It was amazing. But only because we avoided the crowds.

Here's the thing about Santorini: the famous spots are beautiful, but they're also crowded, expensive, and overhyped. The real magic is in the hidden corners — the quiet villages, the secret beaches, the local tavernas where the owner brings you free dessert just because you're nice. This article is about how to experience that Santorini, not the tourist trap version.

Where to Stay: Skip Oia and Fira

Oia is the postcard village with the famous blue domes. It's gorgeous, but it's also a zoo. During sunset, the main pedestrian street is shoulder-to-shoulder. I counted 200 people waiting at the famous blue dome viewpoint. It's not a relaxing experience. Fira is the capital, and it's slightly better — more shops and restaurants — but it's still a cruise ship hub with crowds from 9 AM to 6 PM.

Instead, stay in a smaller village. We booked a cave house in Pyrgos, which is a 15-minute drive from Fira but feels like a different world. Pyrgos is a charming hilltop village with narrow alleys, a Venetian castle, and maybe 5 restaurants. The views from the top are incredible — you can see the entire caldera without the selfie sticks. Our Airbnb cost $120 per night, which is half of what you'd pay in Oia for a room with a partial view.

Other underrated villages: Megalochori (wine country with a lovely square), Akrotiri (near the archaeological site), and Emporio (quiet and authentic). Each has its own character, and none are overrun with tourists — yet.

When to Go (and When to Run)

July and August are peak season. That's when we went, and it was hot (35°C/95°F) and crowded. If you can, go in May, June, September, or October. The weather is still beautiful, and the crowds are thinner. I talked to a local shop owner who said May is paradise — the flowers are blooming, the temperatures are mild, and the cruise ships haven't started their daily invasions yet.

But if you must go in summer, here's the trick: do everything early or late. We woke up at 6 AM to visit Oia (yes, 6 AM). The village was empty. I got photos of the blue domes with no one in them. By 9 AM, the first tour buses arrived, and we were already sipping coffee in a quiet café. For sunset, we skipped Oia entirely and watched from the castle in Pyrgos. There were maybe 10 other people there. The view was just as good, minus the crowds.

The Secret Beaches (That Aren't Red Beach)

Red Beach is famous for its red volcanic sand, but it's also famous for being packed. We went there at 8 AM and had it to ourselves for an hour, but by 10 AM, it was a sardine can. Plus, there's a risk of rockfalls (the area is unstable), so it's not the safest choice.

Instead, try these:

Koloumbes Beach: A black sand beach near Oia that most tourists don't know about. The water is crystal clear, and there's a small taverna where you can get grilled octopus and a cold beer. We spent an entire afternoon there with maybe 20 other people. It's rocky, so bring water shoes.

Vlychada Beach: This one has white volcanic cliffs that look almost lunar. The beach is long and wide, so even when a few people arrive, it doesn't feel crowded. There's a beach club with sunbeds for €15, or you can bring your own towel.

Eros Beach (also called Thermis Beach): A secluded pebble beach at the southern tip. You need to hike down a dirt path to reach it, which keeps the crowds away. The water is incredibly blue, and there are no umbrellas or bars — just you and the sea. Bring your own water and snacks.

Food: Where the Locals Eat

Tourist restaurants in Oia and Fira charge €25 for a basic pasta and the service is rushed. The real food is in the inland villages. We had the best meal of our trip at a tiny taverna in Megalochori called To Gerani. It's run by a family who's been there for 40 years. The grilled lamb chops were perfect — smoky, tender, and served with a side of roasted potatoes and a Greek salad. The bill for two people with wine: €35. The same meal in Fira would be €70.

Another gem: Selene in Pyrgos. It's a high-end restaurant that focuses on modern Greek cuisine, but the prices are reasonable (€40 per person for a tasting menu). The chef uses local ingredients — capers from the island, tomatoes from the valley, and cheese from nearby Naxos. The wine list features Santorini's famous Assyrtiko white wine, which is crisp and minerally. Book ahead, though — it fills up fast.

For a quick bite, look for bakeries selling "kolouri" (sesame bread rings) and "spanakopita" (spinach pie). They're cheap (€2-3) and perfect for a beach picnic.

Getting Around: Rent an ATV or Quad Bike

Public buses exist, but they're crowded and infrequent. Taxis are expensive. The best way to explore Santorini is by ATV or quad bike. You can rent one for about €40 per day, and it gives you the freedom to explore at your own pace. We drove to remote beaches, stopped at roadside vineyards, and took detours whenever a road looked interesting. The island is small — you can drive from one end to the other in an hour — so an ATV is perfect.

Just be careful: the roads are narrow and winding, and some drivers are reckless. Wear a helmet, drive slowly, and avoid the main roads during peak hours (12-2 PM and 5-7 PM).

The Don't-Miss Experiences

1. Wine tasting at an estate. Santorini's volcanic soil produces unique wines. We visited the Domaine Sigalas winery and did a tasting of five wines for €15. The Assyrtiko was my favorite — it pairs perfectly with seafood. Many estates offer tours of the underground cellars, which are carved into the volcanic rock.

2. A sunset cruise around the caldera. Don't do the big party boats with 100 people. Instead, book a small group tour (max 12 people) on a catamaran. We paid €90 each for a 5-hour cruise that included swimming at the hot springs, a BBQ dinner, and views of the sunset from the water. It was the most magical evening of the trip.

3. Hike from Fira to Oia. It's a 10-kilometer walk along the caldera edge, and it takes about 3-4 hours. The views are stunning — you see the volcano, the islands, and the sea from above. Start at 6 AM to avoid the heat, and wear good shoes. The trail is rocky in places. We did it at sunrise, and it was a highlight.

4. Visit the ancient ruins of Akrotiri. This is a Bronze Age settlement buried by a volcanic eruption in 1600 BC. It's like Pompeii but older. The site is covered by a modern roof, so you can walk through the ruins of buildings, streets, and drainage systems. It's not crowded, and the history is incredible. Entry is €12.

What I'd Skip

The sunset wine bar in Fira. It's overpriced (€30 for a glass of wine), and you'll be surrounded by influencers taking selfies. Also, the donkey rides in Fira — the donkeys are mistreated, and it's honestly sad to see. Walk instead; it's steep but manageable.

Final Thoughts

Santorini is worth the hype, but only if you approach it right. Don't try to do it in a day or two from a cruise ship — you'll just get a surface-level experience. Stay for at least 5 days, stay in a quiet village, and explore beyond the famous spots. The island rewards those who take their time. I left feeling like I'd seen the real Santorini, not the one on Instagram. And that's the best souvenir you can bring home.
