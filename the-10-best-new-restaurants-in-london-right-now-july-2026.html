The 10 Best New Restaurants in London Right Now (July 2026)

TR
By Rachel Greene · July 20, 2026 · 5 min read

Why I Spent a Month Eating My Way Through London

I’ve always been skeptical of “best restaurant” lists. They’re usually just ads dressed up as journalism. But after moving to London last year, I got obsessed with finding the real gems—the places that aren’t just hype but actually deliver on flavor, atmosphere, and service. So I did what any reasonable person would do: I ate at 20 new restaurants in the past month. My wallet is lighter, but my soul is full. Here are the 10 that are worth your time and money right now.

1. The Korean Grill That Changed My Mind About BBQ

Let’s start with Dae Bak in Soho. I walked in expecting standard K-BBQ, but what I got was a revelation. The owner, Min-ji Park, used to run a food truck in Seoul, and she’s brought that street-level swagger to a proper sit-down spot. The pork belly is aged for 48 hours, and they serve it with a fermented gochujang sauce that’s unlike anything I’ve had outside Korea. The vibe is loud, the tables are sticky with sauce, and I loved every second. Get the spicy pork bulgogi and a bottle of soju.

2. A Hidden Italian in Hackney Wick

If you blink, you’ll miss Pasta è Vita. It’s tucked behind a graffiti-covered warehouse near the canal, and there’s no sign out front. Inside, it’s just eight tables and an open kitchen where chef Luca Rossi makes pasta by hand every morning. I had the cacio e pepe, and it was the kind of simple perfection that makes you shut your eyes and forget where you are. The secret? He uses three types of cheese—pecorino, parmesan, and a touch of gorgonzola. It’s rich, creamy, and life-affirming. Book weeks ahead.

3. The Dessert Bar That’s Actually Worth the Hype

I’ll admit I walked into Sugar Rush rolling my eyes. A dessert-only bar in Covent Garden? Sounded like a tourist trap. But then I tried the “Burned Basque Cheesecake” with black sesame ice cream, and I shut up. Pastry chef Ella Thompson trained at Noma, and it shows. She does these playful, deconstructed versions of classic desserts—think a lemon tart that’s all foam and brittle, or a chocolate mousse that tastes like a liquid brownie. It’s not cheap (around £15 a plate), but it’s an experience.

4. The Best Sunday Roast in London (Yes, I’m Sure)

Every Londoner has a strong opinion about Sunday roasts. Mine is that The George Inn in Southwark does it best. This is a historic pub—Shakespeare apparently drank here—and they’ve been doing roasts for centuries. But lately, they’ve upped their game. The beef is from a farm in Cornwall, the Yorkshire puddings are the size of your head, and they serve it with a red wine gravy that’s so good I asked for extra to take home. It’s £25 a plate, which feels steep, but you’ll be full for two days.

5. The Vegan Spot That Converts Carnivores

I’m not vegan, but Green House in Shoreditch made me forget I eat meat. The star is the “Jackfruit Birria Tacos”—jackfruit slow-cooked in a spicy broth, served with a consommé for dipping. I swear it tastes like the real thing. The chef, Amara Singh, used to work in a Mexican taqueria and brings that authenticity to every dish. Even the cocktails are plant-based, with a mezcal negroni that uses agave syrup instead of honey. It’s bright, fresh, and full of flavor.

6. The Secret Sushi Spot in a Basement

Walk down a narrow staircase off Carnaby Street, and you’ll find Omakase by Ken. It’s just a counter with 10 seats, and chef Kenji Watanabe makes each piece of nigiri right in front of you. The omakase menu is £80 for 12 courses, which sounds like a lot until you taste the otoro (fatty tuna) that literally melts on your tongue. The rice is seasoned with red vinegar, a traditional style that most London sushi spots skip. It’s intimate, quiet, and the best sushi I’ve had outside Tokyo.

7. A Pub That Does Indian Food Properly

I love a good gastropub, but The Havelock in Ealing takes it to another level. They’ve partnered with a local Indian chef, Ravi Kumar, who does a “curry of the week” using his grandmother’s recipes. I had a lamb rogan josh that was so tender it fell apart with a fork, and the naan bread was baked in a tandoor they installed in the back garden. It’s cozy, unpretentious, and surprisingly affordable—mains are around £14. This is the kind of place you become a regular at.

8. The Brunch Spot That’s Always Full (With Good Reason)

Brunch culture in London can be exhausting, but Sunny Side in Notting Hill earns the hype. Their eggs Benedict comes with a miso hollandaise that’s salty, tangy, and slightly sweet. The hash browns are crispy on the outside, fluffy inside. And the coffee? They roast their own beans and do a flat white that’s as good as any specialty cafe. The wait can be 30 minutes on weekends, but they’ll bring you a free pastry while you queue. That’s how you win people over.

9. The Most Unexpected Dumplings in Chinatown

Chinatown is full of touristy spots, but Dumpling House on Gerrard Street is the real deal. Run by a family from Shanghai, they do xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) that burst with broth when you bite into them. The secret is they use a pork and crab filling that’s incredibly savory. A basket of eight is just £9, making it one of the best value meals in London. I also love the scallion pancakes—crispy, flaky, and perfect for dipping in black vinegar.

10. The Final Spot: A Tiny Bakery That Sells Out by Noon

Last but not least: Flour Power in Bermondsey. This bakery opened in March and has already become a local legend. They do one thing—croissants—but they do it perfectly. The classic butter croissant is flaky, golden, and tastes like a hug. They also do seasonal flavors; right now it’s a strawberry and pistachio version that’s insane. The owner, Marie Dubois, trained in Paris and uses French butter imported weekly. Get there before 11am or they’ll be sold out.

Final Thoughts: Eat Your Way Through London

London’s food scene is overwhelming, but that’s also what makes it exciting. These 10 spots aren’t just trendy—they’re genuinely good, run by people who care about what they’re serving. Whether you’re a local or just visiting, I’d say start with the Korean BBQ at Dae Bak and work your way down. Your taste buds will thank you. And if you find a better Sunday roast than The George Inn, let me know—I’m always looking for the next great meal.
