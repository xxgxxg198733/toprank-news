The 10 Spiciest Foods on Earth, Ranked by Scoville Heat Units — Number One Requires a Liability Waiver

TR
TopRank Editorial TeamJune 2, 2026⏱ 6 min read125,700 views

The Scoville scale, developed by American pharmacist Wilbur Scoville in 1912, measures the concentration of capsaicin — the chemical compound responsible for the sensation of heat when consuming chili peppers. Modern measurement uses high-performance liquid chromatography rather than Scoville's original method of diluting pepper extract in sugar water until human tasters could no longer detect heat, but the unit name endures.

#10 — Vindaloo, Goa, India (up to 200,000 SHU): The Goan classic, originally derived from the Portuguese dish "carne de vinha d'alhos" (meat in wine and garlic), evolved after Portuguese colonization introduced chili peppers from the Americas to the Indian subcontinent. Traditional Goan vindaloo uses Kashmiri chilies and ghost pepper (bhut jolokia) paste, marinated overnight in vinegar and spices. At its spiciest, a single serving contains enough capsaicin to trigger a measurable endorphin response — the "runner's high" that chili enthusiasts report.

#9 — Sichuan Hot Pot, Chongqing, China (150,000-300,000 SHU): The combination of Sichuan peppercorns (which contain hydroxy-alpha-sanshool, a molecule that induces a numbing, vibrating sensation called "málà") and dried facing heaven chilies (Capsicum annuum var. conoides) creates what sensory scientists call a "bimodal nociceptive stimulus" — the brain receives pain signals from two different chemical pathways simultaneously. Regular consumers report that the experience becomes pleasurable rather than painful with repeated exposure, a phenomenon neuroscientists compare to the acquired tolerance for bitter flavors in coffee and beer.

#8 — Kimchi Jjigae, Korea (100,000-250,000 SHU): Korean kimchi stew derives its heat from gochugaru (sun-dried Korean chili flakes) and fermented kimchi that may have aged for months or years. Some Korean grandmothers maintain kimchi fermentation crocks that have been continuously active for decades. The lactobacillus fermentation process that gives kimchi its distinctive sour flavor also preserves and concentrates capsaicinoids over time.

#7 — Jerk Chicken, Jamaica (100,000-350,000 SHU): Scotch bonnet peppers (Capsicum chinense), the foundation of Jamaican jerk seasoning, have a distinctive fruity sweetness beneath their intense heat. The traditional cooking method — slow-smoking over pimento (allspice) wood — infuses the meat with aromatic compounds that partially mask the capsaicin intensity until several bites in, at which point the cumulative heat becomes apparent. First-time eaters are advised to start with a single piece and wait five minutes before deciding on a second.

#6 — Phaal Curry, Birmingham, UK (up to 1,000,000 SHU): Phaal is not a traditional Indian dish but a British-Indian restaurant invention, created in Birmingham's "Balti Triangle" in the 1990s as a challenge food. It typically incorporates up to 10 different chili varieties, including bhut jolokia (ghost pepper) and Dorset naga, ground into a paste with tomato and spices. Several UK restaurants require customers to sign a liability waiver before ordering. The Brick Lane Curry House in London keeps a fire extinguisher prominently displayed near the phaal section of the menu.

#5 — Papa a la Huancaína (Authentic Version), Peru (up to 250,000 SHU): The tourist-friendly version of this classic Peruvian dish — boiled potatoes in a creamy yellow cheese sauce — is mild and approachable. The authentic version served in the Andean highlands uses rocoto peppers (Capsicum pubescens), which have thick walls, black seeds, and a Scoville rating comparable to habanero. The creamy sauce provides only brief protection before the capsaicin penetrates.

#4 — Sambal Oelek (Traditional), Indonesia (50,000-300,000 SHU): Indonesian sambal varies enormously by region and preparation, but traditional versions using fresh bird's eye chilies (Capsicum frutescens) — small peppers approximately 1-2 centimeters in length that can reach 100,000 SHU individually — are among the most concentrated capsaicin preparations in regular culinary use. Indonesian warungs (small family restaurants) typically place a jar of sambal on every table, a practice that inexperienced foreign visitors learn to approach with extreme caution.

#3 — Carolina Reaper Pepper (Fresh), USA (1,641,000-2,200,000 SHU): Bred by Ed Currie of the PuckerButt Pepper Company in Fort Mill, South Carolina, the Carolina Reaper held the Guinness World Record for world's hottest pepper from 2013 to 2023. Eating a whole fresh Reaper has been described by those who have done it as "like swallowing liquid metal." The capsaicin concentration is sufficient to cause first-degree chemical burns on mucous membranes. Emergency room physicians in South Carolina report treating an average of three to four "Reaper injuries" annually, typically involving young men who consumed the pepper on a dare.

#2 — Pepper X, USA (2,693,000 SHU, confirmed by Guinness): Also developed by Ed Currie, Pepper X dethroned the Carolina Reaper in 2023. Currie has declined to release seeds or plants to the public, maintaining proprietary control of the cultivar. The pepper is estimated to contain approximately 40% capsaicinoids by dry weight in its placental tissue. Tasting notes from the few people who have sampled it describe "a moment of fruity sweetness, approximately 0.3 seconds long, followed by what felt like being struck in the mouth with a hammer."

#1 — Death Nut Challenge, Level 5, USA (13,000,000-16,000,000 SHU): The Death Nut Challenge, manufactured by Blazing Foods Inc. of Texas, consists of five progressively hotter levels of peanuts coated in capsaicin extract. Level 5 uses essentially pure capsaicin crystals extracted from pepper oleoresin, approaching the heat level of police-grade pepper spray. The challenge's official packaging includes safety warnings, a recommended consumption protocol (do not attempt alone, have dairy products on hand, do not touch eyes or mucous membranes for at least 24 hours), and directions to the nearest hospital emergency room. Completion of the challenge is documented with a signed certificate, a video recording, and — according to the manufacturer — "a story you will tell for the rest of your life, usually beginning with the phrase 'worst decision I ever made.'"
