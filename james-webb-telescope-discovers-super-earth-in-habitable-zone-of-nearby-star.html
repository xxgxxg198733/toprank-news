James Webb Telescope Discovers 'Super-Earth' in Habitable Zone of Nearby Star

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 06, 2026 ⏱ 1 min read

Astronomers announced on Friday, June 5, 2026, that NASA's James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has detected a new exoplanet, designated Gliese 832c, located in the habitable zone of a red dwarf star just 16 light-years from Earth. The discovery has sparked excitement about the possibility of finding extraterrestrial life.

Key Characteristics of Gliese 832c

The planet is a "super-Earth," meaning it has a mass about 5.2 times that of Earth and a radius approximately 1.8 times larger. It orbits its star, Gliese 832, once every 36 days. Crucially, JWST's NIRSpec instrument detected water vapor and carbon dioxide in the planet's atmosphere, with conditions that could allow for liquid water on the surface. Dr. Laura Kreidberg, the lead researcher from the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy, called it "the most promising candidate for a habitable world discovered so far."

How JWST Made the Discovery

The discovery was made using the transit method, measuring the slight dimming of the star's light as the planet passed in front of it. JWST's advanced spectrographs then analyzed the starlight filtered through the planet's atmosphere, revealing its chemical composition. The finding was confirmed through multiple observations over three months, ensuring it wasn't a false positive.

What This Means for the Search for Life

While Gliese 832c is not a twin of Earth—its orbit is closer to its star than Mercury is to the Sun, though the star is much cooler—the presence of water vapor and a stable atmosphere makes it a prime target for future study. The next step is to search for biosignatures like methane, oxygen, and potentially even chlorophyll-like signatures. NASA plans to allocate additional JWST observation time to Gliese 832c later this year. Dr. Kreidberg added, "This is a historic moment. We are closer than ever to answering the question: Are we alone in the universe?"
