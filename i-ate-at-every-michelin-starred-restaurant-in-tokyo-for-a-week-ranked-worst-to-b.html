I Ate at Every Michelin-Starred Restaurant in Tokyo for a Week (Ranked Worst to Best)

TR
By Lauren Davis · July 21, 2026 · 4 min read

Tokyo has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city in the world. Over 200 of them. Which is insane when you think about it. So I did something stupid: I decided to eat at as many as possible in one week. My wallet is still recovering. But I came back with a definitive ranking of the ones that are actually worth your money.

I stuck to one- and two-star places. Three-star restaurants are a different beast — harder to book, more expensive, and honestly, the difference between two and three stars is often more about the experience than the food. I wanted to focus on places where the food itself was the star.

10. Kikunoi (Kaiseki) — Beautiful but Forgettable

Kikunoi is a Kyoto-style kaiseki restaurant in Akasaka. The presentation is stunning. Each dish looks like a painting. But the flavors didn't match the visuals. Everything was subtle — too subtle. I wanted a punch of umami or a burst of acidity. Instead, I got polite, restrained flavors that faded from memory as soon as I left. At ¥30,000 per person, it's not worth it.

9. L'Osier (French) — Impressive but Stuffy

L'Osier is a three-star French restaurant in Ginza. Yes, I cheated and included one three-star. The food is technically perfect. The service is impeccable. But the atmosphere is so formal it's uncomfortable. I felt like I was being evaluated. The tasting menu is ¥45,000. The food is incredible — the langoustine dish is a masterpiece. But I'd rather eat somewhere I can laugh and relax.

8. Sushi Saito (Sushi) — The Hype Is Real, Almost

Sushi Saito is legendary. It's been called the best sushi restaurant in the world. The booking process is a nightmare — you need a Japanese phone number and a concierge. The omakase is ¥40,000. The sushi is perfect. The rice is perfectly seasoned, the fish is incredibly fresh, the technique is flawless. But is it 10 times better than a ¥4,000 sushi spot? No. It's maybe 20% better. Diminishing returns are real.

7. Narisawa (Innovative) — Creative but Overwrought

Narisawa is famous for its 'innovative' cuisine. The dishes are conceptual — one course is meant to represent a forest, another the ocean. The food is good, but the concepts sometimes overwhelm the flavors. I'd rather taste the ingredients than the idea. The tasting menu is ¥30,000. Worth it for the experience, but not for the taste alone.

6. RyuGin (Modern Japanese) — A Trip Worth Taking

RyuGin is two stars, and it deserves three. The menu is a journey through Japanese seasons and traditions. One course features a soup that arrives in a ceramic bowl that's heated to exactly the right temperature. The flavors are bold and clear. The service is warm. The tasting menu is ¥30,000. This is the one I'd recommend to anyone who wants a special food experience in Tokyo.

5. Sushi Yoshitake (Sushi) — Where Perfection Meets Personality

Yoshitake is another three-star sushi spot, but it's warmer than Saito. The chef talks to you, explains the fish, jokes around. The sushi is just as good. The omakase is ¥45,000. I left feeling like I'd made a friend, not just eaten a meal. That's rare in fine dining.

4. Sezanne (French-Japanese) — The Surprise Hit

Sezanne is a new two-star in the Four Seasons Marunouchi. The chef does French techniques with Japanese ingredients. The result is stunning. A dish of Hokkaido scallops with yuzu and shiso was the best thing I ate all week. The tasting menu is ¥25,000, which is a bargain for the quality. Book this one.

3. Tempura Kondo (Tempura) — Simple Perfection

Tempura Kondo is a one-star restaurant that deserves two. It serves only tempura. That sounds boring, but it's not. The batter is impossibly light. The oil is perfectly seasoned. The shrimp is sweet and tender. The vegetables are crisp and flavorful. The lunch set is ¥8,000. This is the best value in Tokyo. I went twice.

2. L'Effervescence (French) — The Best Meal of My Life

L'Effervescence is a two-star French restaurant in Minami-Aoyama. The menu changes with the seasons. When I went, the highlight was a dish of Hokkaido venison with a sauce made from fermented berries. It was complex, balanced, and deeply satisfying. The wine pairing is excellent. The service is warm but professional. The tasting menu is ¥35,000. This is the restaurant that made me cry. Not from sadness — from joy. The food was that good.

1. Sushi Sho (Sushi) — The Undisputed King

Sushi Sho is small, hidden, and nearly impossible to book. It has two stars, but it should have three. The chef, Mitsuo Yagi, is a genius. He ages his fish for days or weeks, concentrating flavor. His rice is the most flavorful I've ever had. Each piece of sushi is a meditation on flavor and texture. The omakase is ¥50,000, but every yen is worth it. I've never experienced anything like it. If you can get a reservation, go. It'll ruin sushi for you forever.

The Takeaway

Tokyo's Michelin scene is incredible, but not every starred restaurant is worth the hype. The ones that focus on technique and tradition — like Tempura Kondo and Sushi Sho — are the best. The ones that try too hard to be innovative often fall flat. My advice: skip the multi-course tasting menus and go for the specialists. Sushi, tempura, soba — the best food in Tokyo is the simplest.

And book early. Like, six months early. Trust me.
