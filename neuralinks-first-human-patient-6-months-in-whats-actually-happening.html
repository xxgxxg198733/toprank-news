Neuralink's First Human Patient: 6 Months In, What's Actually Happening?

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 4 min read

Back in January, when Elon Musk announced that Neuralink had successfully implanted its first brain chip in a human patient, I rolled my eyes pretty hard. I mean, we've heard this song before, right? Tesla robots, Mars colonies, brain-computer interfaces — Musk's timeline has a, let's say, flexible relationship with reality.

But then I actually started following Noland Arbaugh's story. He's the 29-year-old quadriplegic who volunteered for the PRIME study. And honestly? Six months in, I'm genuinely impressed — but also a little worried.

The Man Behind the Headlines

Noland isn't some faceless test subject. He's been remarkably open on his X account and in interviews. Before his diving accident in 2016, he was an outdoor enthusiast, a camper, a guy who loved hiking in the Pacific Northwest. Now he can't move from the neck down.

The Neuralink device — officially called the N1 Implant — is a coin-sized chip surgically placed in the motor cortex. It uses 1,024 ultra-thin threads to read neural signals. The goal? Let Arbaugh control a computer cursor with his thoughts alone.

And here's the thing: it works. In March, we saw that incredible video of him playing chess on a laptop, moving the cursor just by thinking about moving his hand. He beat his friends at Mario Kart. He spent hours browsing the web independently for the first time in eight years.

The Reality Behind the Hype

But let's not pretend this is all smooth sailing. In May, Neuralink admitted something that didn't get nearly enough coverage: about 85% of the implant's threads retracted from Arbaugh's brain within the first few weeks. That's a huge amount.

The company claims they compensated by tweaking the software algorithms, improving sensitivity to the remaining signals. And apparently, it worked — Arbaugh's performance actually improved after the setback. But nobody's talking about what those retracting threads mean for long-term safety. Scar tissue formation? Inflammation? We just don't know yet.

I spoke with Dr. Emily Chen, a neuroethicist at Stanford, who told me, 'The company's transparency has been better than expected, but there's still a lot we don't know about chronic implantation. This is a marathon, not a sprint.'

And she's right. Arbaugh himself has said the device isn't perfect. The cursor jitters sometimes. He has to recalibrate it. He gets headaches after long sessions. But he also says, point blank, that it's changed his life.

What This Means for the Rest of Us

I get asked a lot: 'When can I get a Neuralink?' And I think that's the wrong question. This technology is, for now, strictly for people with severe paralysis. The FDA approved the human trial for exactly that purpose. The idea of 'brain-computer interfaces for everyone' — that's Musk's long game, but we're years, probably decades away.

What's more interesting to me is the ripple effect. Other companies are accelerating their own BCI research. Synchron, a Neuralink competitor, already has a less invasive device in multiple patients. Blackrock Neurotech has been at this for years. The entire field is moving faster because of the attention Neuralink is generating.

And attention brings funding. That's good for science, even if it makes me uncomfortable that one billionaire controls the narrative.

The Ethical Minefield Nobody's Walking Through

Here's what keeps me up at night: what happens to Arbaugh's data? Neuralink collects massive amounts of neural activity. Who owns that information? Can they sell it? Can the government subpoena it? We don't have laws for this yet.

And what about the animals? Neuralink's record with lab monkeys is, frankly, disturbing. Internal documents and whistleblowers have described rushed procedures, inadequate veterinary care, and monkeys dying from complications. If you're queasy about animal testing, this is not a company with clean hands.

I'm not saying the research shouldn't happen. I am saying we need to talk about the costs — real and potential — alongside the benefits.

Where We're Headed Next

Neuralink just announced they're recruiting for a second patient. This time, they want someone with ALS. The goal is to refine the surgical technique and hopefully reduce thread retraction. They're also developing a next-gen device with more electrodes and better signal processing.

Meanwhile, Arbaugh keeps living his life. He's become something of an internet celebrity, doing Twitch streams and answering questions from curious strangers. He seems to genuinely enjoy being the face of this experiment.

I don't know if Neuralink will fulfill Musk's grand vision. But I do know that right now, one man can play chess with his mind. And that's either terrifying or wonderful — or maybe, like most real things, it's both.
