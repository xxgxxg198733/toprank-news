Why I'm Actually Excited About the 'Snow White' Remake (Yes, Really)

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 23, 2026 · 4 min read

I'll be honest: when Disney announced another live-action remake, I rolled my eyes. 'The Little Mermaid' was fine, 'Pinocchio' was a disaster, and 'Mufasa' felt like a cash grab. So when they said they were doing 'Snow White'—again—I groaned. But then I saw a 20-minute preview at CinemaCon in April, and I completely changed my mind. This isn't your grandma's Snow White.

The movie, directed by Marc Webb (who did 'The Amazing Spider-Man'), stars Rachel Zegler as Snow White and Gal Gadot as the Evil Queen. It's been in development since 2021 and was delayed by strikes and controversy. But the final product? It might be the best live-action Disney remake yet. Here's why I'm genuinely excited.

A Fresh Take on a Tired Story

The original 'Snow White' from 1937 is a classic, but let's be real: it's dated. The prince kisses a corpse, the queen is evil just because, and Snow White's only goal is to find a man. The new movie changes all that. Snow White is no longer a damsel in distress—she's a leader. She's not waiting for a prince; she's trying to save her kingdom from her stepmother's tyranny.

I watched the preview where Snow White rallies the seven dwarfs (who are now CGI but look surprisingly natural) to fight back against the queen. Rachel Zegler has this energy that's infectious—she's not just singing pretty songs; she's shouting about justice. It reminded me of the 'Hunger Games' more than the original Disney film.

The Music: Original Songs That Actually Work

Benj Pasek and Justin Paul, the duo behind 'La La Land' and 'The Greatest Showman,' wrote new songs for the film. I heard two of them in the preview. One is a ballad called 'Waiting for a Sign,' sung by Zegler as she looks out over the kingdom. It's soaring and emotional, with lyrics about hope in dark times. The other is a villain song for Gal Gadot called 'Mirror, Mirror,' which is a creepy, jazzy number that reminds me of 'Poor Unfortunate Souls' from 'The Little Mermaid.'

The classic songs are still there—'Whistle While You Work' gets a fun update with a hip-hop beat, and 'Some Day My Prince Will Come' is reworked as a duet between Snow White and the prince (played by Andrew Burnap). It's not just nostalgia; it's a reinvention.

The Controversy: What Everyone's Been Arguing About

You've probably heard the drama. Casting Rachel Zegler, who is of Colombian descent, as Snow White sparked debate about representation. Then the dwarfs were recast with CGI after Peter Dinklage criticized the original's stereotypes. Disney also changed the prince's role to be less creepy. Some fans are outraged, others are thrilled.

I'm in the 'thrilled' camp. The original dwarfs are problematic—they're portrayed as cartoonish and childlike. The new dwarfs are fully realized characters with distinct personalities. Each one has a name and a backstory. Grumpy is still grumpy, but now he's also a war veteran. Happy is a baker with a tragic past. It's deeper than you'd expect from a Disney movie.

The Visuals: A Feast for the Eyes

The cinematography is by Mandy Walker, who shot 'Elvis.' The colors are vibrant—the forest scenes are lush and green, the castle is dark and gothic, and the queen's lair is filled with mirrors and shadows. The CGI is seamless; the dwarfs look like real actors with prosthetics, not video game characters. And the Evil Queen's transformation scene is genuinely terrifying. Gal Gadot chews the scenery, and I mean that as a compliment.

I saw the preview in IMAX, and it was stunning. The scene where Snow White runs through the forest at night, with fireflies lighting the way, was one of the most beautiful things I've ever seen on screen.

Why This One Might Work When Others Failed

Most Disney remakes fail because they're too faithful or too different. 'The Lion King' was a shot-for-shot copy that felt empty. 'Mulan' was a soulless action movie. 'Snow White' strikes a balance. It honors the original's spirit—the kindness, the hope, the magic—while updating the story for modern audiences.

Director Marc Webb said in an interview that he wanted to make a movie about 'the power of community.' In the original, Snow White is saved by a prince. In this one, she's saved by her friends—the dwarfs, the animals, and even the prince, but only because she inspires him to be brave. It's a subtle shift, but it changes everything.

The Bottom Line

I know people are tired of remakes. I am too. But 'Snow White' feels different. It's not a cash grab—it's a genuine reimagining by people who care about the material. Rachel Zegler is a star, Gal Gadot is having the time of her life, and the music is stuck in my head.

The movie comes out on August 14, 2026. I'll be there opening night. And I'm not ashamed to admit it.
