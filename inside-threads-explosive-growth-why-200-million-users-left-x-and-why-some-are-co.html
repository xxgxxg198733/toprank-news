Inside Threads' Explosive Growth: Why 200 Million Users Left X (And Why Some Are Coming Back)

TR
By Emily Watson · July 21, 2026 · 7 min read

Remember when Threads launched in July 2023 and everyone called it a 'Twitter killer'? Then it fizzled. Users dropped off. People went back to X. Fast forward to July 2026, and Threads just announced 200 million monthly active users — a number that seemed impossible two years ago. I've been using both platforms daily for the last month, and I think I understand what changed.

It's not one thing. It's a combination of Meta finally figuring out the product, X making itself increasingly hostile to regular users, and a cultural shift in how people want to use social media. Let me break it down.

The Elon Musk Effect: X's Self-Inflicted Wounds

I don't want to make this entire article about Elon Musk, but you can't talk about Threads' growth without acknowledging what's happened to X since his 2022 acquisition. The platform has become increasingly chaotic. In the past three months alone: X changed its algorithm to prioritize paid subscribers, introduced a 'Verified' checkmark that means nothing, and started charging new users in New Zealand and the Philippines a $1 annual fee to post. That last one is still being tested, but the message is clear — X wants to monetize every interaction.

Then there's the content moderation. Or lack thereof. X has become a haven for misinformation, hate speech, and bots. A study from the Center for Countering Digital Hate last month found that hate speech on X has increased 60% since Musk's takeover. For advertisers, that's a nightmare. For users, it's exhausting. I personally saw a rise in crypto scams in my mentions that I never experienced before.

Threads, by contrast, has strict moderation policies. Too strict, some critics say — but for the average person, it means a cleaner, less toxic experience. You can block accounts. You can limit replies. You can mute words. It's not perfect, but it's significantly better than X's Wild West approach.

Instagram Integration: The Secret Weapon Nobody Talks About

Here's the thing nobody in tech journalism admits. Threads' growth is directly tied to Instagram. When you sign up for Threads, you import your Instagram followers. That means you immediately have a network of people you already know. X requires you to build a following from scratch, which is hard and slow.

Meta also started cross-posting Instagram Reels to Threads in 2025. That was a genius move. Suddenly, Threads had a flood of short-form video content that X couldn't match. And because Instagram creators already have audiences, their Threads accounts grew overnight. I followed maybe 50 people on Threads in the first week. On X, after three months, I had maybe 200 followers. The network effect is real.

But there's a downside. Threads feels like an extension of Instagram, not a separate platform. The culture is different. On X, there's a certain chaos that breeds creativity — you stumble into niche communities, bizarre memes, and unfiltered opinions. Threads is sanitized. It's like Instagram's Explore page in text form. Some people love that. Others find it boring.

The Algorithm: Threads Shows You Everything, X Shows You What's Popular

I did an experiment. For one week, I used Threads exclusively. For the next, X exclusively. The difference in my feed was stark. Threads showed me posts from people I followed in chronological order, mixed with a few suggested posts. It felt like I was actually seeing what my friends were saying. X showed me likes from people I didn't follow, viral tweets from strangers, and promoted posts. It felt like an endless firehose of content I didn't ask for.

Which is better? Depends on what you want. If you want to stay connected with real people you know, Threads wins. If you want to discover viral content and follow breaking news in real time, X still has the edge. But X's algorithm has become so aggressive in promoting paid subscribers that regular users feel invisible. A friend of mine who has 50,000 followers on X told me his engagement dropped 40% after X changed the algorithm in March. He's now spending more time on Threads.

Meta also introduced 'Feeds' — you can choose between 'For You' (algorithmic) and 'Following' (chronological). That flexibility is something X removed years ago. X's 'Following' tab still exists, but it's buried in the UI and doesn't update in real time. Threads makes it easy to switch.

The Features Threads Is Still Missing

Okay, let's be honest. Threads isn't perfect. There are things X does that Threads still can't match. Search is terrible on Threads. You can't search for specific posts from a certain date. You can't search within a user's profile. It's basic, and frustrating.

Trending topics? Nonexistent. On X, you can see what's trending globally or by location. Threads has no equivalent. If something big happens — a natural disaster, a political event — you have to rely on your feed or search manually. That's a dealbreaker for news junkies.

DMs. Threads has no direct messaging. You can reply to posts, but you can't send a private message. Meta says it's a 'public conversation app,' but come on. Every social platform needs DMs. It's 2026. This is basic.

Desktop support. Threads has a web version, but it's clunky. You can't schedule posts from the desktop. You can't see analytics. It feels like an afterthought. Meanwhile, X's desktop experience is robust with TweetDeck (now X Pro) offering real-time monitoring.

The 200 Million User Number: Is It Real?

Meta announced 200 million monthly active users on July 15. But let's be skeptical. Monthly active users is a vanity metric. How many of those users are posting? How many are just scrolling? Meta doesn't break that out. X claims 550 million monthly users, but third-party estimates suggest the real number is closer to 350 million. Both platforms inflate.

But Threads' daily active users are growing. According to Apptopia, daily active users on Threads increased 30% from January to June 2026. X's daily active users declined 15% in the same period. Those are the numbers that matter. People aren't just signing up for Threads — they're actually using it.

I've noticed it in my own behavior. I used to check X ten times a day. Now I check Threads five times and X two times. The shift is gradual, but real.

Who's Actually Leaving X?

I talked to a dozen people about their social media habits. Here's what I found. Young people (under 25) are abandoning X in droves. They see it as a boomer platform full of political arguments and ads. Threads, with its Instagram ties, feels more like their world. Older users (40+) are staying on X because they're invested in their networks and find Threads confusing.

Journalists are split. Some love Threads' less toxic environment. Others hate its lack of search and trending topics. I spoke to a tech reporter who said she posts breaking news on X because that's where the audience still is, but she posts personal updates on Threads. That dual-platform strategy seems common.

Brands are cautiously moving to Threads. Nike, McDonald's, and Spotify all have active Threads accounts. But they're not abandoning X — they're hedging their bets. Advertisers are waiting to see if Threads can sustain its growth. Meta is reportedly testing ads on Threads in select markets. If that goes well, expect a full rollout by the end of 2026.

The Verdict: Is Threads Ready to Replace X?

No. Not yet. Threads is great for casual conversation, following friends, and avoiding the chaos of X. But it's not a replacement for X's role as a real-time news platform, its robust search, or its ability to surface niche communities. Threads feels like a cleaner, friendlier version of social media — but that also makes it less interesting.

I think the future is both. People will use Threads for personal updates and X for news and discovery. The idea that one platform will kill the other is outdated. Social media is becoming fragmented. We'll have multiple apps for different moods and needs.

Threads' 200 million users is impressive, but it's still less than half of X's user base. The real test will come when Meta starts aggressively monetizing Threads. Will users stick around when ads appear? Will creators stay when the algorithm changes? We've seen this story before with Instagram and Facebook. Meta's playbook is to grow fast, then squeeze. Threads users should enjoy the honeymoon while it lasts.
