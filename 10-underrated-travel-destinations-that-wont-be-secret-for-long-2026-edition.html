10 Underrated Travel Destinations That Won't Be Secret for Long (2026 Edition)

TR
By Lauren Davis · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

Let's be honest: the whole 'hidden gem' thing has gotten a little tired. Every travel blogger calls everywhere a hidden gem. But I've actually been to most of these places, and I can tell you — they're the real deal. I'm talking about spots that still feel like you've stumbled onto something special, not like a curated Instagram set.

This list isn't about showing off. It's about sharing places that genuinely surprised me. Places where I didn't feel like a tourist, where the locals were curious, not annoyed, and where the food made me actually angry that I couldn't stay longer. So here's my honest take on 10 underrated destinations that I think will be huge in the next few years — for better or worse.

1. Kotor, Montenegro — The Fjord That Disney Couldn't Design

I know, I know — everyone says Kotor is the next Dubrovnik. But I went last September, and it's still way less crowded. The bay is absurdly beautiful, like a Norwegian fjord but with Mediterranean warmth. The old town is tiny but perfect — cobblestone streets, cats everywhere (seriously, they have a cat museum), and bakeries selling burek that'll ruin all other pastries for you. The hike up to the fortress is brutal — 1,350 steps in the sun — but the view from the top made me tear up. Not joking. I sat there for an hour just staring.

2. San Pedro de Atacama, Chile — The Mars-Like Desert That's Actually Affordable

If you're into landscapes that don't look like Earth, this is it. The Valle de la Luna at sunset is pure magic — the colors shift from orange to purple to deep blue. But here's what nobody tells you: the altitude is brutal (2,400 meters), so you'll be winded just walking. Bring coca tea. Also, the stargazing is world-class — the sky is so clear you can see the Milky Way with your naked eyes. I booked a tour with a local astronomer named Carlos who pointed out constellations I'd never heard of. Cost me $30. In Chile, your dollar goes far.

3. Gjirokastër, Albania — The Stone City That Time Forgot

Albania is having a moment, but most tourists head to the coast. Gjirokastër is inland, built on a mountainside, with these incredible stone-roofed houses that look like they're from a fairy tale. The castle is massive and mostly empty — I had whole sections to myself. The food is phenomenal: qofte (meatballs) with yogurt sauce, and a lamb stew that I still dream about. Best part? A nice hotel room costs $40 a night. I stayed at a family-run guesthouse where the grandmother insisted on feeding me breakfast, lunch, and dinner. She didn't speak English, but we communicated through smiles and pointing.

4. Lofoten Islands, Norway — But Go in Spring or Fall

Okay, Lofoten isn't exactly unknown. But most people go in summer when it's packed. I went in late May, and it was perfect — the midnight sun was starting, the weather was mild, and I had hiking trails almost to myself. The fishing villages are postcard-perfect: red cabins on stilts over turquoise water. It's expensive — Norway is Norway — but camping is legal almost everywhere. I pitched a tent near a beach and woke up to sea eagles circling overhead. That's not something you forget.

5. Luang Prabang, Laos — The Most Chill City in Southeast Asia

Forget Bangkok or Hanoi. Luang Prabang is where you go to actually relax. The town is a UNESCO World Heritage site, with French colonial architecture and Buddhist temples everywhere. Every morning at dawn, monks walk through the streets collecting alms — it's a beautiful ritual, but please be respectful (no flashing cameras). The night market sells handmade textiles that put the factory-made junk in other cities to shame. I bought a scarf for $5 that I still wear. Also, the Kuang Si waterfalls are the most beautiful I've ever seen — turquoise pools you can swim in.

6. Da Nang, Vietnam — Better Than Ho Chi Minh or Hanoi

I said it. Da Nang is the best Vietnamese city for travelers. It has beaches, mountains, incredible food (try the mi Quang noodles), and it's not overwhelming like the bigger cities. The Marble Mountains are a quick trip — you can climb to caves with Buddhist shrines inside. And the Hai Van Pass is a legendary motorcycle road. I rented a Honda for $10 a day and spent a week just driving around. The Golden Bridge (the one with giant stone hands) is touristy but genuinely impressive in person.

7. Colmar, France — The Alsatian Town That's Better Than Strasbourg

Strasbourg gets all the love, but Colmar is actually prettier. It's like a fairy tale — half-timbered houses, canals with flower boxes, cobblestone streets that smell like fresh bread and wine. The Christmas market is incredible, but even in summer it's magical. The Alsace wine route is right there, so you can spend afternoons tasting Riesling and Gewürztraminer. I did a bike tour through the vineyards with a guide named Marie who told me stories about the World Wars. It was one of those days that makes you glad you exist.

8. Salento, Colombia — Coffee Country That's Not Just for Coffee Snobs

I'm not a coffee person — I drink it but don't know beans (pun intended) about flavor notes. But Salento made me care. The Cocora Valley has these absurdly tall wax palms, the national tree of Colombia, which look like something from Dr. Seuss. I went on a coffee farm tour and actually learned something: how the beans are picked, dried, roasted. The farmer, Don José, was 72 years old and had been doing it for 50 years. He let me taste the coffee straight from the plant. It was bitter and sweet and earthy — nothing like what we drink at Starbucks. Also, the town itself has colorful buildings and great street food (arepas with cheese and hogao sauce).

9. Mostar, Bosnia — The Bridge That Survived a War

Mostar's Old Bridge was destroyed in the 1990s war, then rebuilt. Standing on it, watching divers jump 24 meters into the Neretva River, you feel something heavy — history, resilience, hope. The old town is small but vibrant, with Ottoman architecture and minarets. The food is incredible: cevapi (grilled meat in flatbread) and baklava that's better than anything in Turkey. I stayed with a family that had fled during the war and returned in 2000. The grandmother showed me photos of the bridge before it fell. I cried a little. Not ashamed.

10. Sihanoukville, Cambodia — But Only the Islands

Okay, Sihanoukville itself has been ruined by casinos and Chinese investment. It's ugly now. But the islands off the coast — Koh Rong and Koh Rong Sanloem — are still paradise. Soft white sand, bioluminescent plankton at night, wooden bungalows with no electricity after 10pm. I spent three days on Koh Rong Sanloem, reading books, swimming, eating grilled fish. No Wi-Fi. No stress. Just the sound of waves and the occasional monkey stealing your banana. It's the kind of place that resets your brain.

So there you have it — 10 places that deserve way more attention. Go before they get ruined by influencers and cruise ships. And if you visit any of them, please be a good traveler: spend local, don't litter, and learn a few words of the language. It matters.
