How I Finally Cooked Perfect Pasta After 20 Years of Trying

TR
By Rachel Greene · July 24, 2026 · 4 min read

I thought I knew how to cook pasta. I've been doing it for over 20 years – boil water, add salt, drop in the pasta, wait 10 minutes, drain. Simple, right? Wrong. It turns out I was making at least four major mistakes, and I didn't realize until a TikTok video from a Italian chef named Giovanni went viral last month. I tried his method, and honestly, the difference was shocking. Here's everything I learned about making pasta that actually tastes restaurant-quality.

Mistake #1: Not Enough Water

Giovanni's first tip was obvious once I heard it: for every 100 grams of pasta, you need at least 1 liter of water. I was using maybe half that. Less water means the starch concentration gets too high, making the pasta gummy instead of al dente. I tested it side by side – the batch with more water was noticeably firmer and less sticky. The starch also helps the sauce cling better. So now I use a big pot and plenty of water, no exceptions.

Mistake #2: Salting Wrong

This was a game-changer. I used to add a pinch of salt to the water, maybe a teaspoon. Giovanni says you need 10 grams of salt per liter of water – that's about 2 teaspoons. The water should taste like the sea. I was skeptical, but I tried it. The pasta itself now has flavor, not just the sauce. And here's the key: don't salt the water after it boils. Add the salt when the water is still cold, so it dissolves evenly. That small change makes a difference.

Mistake #3: Not Saving the Pasta Water

I used to dump the pasta water down the drain like a barbarian. Now I know better. The starchy water is liquid gold for thickening and binding sauces. I reserve a cup before draining. When I toss the pasta with sauce, I add a splash of the water and stir vigorously. The starch emulsifies the sauce, making it creamy without adding cream. It works for tomato sauces, pesto, even simple butter and cheese. I've been doing this for a month and I'll never go back.

Mistake #4: Rinsing the Pasta

This one hurts to admit, but I used to rinse pasta after draining. I thought it stopped the cooking process. But it also washes away the starch that helps sauce stick. Now I just drain and immediately toss with sauce. If I'm making a cold pasta salad, I'll rinse it, but for hot dishes, never. The texture is better, and the sauce clings perfectly.

The $12 Tool That Changed Everything

I bought a pasta fork – that's the big wooden spoon with prongs. It cost $12 on Amazon. I thought it was a gimmick, but it's actually useful. The prongs make it easy to stir the pasta while it cooks, preventing clumps. And it's great for serving. I also picked up a pasta pot with a built-in strainer lid – another $20 – and that's been a timesaver. You don't need expensive equipment, but these small tools genuinely help.

The Technique That Makes the Most Difference

Giovanni's most controversial tip: cook the pasta 2 minutes less than the package says, then finish cooking it in the sauce. I tried this with a simple puttanesca – olive oil, garlic, anchovies, olives, capers, tomatoes. I transferred the undercooked pasta into the sauce pan with a ladle of pasta water, cranked the heat, and stirred until the pasta absorbed the sauce and cooked to al dente. The result was silky, flavorful, and perfectly textured. My wife said it was the best pasta I've ever made, and she's not one for compliments.

The Best Pasta Shapes for Different Sauces

This is another thing I learned the hard way. Not all pasta shapes work with all sauces. Thick, hearty sauces like bolognese need wide shapes like pappardelle or tagliatelle to hold the meat. Delicate sauces like aglio e olio (garlic and oil) work best with thin spaghetti or linguine. Creamy sauces cling to ridges like those on penne rigate or fusilli. And for chunky vegetable sauces, short shapes like orecchiette or farfalle are ideal. I used to just grab whatever box was cheapest, now I think about the pairing.

The Simple Sauce That Changed My Mind

Last week I made cacio e pepe – just pasta, pecorino cheese, and black pepper. It sounds too simple to be good, but when done right, it's transcendent. The key is to mix the grated cheese with the pasta water off the heat, stirring constantly, so it forms a creamy sauce without clumping. It took me three tries to get it right, but when I did, it was like magic. Three ingredients, 15 minutes, and a dish that tastes like it came from a Roman trattoria.

What I Still Get Wrong

I'm not perfect. I still occasionally overcook the pasta when I'm distracted. I sometimes forget to reserve the water. And I haven't mastered fresh pasta yet – that's my next project. But these changes have genuinely improved my cooking. If you're like me and thought you knew how to make pasta, try these tips. You might be surprised at how much better it can be.

The best part? Pasta is cheap. A pound of good dried pasta costs $2, a can of tomatoes is $1, garlic is pennies. With a few technique tweaks, you can eat like a chef without spending like one. That's the kind of win I can get behind.
