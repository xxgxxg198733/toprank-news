10 Best Pizza Places in NYC Right Now (July 2026 Edition)

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Why I Spent Two Weeks Eating 37 Pizzas

Look, I know what you're thinking: another pizza list? But hear me out. New York City's pizza scene has been absolutely wild the last few months. The past week alone saw the reopening of a legendary spot that had been closed since 2023, and a new Neapolitan place in Astoria that's getting James Beard whispers. I'm a born-and-raised New Yorker who's eaten pizza on every block from the Bronx to Staten Island. But for this list, I went all in: 14 days, 37 slices, and a lot of heartburn. Here's what I found.

1. L'Industrie Pizzeria (Williamsburg) – Still the King

I know, I know. Everyone talks about L'Industrie. But there's a reason. I went last Tuesday and the line was still 45 minutes. That's not a flex – that's a problem. But the second that burnt-edged pepperoni slice hits your tongue, you'll forget the wait. The crust is this impossibly airy, chewy masterpiece with a crisp bottom that's almost caramelized. The trick? They use a mix of double-zero flour and a secret starter that's been alive since 2019. Order the "Burrata Experience" – it's $18 but it's a full meal. I'd skip the hype and go at 11am on a weekday. You're welcome.

2. Una Pizza Napoletana (Lower East Side) – The Perfectionist Strikes Again

Anthony Mangieri is a madman in the best way. He's been making pizza for 30 years, and his spot on Orchard Street is a temple. The Marinara here – just tomatoes, garlic, oregano, olive oil – is a religious experience. I saw a guy cry last week. Not kidding. The wait is brutal (two hours on a Saturday), but the pizza comes out in 90 seconds. Each pie is a work of art. The crust is puffy, leopard-spotted, and tastes like it was kissed by an Italian wood-fire. If you're looking for a quick slice, run the other way. But if you want to understand what pizza can be, this is it.

3. Lucali (Carroll Gardens) – The Legend Endures

I almost didn't put Lucali on this list because the hype is so massive. But I went last Friday, and the pizza was better than I remembered. The space is tiny – no sign, just a doorbell. You call at 4pm to get on the list. The pizza is thin, crispy, and incredibly simple. A margherita with fresh basil that's so fragrant it hits you before the slice gets to the table. The crust has a sourdough tang that's addictive. Is it worth the three-hour wait? Honestly, maybe not. But if you go with a group, share a bottle of wine, and soak in the vibe, it's a perfect night.

4. Scarr's Pizza (Lower East Side) – The Revival

Scarr's closed in 2023 and I was devastated. It reopened two weeks ago and I ran there. The new spot is bigger, but the soul is intact. The pepperoni square is a masterpiece – thick, doughy, with a crispy bottom and a layer of cheese that's almost custard-like. They grind their own flour in-house, which is insane but it works. The place was packed on reopening day, but the staff is still the same friendly crew. I had a slice and a grape soda and felt like I was 12 again. This is the kind of pizza that makes you want to hug a stranger.

5. Paulie Gee's (Greenpoint) – The Slice Shop Dream

Paulie Gee's is a Greenpoint institution, and their slice shop on Franklin Street is underrated. The Hellboy slice – mozzarella, spicy honey, and soppressata – is a flavor bomb. The crust is thin but sturdy, and the honey gives it a sweet heat that's ridiculous. I went last Sunday and the owner was behind the counter, which is always a good sign. The wait is manageable (15 minutes max), and you can grab a beer from the cooler. It's not fancy, it's just excellent pizza. The kind you crave at 2am.

6. Ribalta (Multiple Locations) – The Unsung Hero

Ribalta is low-key one of the best Neapolitan places in the city. Their location in Midtown East is a hidden gem. The Margherita D.O.C. is picture-perfect: a thin, bubbly crust, San Marzano tomatoes, fresh mozzarella, and basil that tastes like it was picked that morning. The trick is the dough – it's fermented for 72 hours, which gives it a complex flavor that most places don't achieve. I went on a Monday at 6pm and got a table instantly. It's almost too quiet for how good the pizza is. Go before the secret gets out.

7. Best Pizza (Williamsburg) – Yes, That's the Name

Frank Pinello's spot is called Best Pizza, and while it's not literally the best, it's close. The classic slice is perfect: a foldable, greasy, cheesy slice that tastes like 1980s New York. The sesame seed crust is a nice touch. I went last Wednesday and the line moved fast. The vibe is no-nonsense – you order, you eat, you leave. But the quality is consistent. I've never had a bad slice here. It's the pizza you want when you don't want to think about it.

8. Mama's Too! (Upper West Side) – The Squared Up

Mama's Too! is a Sicilian specialist that's been killing it. The upside-down pepperoni square (cheese on the bottom, sauce on top, pepperoni on top of that) is a work of art. The crust is thick but airy, almost like focaccia. I went last Saturday and the place was a zoo. But the staff handled it like pros. The grandma slice with ricotta is also incredible. If you're on the Upper West Side, this is a must.

9. John's of Bleecker Street (Greenwich Village) – The Old Guard

John's is a no-slice, whole-pie-only spot that's been around since 1929. The coal-fired oven gives the crust a charred, smoky flavor that's unmatched. I went last Thursday and the place was packed with tourists and locals alike. The pepperoni pie is the move: crispy, slightly greasy, and utterly satisfying. The wait is long (45 minutes if you're lucky), but the atmosphere is classic New York – red checkered tablecloths, vintage photos, and the smell of burning coal. It's a time capsule, and the pizza is still great.

10. Di Fara Pizza (Midwood) – The Legend (Still Cooking)

Dom DeMarco passed away in 2022, but his family has kept Di Fara going. I went last Monday, and the pizza is still excellent. The classic pie is a beautiful mess: a thin, crackly crust, a generous layer of mozzarella, and fresh basil that's thrown on after the bake. The line is still long (an hour on a bad day), and the price is steep ($35 for a pie). But there's something special about eating pizza in a place where legend was made. The new generation is doing it justice.

So there you have it – 10 slices that define New York pizza in 2026. I'm still recovering from the cheese intake, but it was worth every single bite.
