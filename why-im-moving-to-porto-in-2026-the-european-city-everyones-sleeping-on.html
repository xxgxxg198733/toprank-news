Why I'm Moving to Porto in 2026: The European City Everyone's Sleeping On

TR
By Nicole Barnes · July 17, 2026 · 5 min read

The City That Won Me Over

I've been a digital nomad for five years. I've lived in Berlin, Mexico City, Chiang Mai, and a dozen other places. But after three months in Porto, I'm ready to settle down. I know, I know – everyone's talking about Lisbon. It's trendy, it's full of influencers, and it's getting expensive. But Porto? It's the underdog. And I think it's the best city in Europe for living well on a budget. Let me tell you why I'm moving here permanently in September.

First, let's talk about what Porto is not. It's not a party city like Berlin. It's not a tech hub like Tallinn. It's not a fashion capital like Milan. But it is a city with soul. The old town is a UNESCO World Heritage site, with narrow cobblestone streets, tile-covered buildings, and views of the Douro River that will stop you in your tracks. And it's affordable – at least for now. I found a one-bedroom apartment in the Cedofeita neighborhood for €750 a month. In Lisbon, that same apartment would be €1,200. In Paris? Forget about it.

The Cost of Living

Let's get down to numbers. As of July 2026, Porto is about 30% cheaper than Lisbon. My monthly expenses average around €1,500, including rent, utilities, food, and the occasional night out. A meal at a good restaurant costs €15-20. A glass of vinho verde at a tasca is €2. The metro pass is €40 a month. I can get a haircut for €15. This is not a city where you need to be rich to live well. Of course, prices are rising. The digital nomad influx is real. But compared to most Western European cities, Porto is still a steal. I've heard locals complain about gentrification, and they're right to be worried. But for now, it's still possible to live comfortably without breaking the bank.

The Food Scene

Porto's food is simple and addictive. The most famous dish is the francesinha – a monstrous sandwich with layers of ham, sausage, steak, and melted cheese, all drenched in a tomato-beer sauce. It's not healthy. It's not refined. But it is the greatest hangover cure known to man. Café Santiago on Rua de Passos Manuel makes the best one, in my opinion. Then there's the seafood. The grilled sardines at Mercado do Bolhão are incredible. The octopus at Tascö is tender and smoky. And you can't leave without trying pastéis de nata – the ones from Manteigaria are my favorite. The food cost me about €400 a month, and I ate out almost every day. If you cook, it's even cheaper. The markets are full of fresh produce, cheese, and bread for pennies.

The Weather

Porto has a Mediterranean climate with a twist. Summers are warm and dry, with temperatures around 25-30°C. Winters are mild but rainy – very rainy. I'm talking drizzly, grey days from November to February. It's not for everyone. If you need constant sunshine, go to the Algarve. But I kind of like the rain. It makes the city feel cozy. The cafes are full of people reading books. The streets are empty. And when the sun does come out, you appreciate it more. The spring and fall are perfect – 20°C, sunny, and the city is alive. You can sit at a riverside café for hours. It's the kind of weather that makes you want to write a novel.

The Lifestyle

Life in Porto moves slowly. People take long lunches. Shops close for siesta. Dinner starts at 9 PM. It's frustrating at first if you're used to efficiency, but you adapt. I've learned to embrace the pace. I start my workday at 8 AM, take a break for a two-hour lunch, then work until 6 PM. Then I go for a walk along the Douro, maybe have a glass of port wine at a bar in Vila Nova de Gaia, and watch the sunset. The city is walkable – I barely use the metro. The hills are killer (Porto is built on steep slopes), but you get used to them. The people are friendly but not pushy. Portuguese is hard to learn, but locals appreciate the effort. I've made friends at my local café, at the gym, and at a weekly language exchange at a bar called O Porto.

The Downsides

I'm not going to romanticize everything. Porto has problems. The bureaucracy is a nightmare. Getting a NIF (tax number) took me three weeks. The internet is fast but not always reliable. The airport is small, and direct flights are limited – you'll probably have to connect through Lisbon. Healthcare is good and cheap (I paid €40 for a doctor's visit), but the system is slow. And the tourism is getting out of hand. In the summer, the center is packed with cruise ship passengers. The trams are a tourist trap. I avoid the Ribeira district on weekends. But you learn to find your own spots. I live in Cedofeita, which has great cafes and fewer tourists. It's a trade-off.

Why I'm Committing

I've been nomadic for too long. I'm tired of packing my life into a suitcase. Porto feels like a place where I can actually plant roots. The cost of living allows me to save money while still enjoying life. The community is welcoming. The pace is right for me. I'm learning Portuguese, I've joined a local running club, and I'm even thinking about buying a small apartment. Is it perfect? No. But it's the closest I've found to a home. If you're thinking about making a move to Europe, consider Porto before it becomes the next Lisbon. The prices are rising, the secret is getting out. Get here while you can.

I'll be at Café Progresso on Rua de Santa Catarina most mornings. Come say hello. I'll buy you a pastel de nata.
