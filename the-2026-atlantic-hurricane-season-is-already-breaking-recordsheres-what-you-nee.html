The 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season Is Already Breaking Records—Here's What You Need to Know

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

I live in South Florida, so hurricane season is a fact of life. Every year from June 1 to November 30, I stock up on canned beans, buy extra bottled water, and obsessively check the National Hurricane Center website. But this year feels different. It's only July 16, 2026, and we've already had seven named storms, three of which became major hurricanes. The average for this point in the season is three named storms and one hurricane. Something is off.

To make sense of what's happening, I've been following the forecasts closely and talking to people who know way more than I do. Dr. Emily Becker, a research scientist at the University of Miami's Rosenstiel School, told me that the Atlantic is experiencing "the warmest sea surface temperatures on record for this time of year." That's not hyperbole—it's a direct quote from NOAA's July 2026 climate update. Warm water is fuel for hurricanes. More fuel means stronger storms, faster intensification, and more unpredictability.

The Numbers So Far

Let me give you the quick stats, because they're staggering. As of July 16, we've had: Hurricane Alex (Category 1, made landfall in Texas on June 14, caused $2.3 billion in damage), Hurricane Bonnie (Category 3, hit the Yucatán Peninsula on June 28, killed 14 people), and Hurricane Colin (Category 4, which is currently churning in the central Atlantic with winds of 145 mph, not yet threatening land but expected to recurve).

That's three hurricanes in six weeks. The 2024 season, which was already considered above-average, didn't see its third hurricane until August 12. The 2025 season was even quieter. This pace is unprecedented in the satellite era, which started in the 1960s. Dr. Phil Klotzbach, a hurricane researcher at Colorado State University, published a paper last week showing that the Accumulated Cyclone Energy (ACE) index—a measure of total storm activity—is already at 160% of the average for an entire season. And we're not even at the peak yet.

Why Is This Happening?

There's no single reason, but three factors are converging in a way that makes meteorologists nervous. First, sea surface temperatures in the Atlantic's Main Development Region—the area between Africa and the Caribbean where most major hurricanes form—are averaging 29.5°C (85.1°F) right now. That's more than 2°C above the 30-year average. For context, hurricanes need at least 26.5°C to form. The warmer the water, the more energy they can draw, and the stronger they can get.

Second, the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is in a neutral state this year, but it's transitioning toward La Niña, which typically reduces wind shear over the Atlantic. Wind shear—the change in wind speed and direction with height—can tear apart tropical systems. Less wind shear means more hurricanes can form and intensify. NOAA's Climate Prediction Center issued a statement on July 10 saying they expect La Niña conditions to develop by August, which would further amp up the season.

Third, the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), a long-term pattern of ocean temperature variability, has been in a warm phase since 1995. This warm phase has been associated with more active hurricane seasons. But Dr. Gabriel Vecchi, a climate scientist at Princeton, told me that the current warming is beyond what the natural cycle would produce. "We're seeing a clear climate change signal superimposed on natural variability," he said. "The ocean is warming faster than our models predicted even five years ago."

What This Means for the Rest of the Season

NOAA updated its seasonal forecast on July 8, predicting 22-28 named storms, 11-15 hurricanes, and 5-8 major hurricanes (Category 3 or higher). For comparison, an average season has 14 named storms, 7 hurricanes, and 3 major hurricanes. The 2026 forecast is the most aggressive NOAA has ever issued. Dr. Matthew Rosencrans, the lead forecaster for NOAA's Climate Prediction Center, said in a press conference that "all indicators point to an extremely active season."

The peak of the season—mid-August through October—is still ahead of us. That's when the most powerful storms typically form. If the current trends continue, we could see storms that intensify rapidly, going from a tropical depression to a major hurricane in 24-48 hours. That happened with Hurricane Colin last week, which went from a Category 1 to a Category 4 in less than 30 hours. Rapid intensification is one of the most dangerous phenomena because it leaves less time for evacuations and preparations.

What You Should Do Right Now

If you live anywhere along the Gulf Coast or the Eastern Seaboard from Texas to Maine, now is the time to prepare. Not in September, not when a storm is forming—now. Here's what emergency managers are telling people:

First, make sure your hurricane kit is stocked. The basics: seven days of non-perishable food and water (one gallon per person per day), a first aid kit, flashlights with extra batteries, a battery-powered or hand-crank radio, important documents in a waterproof container, and cash (ATMs won't work after a storm). Don't forget pet supplies and prescription medications. FEMA has a checklist on its website that's worth printing out.

Second, know your evacuation zone. Many counties have updated their maps after Hurricane Ian in 2022 showed that storm surge can reach farther inland than previously thought. Check with your local emergency management office to see if you're in a surge zone. If you are, have a plan for where you'll go and how you'll get there. Don't wait until the last minute—gasoline can run out, and roads can get clogged.

Third, review your insurance. Standard homeowners policies don't cover flood damage. You need separate flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program or a private provider. Even if you're not in a designated flood zone, heavy rainfall from hurricanes can cause flooding. After Hurricane Harvey in 2017, over half of the flooded homes were outside FEMA's high-risk zones.

Fourth, have a communication plan. Cell towers can go down during and after a storm. Designate an out-of-state contact who family members can check in with. Keep a list of emergency numbers written down—don't rely on your phone.

The Bigger Picture: Climate Change and Hurricanes

I don't want to turn this into a climate change lecture, but the connection is impossible to ignore. Warmer oceans, higher sea levels, and more moisture in the atmosphere all make hurricanes more dangerous. Sea levels have risen about 8 inches globally since 1900, which means storm surge can push farther inland. A warmer atmosphere holds more water vapor, which means hurricanes can dump more rain—Hurricane Harvey dropped over 60 inches in some parts of Texas in 2017, a record that may not stand for long.

Dr. Kerry Emanuel, a professor of atmospheric science at MIT who has been studying hurricanes for decades, told me that "the evidence is clear that climate change is making the strongest hurricanes stronger." His research shows that the proportion of Category 4 and 5 hurricanes has increased over the past 40 years, and that trend is expected to continue. The 2026 season is looking like a preview of what might become normal in a warming world.

A Personal Note

I've been through my share of hurricanes. I rode out Wilma in 2005, Irma in 2017, and Ian in 2022. Each one was terrifying in its own way. But Colin, the storm that just passed, was different. It formed so quickly that many people in the Caribbean didn't have time to prepare. One woman in the Bahamas told a reporter that she woke up to a Category 1 and went to bed with a Category 4. Her house survived, but barely.

I'm not trying to scare you. I'm trying to get you to take this seriously. The 2026 season is not normal. It's breaking records, and the worst may still be ahead. The best thing you can do is prepare now, pay attention to forecasts, and don't assume it won't happen to you. Hurricanes don't care about your plans. But you can care about your safety.
