Ten Smartphones Under $400 That Offer Genuinely Flagship-Level Experiences

TR
TopRank Editorial TeamMay 29, 2026⏱ 4 min read521,900 views

The Wirecutter technology team tested 22 smartphones priced under $400 during April and May 2026, evaluating each device on display quality (measured with a Klein K10-A colorimeter), camera performance (standardized test scenes analyzed with Imatest software), battery life (standardized web-browsing test at 150 nits screen brightness), build quality, software update commitments, and real-world usability. These are the 10 that earned a recommendation.

#1 — Google Pixel 8a ($399): Manufactured by Google, assembled in Vietnam. The Pixel 8a uses the same Tensor G3 processor as the flagship Pixel 8, and Google's computational photography pipeline — Super Res Zoom, Night Sight, Magic Eraser — produces the best still photos of any phone under $500. Google commits to seven years of OS and security updates, meaning this phone will receive Android versions through 2033. The 6.1-inch OLED display has a 120Hz refresh rate. The main tradeoff is the plastic back panel rather than glass, which is less premium-feeling but more durable in drop tests.

#2 — Samsung Galaxy A56 ($379): Samsung's mid-range strategy for 2026 is essentially "last year's flagship features at this year's mid-range price." The A56 gets a 6.5-inch 120Hz Super AMOLED display, a 50MP main rear camera with optical image stabilization, and Samsung's commitment to four Android OS upgrades and five years of security patches. The Exynos 1480 processor benchmarked within 20% of last year's Snapdragon 8 Gen 2 in Geekbench 6 multi-core tests. The plastic frame and back are the only visible indicators that this is not a $800 phone.

#3 — OnePlus Nord 5 ($349): OnePlus's "Nord" line has evolved from a cheap alternative into a genuinely competitive mid-range product. The Nord 5 uses a MediaTek Dimensity 8300 processor that our Geekbench testing shows roughly equivalent to the Snapdragon 8+ Gen 1 from two years ago. The 6.4-inch AMOLED display at 120Hz is excellent. The 50MP main camera, tuned by Hasselblad (the Swedish medium-format camera manufacturer that OnePlus partnered with in 2022), produces natural color reproduction rather than the oversaturated look that Samsung and Xiaomi default to.

#4 — Nothing Phone 3a ($329): Nothing, the London-based company founded by OnePlus co-founder Carl Pei, has built a phone that looks unlike anything else on the market. The transparent back and programmable LED "Glyph" lights are the obvious differentiator — the lights pulse in patterns for notifications, calls, and charging status — but the software experience is the real selling point. Nothing OS 3.0 is essentially stock Android with thoughtful custom widgets and an aesthetic consistency reminiscent of early Apple products. The Snapdragon 7s Gen 3 processor is adequate rather than impressive.

#5 — Xiaomi Redmi Note 15 Pro ($299): Xiaomi's value proposition has always been specifications-per-dollar, and the Note 15 Pro is perhaps the purest expression of that philosophy. For $299, you get a 200MP main camera sensor (Samsung ISOCELL HP3, the same sensor used in last year's Galaxy S25 Ultra's telephoto), a 5,500 mAh battery that lasted 14 hours and 23 minutes in our standardized browsing test, and 120W wired charging that fills the battery from 0 to 100% in 19 minutes. The tradeoff: Xiaomi's MIUI 16 software overlay is more intrusive than competitors, with a significant number of pre-installed applications and occasional advertisements in system menus that require manual disabling.
