The Best Pizza in NYC Is at a Gas Station — No, Seriously

TR
By Lauren Davis · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

I moved to New York City in 2018 with a mission: find the best slice of pizza in the five boroughs. It's a cliché, I know. Every tourist, every food blogger, every "Best Pizza in NYC" list promises to reveal the one true slice. But I've been at this for eight years. I've eaten at Lombardi's, Di Fara, John's of Bleecker, Prince Street Pizza, Scarr's, L'Industrie, and a hundred other spots. I've had slices that were transcendent and slices that were sad, sweaty triangles of regret.

So when a friend told me about a gas station in Queens that sold pizza, I laughed. "Come on," I said. "A gas station?" He was serious. "Just try it," he said. "It'll change your life." I rolled my eyes, but I was intrigued. The place is called Tony's Pizza, and it's attached to a Mobil station on Northern Boulevard in Jackson Heights. No sign, no website, no Instagram. Just a handwritten menu taped to the window.

I went on a Tuesday afternoon, expecting the worst. What I found was a revelation. The slice — a plain cheese — had a crust that was thin, crispy, and chewy all at once. The sauce was bright and acidic, like fresh tomatoes with a hint of oregano. The cheese was a perfect blend of mozzarella and pecorino, salty and rich. It cost $4. I ate two slices standing at a counter next to a display of energy drinks. It was the best pizza I've had in New York City. And I've spent thousands of dollars trying to find it.

The Hunt: How I Tested 12 Pizza Spots in 7 Days

I don't want you to think I'm exaggerating, so I did what any reasonable person would do: I spent a week eating 12 different slices from 12 different pizzerias, all within a 10-block radius of Tony's. My criteria were simple: I ordered a plain cheese slice (no toppings, no frills), ate it within 5 minutes of it coming out of the oven, and rated it on crust, sauce, cheese, and overall balance. I went at the same time each day (2 PM) to control for freshness. I drank only water to keep my palate clean. I know, I'm insane.

Here's the ranking, from worst to best:

12. Domino's (Northern Blvd). I'm not proud. But I needed a baseline. It's floppy, greasy, and tastes like cardboard with ketchup. It's not pizza; it's a food product.

11. Pizza Hut (30th Ave). Slightly better than Domino's — the crust has some structure — but still aggressively mediocre. The cheese has a plasticky texture.

10. Gino's Pizzeria (35th Ave). A neighborhood spot that's been around for 30 years. The slice is fine. Not bad, not great. The crust is too thick, the sauce is too sweet.

9. Joe's Pizza (Jackson Heights location). Joe's is a legend for a reason, but this outpost doesn't live up to the original. The slice was reheated, not fresh from the oven. Still better than most, but not transcendent.

8. Sal's Pizzeria (Roosevelt Ave). A solid, no-nonsense slice. Good crust, decent sauce. Nothing special, but reliable. If I lived nearby, I'd eat here once a week.

7. Luigi's Pizza (37th Ave). A Sicilian slice that's thick and fluffy, with a crunchy bottom. The sauce is chunky and rustic. It's good, but I'm a thin-crust guy.

6. New Park Pizza (37th Ave). Known for their grandma slice. The crust is buttery, the sauce is sweet. It's excellent, but it's not classic New York pizza — it's more like a focaccia with toppings.

5. L'Industrie (Williamsburg, but I took the L train). I know, I know, it's not in Queens. But I had to include it because it's universally praised. The slice is good — high-quality ingredients, a nice char on the crust. But it's overhyped. The crust is too thick for my taste, and the price ($6) is absurd for a slice.

4. Roberta's (Bushwick). A Neapolitan-style slice that's thin, chewy, and blistered from a wood-fired oven. The sauce is simple — just crushed tomatoes — and the cheese is fresh mozzarella. It's delicious, but it's not the same as a classic New York slice. It's a different category.

3. Di Fara Pizza (Midwood, Brooklyn). I took the Q train for an hour. It was worth it. Dom DeMarco's legacy lives on. The crust is thin and crispy, the sauce is bright, and the cheese is a triple blend. But the wait is 45 minutes, and the slice costs $6.50. It's great, but it's not worth the pilgrimage.

2. Scarr's Pizza (Lower East Side). Scarr's is famous for milling its own flour. The crust has a nutty, whole-wheat flavor that's unique. The sauce is tangy, the cheese is sharp. It's a fantastic slice. But it's not the best. Sorry, Scarr.

1. Tony's Pizza (Gas Station, Jackson Heights). And here we are. The slice from a gas station that beat every famous pizzeria in New York. Why? Because it's perfectly balanced. The crust is thin and crispy but has enough structure to hold the toppings. The sauce is acidic and herby without being sweet. The cheese is a perfect blend — not too oily, not too dry. And it costs $4. No wait, no hype, no Instagram photo. Just a perfect slice of pizza.

Why Tony's Is So Good (And Why Nobody Knows About It)

I talked to Tony, the owner, after my third visit. He's a 62-year-old Italian-American who's been making pizza for 40 years. He used to own a restaurant in Brooklyn but sold it after his wife passed away. Now he runs this gas station joint because, as he put it, "I don't want the stress of a real restaurant." He makes his dough fresh every morning, sources his tomatoes from Italy (San Marzanos, naturally), and uses a blend of three cheeses — whole milk mozzarella, aged provolone, and a touch of parmesan. The oven is a 30-year-old Bari deck oven that he bought used for $500.

"The secret is patience," he told me. "You can't rush the dough. You can't rush the oven. You have to let it happen." He's not interested in expanding, or getting a website, or being on TV. He just wants to make pizza and go home. And because of that, he makes the best pizza in New York City.

The Verdict

I know this sounds like a tall tale. A gas station pizza beating Di Fara, L'Industrie, and Scarr's? It doesn't make sense. But I've eaten at all of them, and I'm telling you: Tony's slice is better. It's not about nostalgia or hype or a cool story. It's about the taste. The first bite is perfect. The last bite is perfect. And the whole time you're eating it, you're thinking, "Why doesn't everyone know about this place?"

Maybe that's the magic. Tony's is a secret, and secrets taste better. But I'm sharing it with you because good food deserves to be celebrated, even if it comes from a gas station. Go to Jackson Heights, find the Mobil station on Northern Boulevard, and ask for Tony. Tell him the guy who ate 12 slices in a week sent you. He'll probably roll his eyes — but then he'll hand you a slice that changes everything.
