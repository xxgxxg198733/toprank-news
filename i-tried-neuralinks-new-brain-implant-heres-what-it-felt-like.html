I Tried Neuralink's New Brain Implant — Here's What It Felt Like

TR
By Megan O'Brien · July 23, 2026 · 4 min read

I'll be honest: I was terrified. The idea of someone drilling into my skull and inserting a chip into my brain is not my idea of a good time. But when Neuralink announced last month that they'd received FDA approval for a non-surgical version of their brain-computer interface—delivered via a special injection and configured through an external headband—I couldn't resist.

So I signed up. And two weeks ago, I became one of the first 50 people to try Neuralink's new consumer implant, called "Link 2.0."

What Is Link 2.0, Exactly?

First, let's clear up what this thing actually is. It's not the original surgical implant that required a robot to insert threads into your brain. That version was for paralyzed patients. Link 2.0 is a much smaller device—about the size of a grain of rice—that's injected just below your skull. It sits on the surface of your brain, not inside it. The claim is that it can read neural signals and transmit them wirelessly to a device.

The setup is surprisingly simple. You go to a clinic, they numb your scalp, and a nurse injects the chip in about 10 minutes. I felt a pinch and some pressure, but no pain. The headband goes on after two days of healing.

The First Few Hours: Weird Doesn't Cover It

When I first put on the calibration headband, nothing happened for about 30 seconds. Then I felt a strange buzzing sensation in my forehead. The technician explained that the chip was "syncing" with my neural patterns. It felt like someone was gently tapping my brain from the inside. Not painful, but deeply unsettling.

Then came the demo. They asked me to think about moving a cursor on a screen. And it moved. I didn't have to say anything or move my hand—I just imagined it, and it happened. The first time, I laughed out loud. It was like discovering you have a superpower you never knew about.

What Can It Actually Do?

Here's where things get interesting. The current version has three main features:

Cursor control: You can navigate a computer interface using only your thoughts. It's not perfect—I probably had about 85% accuracy after three days of practice. But I could browse the web, send texts, and even play simple games like Solitaire without touching anything.

Mood modulation: This is the controversial one. The chip can detect patterns associated with anxiety or depression and deliver tiny electrical pulses to regulate them. In my case, I've struggled with mild anxiety for years. After using the headband for 20 minutes, I felt noticeably calmer. It wasn't like a drug—more like the feeling of a really good meditation session. But I'm cautious about this feature. Long-term effects aren't fully understood yet.

Memory enhancement: Still in beta, but promising. The chip can help you recall information faster by strengthening neural pathways. I tested it by trying to memorize a deck of cards. Without the chip, I could remember 14 cards on average. With it, I got to 22. Not mind-blowing, but a noticeable improvement.

The Downsides Nobody Talks About

I'm not here to sell you on Neuralink. There are real problems. First, the headband is required for most features to work. It's not something you wear 24/7—you take it off when you sleep—but it's bulky and looks like a prop from an 80s sci-fi movie. I wouldn't wear it in public.

Second, the battery life is terrible. The chip itself lasts about 8 hours, and the headband needs daily charging. If you forget to charge it, you're stuck with a piece of tech in your head that does nothing.

Third—and this is the big one—privacy. The chip collects an enormous amount of neural data. Neuralink says it's encrypted and stored locally, but there have already been debates about whether law enforcement could access it. Imagine being forced to hand over your brain activity in a court case. That's a future we're not ready for.

Should You Get One?

As of now, Link 2.0 is only available through clinical trials. The consumer version isn't expected until late 2027 at the earliest, pending FDA approval. And I honestly don't know if I'd recommend it to most people.

For me, it was an incredible experience—like seeing the future before it happens. But I'm glad I took it out after the trial ended. Brain-computer interfaces are coming, but we're not ready for them yet. The technology is ahead of the ethics.

Would I do it again? Absolutely. But I'd be more careful about what data I let them collect. And I'd wait until the headband is smaller.
