I Spent a Week in Santorini Without Visiting a Single Instagram Spot

TR
By Hannah Powell · July 20, 2026 · 4 min read

Why I Skipped the Famous Spots

I love Santorini, but I hate the crowds. In July 2026, the island is packed—cruise ships dump thousands of tourists into Fira and Oia every day, all chasing that iconic sunset photo. I’ve done that once, and it was beautiful, but also stressful. This time, I wanted something different. So I spent a week exploring the parts of Santorini that aren’t on Instagram. And honestly? It was the best trip I’ve ever taken.

The Village That Time Forgot: Pyrgos

Most people stay in Fira or Oia. I booked a room in Pyrgos, a hilltop village in the center of the island. It’s quiet, with narrow streets, a medieval castle, and almost no tourists. The first morning, I sat at a small café called Metaxi Mas and watched the sunrise over the caldera. No crowds, no noise—just the sound of wind and a cat meowing for scraps.

Pyrgos has a few tavernas, but the best is a family-run spot called Kritikos. The owner, Yannis, makes his own wine from grapes grown on the island. I tried the Assyrtiko, a crisp white wine that paired perfectly with fresh grilled octopus. Yannis told me his family has been making wine here for four generations. That’s the kind of Santorini you don’t see in photos.

The Beach Nobody Talks About: Vlychada

Santorini’s famous beaches—Red Beach, White Beach—are packed. Vlychada, on the southwest coast, is not. The sand is dark volcanic gravel, and the cliffs are carved into strange, lunar-like shapes by wind and waves. There’s a small taverna called Aktaion that rents sunbeds for €5 and serves the best tzatziki I’ve ever had. I spent an afternoon swimming in the cold, clear water, reading a book, and watching fishermen repair their nets. There were maybe 15 other people there.

If you want a beach with facilities, head to Perissa or Kamari, which have black sand and more amenities. But Vlychada is perfect for solitude. Bring water and snacks—there’s only one shop.

The Hike from Fira to Oia (But in Reverse)

The classic hike from Fira to Oia is famous for a reason: the views are stunning. But it’s also crowded, especially near Oia. I did the hike in reverse, starting at 6am from Oia and walking toward Fira. The sun was at my back, and I had the trail mostly to myself. The path winds along the caldera edge, past blue-domed churches and whitewashed houses. My favorite moment was stopping at the small chapel of Agios Nikolaos, where a monk offered me water and a blessing.

The hike takes about 3 hours at a moderate pace. Wear good shoes—it’s rocky in parts. And bring a hat; there’s no shade. But the payoff is the view of the caldera from above. It’s humbling.

The Best Meal I’ve Ever Had (Yes, Really)

I don’t say this lightly. The best meal of my life was at a restaurant called Selene, in Pyrgos. It’s run by chef Nikos Roussos, who uses only local ingredients. I had the tomato fritters, made with Santorini’s famous cherry tomatoes, which are sweet and intense. Then the lobster spaghetti, with pasta cooked al dente and a sauce that tasted like the sea. But the dessert—a chocolate mousse with olive oil and sea salt—was transcendent. The bill came to €80 for two people, which is expensive for Greece, but worth every euro. Book weeks ahead.

The Secret Winery: Estate Argyros

Santorini has dozens of wineries, but most are touristy. Estate Argyros, on the south of the island, is different. It’s a family estate that’s been producing wine since 1903. The tasting room is a simple stone building with a terrace overlooking the vineyards. I tried a Vinsanto, a sweet dessert wine aged for 20 years, and it was like drinking liquid gold. The owner, Yannis Argyros, walked me through the tasting himself, explaining how the volcanic soil gives the wine its minerality. A tasting costs €20, and you get to keep the glass.

Unlike the bigger wineries, it’s never crowded. Go on a weekday afternoon.

The Sunset That Changed My Mind

I avoided Oia’s sunset, but I still wanted a good view. The best spot is actually the castle in Pyrgos. It’s a 10-minute walk from the village, and from the top, you see the entire caldera. I sat on a stone wall with a bottle of local beer, watching the sky turn pink, then orange, then deep purple. A few other people were there, but everyone was quiet. It felt sacred.

If you want a sunset dinner, book a table at Phira Restaurant in Fira’s old port. It’s less famous than the cliffside spots, but the food is excellent and the views are the same. Just don’t expect to be alone.

Practical Tips for a Different Santorini

Rent a car or ATV. Public transport is slow and crowded. Stay in Pyrgos, Megalochori, or Akrotiri—not Fira or Oia. Eat at tavernas that don’t have English menus. Wake up early. Skip the winery tours with buses and find the small family estates. And most importantly, don’t feel pressured to see everything. Santorini is small, but its beauty is in the quiet moments. I came home with no Instagram-worthy photos, but I left with a deeper love for the island.
