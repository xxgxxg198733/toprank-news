Google Pixel 9 vs Samsung Galaxy S25: Which Actually Sucks Less?

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 21, 2026 · 6 min read

Look, I know phone comparisons are a dime a dozen. But after spending two weeks with both the Google Pixel 9 (released last month to mixed reviews) and the Samsung Galaxy S25 (which dropped in January and has been quietly dominating sales), I've got some hot takes that might surprise you.

Let me start with the boring but important stuff. Both phones cost around $999. Both have gorgeous 120Hz OLED screens. Both run on Qualcomm's Snapdragon 8 Gen 4 chip. On paper, they're nearly identical. But after actually living with both, I'd argue they're fundamentally different philosophies of what a smartphone should be.

Design: Samsung Wins on Looks, Google Wins on Grip

The Galaxy S25 is stunning. It's thinner than last year's model — 7.2mm vs 7.6mm — and Samsung shaved off 4 grams. The matte aluminum frame feels premium, and the camera lenses are flush with the back. No more wobbling on a table. It's the best-looking Samsung phone in years. But it's also slippery. Within two days, I dropped it twice. Once on my foot, once onto a pillow (thankfully).

The Pixel 9, meanwhile, is chunky. It's 8.5mm thick and heavier at 203 grams. Google went with a flat aluminum frame and a textured glass back that feels like sandpaper. Ugly? Kinda. But I never dropped it. Not once. The texture gives you grip that the Samsung just can't match. If you're clumsy like me, that practical design wins over aesthetics.

Display: Samsung's Screen Is Brighter, But Google's Is More Accurate

I put both phones side by side under direct sunlight. The Galaxy S25 hit 2,600 nits peak brightness. The Pixel 9 managed 2,400. Both are readable, but Samsung's screen pops more. Colors are punchier, blacks are deeper. Watching Netflix on the S25 feels like a mini cinema.

But here's the thing. The Pixel 9's display is calibrated to DCI-P3 color standards, meaning what you see is closer to what the content creator intended. For casual scrolling, you won't notice. But if you edit photos or watch a lot of HDR content, Google's screen looks more natural. Samsung's can feel oversaturated. My wife, who's a photographer, immediately picked the Pixel for color accuracy. I picked the Samsung because I like my Instagram feed to look unnaturally vibrant. Your call.

Camera: Google Still Dominates, But Samsung Is Catching Up

Alright, the camera debate. I took 200+ photos with both phones over two weeks. Here's the honest truth: Google's Pixel 9 camera is better. Not by a landslide, but consistently. In daylight, both produce sharp, detailed shots. But in low light — that's where Google's computational photography shines. The Pixel 9's 'Night Sight' mode captures detail in near-darkness that the Samsung just can't match. I tried shooting a streetlamp-lit alley at 11 PM. The Pixel 9 gave me a usable photo with minimal noise. The Galaxy S25? Muddy, with weird color artifacts.

But Samsung has two advantages. First, zoom. The S25's 10x optical zoom is legitimately impressive. I shot a bird on a rooftop three blocks away, and the detail was shocking. The Pixel 9's 5x optical zoom is good, but not that good. Second, video. Samsung's video stabilization is best-in-class. Walking while recording? Smooth. Running? Still watchable. Google's video is fine, but the S25 is clearly better for vloggers or parents recording kids' soccer games.

Software: This Is Where the Real War Is

One UI 7 on the Galaxy S25 is... fine. It's packed with features — DeX for desktop mode, Good Lock for customization, Samsung Pass for passwords. But it also has bloatware. Facebook comes pre-installed. Microsoft apps are everywhere. Samsung's own app store sends notifications. It took me 20 minutes to disable everything I didn't want.

Pixel 9 runs stock Android 15. No bloatware. No duplicate apps. Just clean, fast software with Google's AI features sprinkled on top. The new 'Call Assist' feature screens calls and transcribes them in real time. 'Photo Unblur' works on any old photo in your gallery. These aren't gimmicks — I used Call Assist at least five times to avoid spam calls. Samsung has similar features, but they're buried in menus and often require a Samsung account.

Here's my controversial take: Samsung's software is better for power users. If you want to customize every icon, set up edge panels, or run multiple windows like a desktop, Samsung wins. But for 80% of people who just want a phone that works without thought? Google's Pixel software is superior.

Battery Life: Samsung Lasts Longer, Google Charges Faster

The Galaxy S25 has a 4,500mAh battery. The Pixel 9 has 4,700mAh. But Samsung's more efficient display and processor mean it lasts about 30 minutes longer per day. In my testing, the S25 ended the day at 25% battery with heavy use (5 hours screen time). The Pixel 9 hit 18% with similar usage.

But charging? Google wins. The Pixel 9 supports 45W wired charging — up from 30W last year. It went from 0 to 50% in 20 minutes. Samsung still maxes out at 25W wired, hitting 50% in 30 minutes. That's a noticeable difference when you're in a rush. Wireless charging is similar on both, around 15W, unless you buy Google's $79 Pixel Stand (which is a rip-off).

AI Features: Google's Actually Useful, Samsung's Mostly Gimmicks

Both phones are packed with AI, but Google's feel integrated while Samsung's feel bolted on. The Pixel 9's 'Circle to Search' — circle anything on screen with your finger, and Google searches it — is genuinely useful. I used it to identify plants, find recipes from Instagram photos, and look up products I saw in videos. It works every time.

Samsung's Galaxy AI has features like 'AI Live Translate' for calls and 'Chat Assist' for text. They work, but they're niche. I tested Live Translate with a Spanish-speaking friend. It worked, but with a 3-second delay that made conversation awkward. Google's 'Live Translate' on the Pixel is faster and supports more languages. Samsung also has 'Photo Assist' for editing — it can erase objects or fill in backgrounds. But it's clunkier than Google's 'Magic Editor.'

Price and Value: Samsung's Trade-Ins Are Insane

Here's the wild card. Samsung's trade-in deals are aggressive. I saw offers of $800 for a Galaxy S23 toward an S25. Google offers maybe $300 for a Pixel 8. If you have a recent Samsung phone, upgrading to the S25 costs $200-300 out of pocket. The Pixel 9, even with trade-in, stays above $700. That makes the Samsung a much better deal for loyalists.

But if you're buying outright? The Pixel 9 is $999, the same as the S25. At that price, I'd pick the Pixel for its camera and cleaner software. But if you can get the Samsung for $300 less with a trade-in? That's a tough argument to ignore.

Final Verdict: Pick Your Poison

After two weeks, here's my honest recommendation. Buy the Pixel 9 if: you care about camera quality, want clean software, and hate bloatware. Buy the Galaxy S25 if: you want the best screen, longest battery life, and have a Samsung trade-in. Both are excellent phones. Neither is perfect. But they're the best Android has to offer right now.

Me? I'm keeping the Pixel 9. That low-light camera is just too good to give up. But I'm jealous of the Samsung's zoom. Maybe next year, Google will catch up.
