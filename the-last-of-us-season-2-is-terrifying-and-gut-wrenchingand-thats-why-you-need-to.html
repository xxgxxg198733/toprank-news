‘The Last of Us’ Season 2 Is Terrifying and Gut-Wrenching—And That’s Why You Need to Watch It

TR
By TopRank Team · June 23, 2026 · 3 min read

I loved the first season of The Last of Us. I said it was the best video game adaptation ever made, and I meant it. But I also said I was nervous about Season 2, because the source material—The Last of Us Part II—is divisive among fans. Some people hated the story because it’s brutal, it kills off beloved characters, and it forces you to play as a character you’re not sure you like. I was one of the people who loved the game, but I had no idea how they’d translate it to TV without alienating half the audience. Well, the first three episodes of Season 2 dropped on HBO Max on June 15, 2026, and I’ve watched each one twice. Here’s my honest review.

The First Episode: A Slow-Burn Masterpiece

Episode 1 is called “The Farm,” and it’s set four years after the events of Season 1. Joel and Ellie are living in Jackson, a thriving community in Wyoming. Joel is greyer, softer, and clearly trying to be a dad. Ellie is a teenager now, played by Bella Ramsey with the same ferocity but a new layer of teenage angst. The episode is quiet: there’s a dance, a argument, a moment where Joel buys Ellie a guitar. The infected barely appear. Some people on Reddit are calling it “boring,” but I think they’re missing the point. This episode is setting the emotional trap. You get comfortable. You love them again. And then the finale of the episode—a shot of a woman stalking Ellie in the woods—hints at the storm to come.

The Second Episode: The Moment Everyone Will Be Talking About

I won’t spoil it, but anyone who knows the game knows that scene. It happens in Episode 2. And let me tell you: it’s even more horrifying on screen. The director, Craig Mazin, uses silence and long takes to build dread. You know what’s coming, but you still hope it won’t. When it does, I gasped. I actually gasped. Then I cried for about ten minutes. The acting is incredible—Pedro Pascal delivers a performance that’s raw and haunting. The episode ends with a credit song that made me sit in silence for a full minute.

The Third Episode: The Controversial Switch

Episode 3 follows Abby, the new character who’s at the center of the game’s controversy. The internet has been arguing about Abby for five years, and the show doesn’t shy away. In this episode, we see her backstory, her motivations, and her own version of love and loss. I’ll be honest: I went into this episode skeptical. I didn’t want to like Abby. But the show does something clever: it doesn’t ask you to forgive her. It just asks you to understand her. By the end, I didn’t love her, but I didn’t hate her either. That’s a huge achievement. The episode is directed by Lulu Wang, and it’s a visual stunner—the rain-soaked Seattle landscape is gorgeous and miserable at the same time.

Is It Too Dark?

Look, The Last of Us Season 2 is not a fun watch. It’s about grief, revenge, and the cycle of violence. There are moments that made me cover my eyes. One scene in particular—a brutal fight in a theater—made me feel physically ill. But that’s the point. The show doesn’t glorify violence; it shows the cost. If you want a light comedy, watch Crash Landing on Laughter. But if you want a story that will stay with you for days, this is it.

The Verdict

I’m only three episodes in, but I’m calling it: The Last of Us Season 2 is the best TV drama of 2026 so far. It’s terrifying, heartbreaking, and unflinching. Bella Ramsey and Pedro Pascal deserve Emmys. The writing is sharp. The production design is cinematic. And the story, despite its darkness, has a message about hope that shines through. If you can handle the pain, watch it. If not, wait until you’re in a better headspace. But don’t skip it. It’s a masterpiece.
