10 Things I Learned From Watching the SAG-AFTRA Strike Fallout (One Year Later)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 6 min read

A year and a half after the SAG-AFTRA strike ended, I keep hearing people say 'the industry has changed forever.' And yeah, that's true. But not in the ways you might think.

I've been covering Hollywood for a decade, and I've never seen anything like what happened between July and November 2023. The union, representing 160,000 actors, walked off the job alongside the WGA writers. It was the first time both unions had struck simultaneously since 1960. Production ground to a halt. The fall TV season evaporated. Movie releases got pushed. It was chaos.

But a year later, what's the actual legacy? I dug into the data, talked to industry insiders, and watched the landscape shift. Here are 10 things I learned.

1. The AI Fight Isn't Over — It's Just Started

The biggest win of the strike was the AI protections. The new contract requires studios to get an actor's consent before using their likeness digitally, and to pay them for it. Sounds good, right?

Except the loopholes are enormous. The contract only covers 'principal' performers — background actors are largely unprotected. And the definition of 'digital replica' is narrow enough that studios are already finding ways around it. I've heard from three different agents that their clients have been asked to sign away digital rights in standard contracts, with no additional compensation.

The union is fighting these battles case by case, but it's slow. The technology is moving faster than the law, as always.

2. Streaming Residuals Finally Got Real — But It's Not Enough

Before the strike, streaming residuals were a joke. A hit show on Netflix paid actors pennies compared to a syndicated network show. The new contract introduced a 'streaming participation bonus' — basically, a performance-based bonus for shows that succeed on streaming platforms.

But here's the thing: the bonus only kicks in for shows that achieve a certain viewership threshold, and the formula is opaque. Netflix, Apple, Amazon — they all guard their data like state secrets. The union negotiated for more transparency, but the studios pushed back hard.

One working actor I spoke with said her residual check for a Netflix series that ran for three seasons was $87. For the entire year. That's not a typo.

3. The 'Haves' and 'Have-Nots' Gap Widened

The strike was supposed to unite actors. And in some ways, it did. Big-name stars like Meryl Streep and George Clooney publicly supported the strike and contributed to the union's hardship fund.

But the reality is that A-list actors were fine. They have production companies, endorsement deals, and enough money to wait out a strike. The people who really suffered were the middle-class actors — the ones who do guest spots, indie films, and voice work. Many of them lost their health insurance during the strike because they couldn't meet the earnings threshold.

And the gap has only grown since. Big stars are getting bigger deals. Everyone else is fighting for scraps.

4. Production Hasn't Returned to Pre-Strike Levels

If you've felt like there's less new content, you're not imagining it. According to data from FilmLA, on-location filming in Los Angeles was down 34% in 2024 compared to the pre-strike average. And it's not just because of the strike.

Studios are producing less. They're more cautious. The streaming gold rush is over, and the industry is contracting. Fewer shows being made means fewer jobs for actors, writers, and crew. The strike accelerated a trend that was already happening.

5. Independent Film Had a Moment — And It Might Last

One unexpected consequence of the strike was a boost for indie films. With major studios shut down, attention shifted to smaller projects that weren't affected by the strike because they were made under different contracts (like SAG-AFTRA's low-budget agreement).

Festivals like Sundance and SXSW in 2024 were packed with strong independent work. Films like 'A Real Pain' and 'The Assessment' got major distribution deals. And audiences seem to be responding. The box office share for indie films grew in 2024 for the first time in five years.

Is this a blip or a trend? I'm cautiously optimistic. If audiences keep showing up, the industry might finally remember that you don't need a $200 million budget to tell a great story.

6. The Podcast and Audiobook Boom for Actors

During the strike, actors couldn't promote their film or TV projects. So many turned to podcasts — not just as guests, but as creators. I've seen a massive increase in actor-hosted podcasts since 2023. It's become a way to maintain visibility and income when traditional acting work is scarce.

Audiobook narration also exploded. SAG-AFTRA's audiobook contract actually saw increased membership during the strike. For many actors, it became a reliable income stream. And the quality has been impressive — some of the best audiobooks I've listened to this year have been narrated by actors I recognize from TV.

7. The 'One Show at a Time' Model Is Dead

Before the strike, it was common for actors to work on multiple shows simultaneously — a guest spot here, a recurring role there. But the new contract changes how scheduling works. Studios now have options to hold actors exclusively, which means fewer opportunities to juggle projects.

For actors, this is a double-edged sword. Exclusivity means more stability and commitment from the studio. But it also means turning down other work. And in an industry with fewer jobs, that's a tough choice.

8. The Role of Social Media Changed Forever

During the strike, social media became a battleground. Actors used their platforms to share information, rally support, and call out studios. But it also exposed a divide. Some actors were criticized for not being visible enough. Others faced harassment for taking side deals.

Post-strike, the relationship between actors and social media is more complicated. Many are more cautious about what they post. Some have hired social media managers to handle their accounts. The era of the 'authentic' celebrity may be over — or at least, deeply mediated.

9. The Human Cost Is Still Being Felt

I can't write about the strike without mentioning the real people affected. I know a camera operator who lost his house during the strike. A makeup artist who drained her retirement savings. A writer who had to move back in with his parents at 45.

The industry talks about 'recovery,' but for many, there's no recovery. They've left Hollywood entirely. The strike accelerated an exodus that was already happening — people leaving LA for cheaper cities, leaving the industry for stable jobs. The talent drain is real, and it's going to take years to rebuild.

10. The Unions Are Stronger — But for How Long?

The SAG-AFTRA and WGA strikes showed that collective action works. The unions won meaningful concessions. Membership has actually grown since the strike, with many non-union actors rushing to join.

But the studios are already looking for revenge. Contract negotiations for the next cycle are looming. And the AMPTP (the group that represents the studios) has shown no signs of compromising. The next fight could be even harder.

If there's one lesson from the strike, it's this: the industry only changes when people force it to. The actors did that in 2023. Whether they can do it again — and whether the public will still support them — remains to be seen.

I'll be watching. And I'll be here to tell you what happens next.
