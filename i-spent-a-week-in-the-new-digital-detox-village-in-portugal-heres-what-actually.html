I Spent a Week in the New 'Digital Detox' Village in Portugal — Here's What Actually Happened

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 21, 2026 · 7 min read

I'm writing this on a legal pad, sitting on a stone wall overlooking a valley of cork trees. There are sheep somewhere to my left. The only sound is wind and an occasional bell. I haven't checked my phone in six days. And I'm not panicking.

This is the village of São João das Lampas, about an hour north of Lisbon. It's one of five villages in Portugal's 'Digital Detox' initiative, launched by the government in April 2026. The program pays visitors €200 a week to stay in rural villages and disconnect from technology. No smartphones. No laptops. No Wi-Fi. The idea is to revive dying villages while offering stressed-out city people a real break.

I was skeptical. I'm a journalist. I'm addicted to my phone. The idea of going a week without internet sounded like torture. But I applied, got accepted, and spent seven days in a whitewashed house with no Wi-Fi, no TV, and only a landline phone for emergencies. Here's what happened.

How the Program Works (And Whether It's a Gimmick)

The Portuguese government partnered with local councils to identify five villages with declining populations. Each village has 10-15 houses available for visitors. You apply online, fill out a form explaining why you need a digital detox, and get approved based on availability. The cost is €200 per week — that's about $215. You get a private room in a shared house (with up to three other people) or a small apartment. Meals are provided by local families — breakfast and dinner, home-cooked, traditional Portuguese food.

The rules are strict. You surrender your phone and laptop upon arrival. They're locked in a safe that only the village coordinator can open. You get a basic Nokia brick phone for emergencies. No internet access anywhere in the village. There's a landline in the main square for calls. That's it.

I thought it would feel like prison. It didn't. It felt like summer camp for adults. There were about 20 other visitors during my week — a mix of Germans, Brits, Americans, and one Brazilian couple. Ages ranged from 22 to 67. Everyone was there for the same reason: burnout.

The First Two Days Were the Hardest

Day one, I was irritable. I kept reaching for my pocket to check notifications that weren't there. I walked around the village, which took about 15 minutes, and then didn't know what to do. The village coordinator, a woman named Teresa, suggested I help with the morning bread-making. I agreed, mostly because I was bored.

Bread-making is hard. Kneading dough for 20 minutes left my arms sore. But there's something meditative about it. No multitasking. Just your hands in dough, the smell of yeast, and the sound of the wood-fired oven crackling. I made two loaves that morning. They were ugly. But eating them with butter and local honey at breakfast? That was genuinely satisfying.

Day two, I felt the withdrawal peak. I wanted to google something — I can't remember what. I wanted to scroll Instagram. I felt phantom vibrations in my thigh. I went for a hike in the cork forest with a British woman named Sarah. We talked for three hours. About her kids, my job, the cork trees, nothing important. It was the longest conversation I've had with a stranger in years.

By day three, something shifted. I stopped reaching for my phone. I started noticing things — the way light filters through cork trees, the sound of footsteps on gravel, the taste of coffee (which is better when you're not scrolling). I slept nine hours that night. I usually sleep six.

The Activities Are Surprisingly Fun

The program offers daily activities, but you're not forced to participate. I tried most of them. Cork harvesting was the highlight. I spent a morning with a 70-year-old farmer named João who showed me how to strip bark from cork trees without damaging them. It's skilled work — one wrong cut can ruin a tree. João has been doing it for 50 years. He told me he's never owned a smartphone. 'I don't need one,' he said. 'The trees tell me everything I need to know.' I'm not sure what that means, but I believed him.

Cooking classes with local women. I learned to make cataplana — a seafood stew cooked in a copper pot. We used clams, pork, and coriander. It was incredible. The women laughed at my attempts to chop onions properly. They taught me their grandmother's tricks. No recipes. Just instinct and taste.

Stargazing. The village has virtually no light pollution. On a clear night, the Milky Way is visible as a thick band across the sky. The coordinator set up a telescope, and we spent two hours looking at Saturn's rings and the craters of the moon. A German astrophysicist (yes, one of the guests) gave an impromptu lecture on constellations. It was better than any Netflix documentary.

Walking tours of the cork forest. We learned about the ecosystem — wild boar, eagles, and the endangered Iberian lynx (which apparently lives nearby, though I didn't see one). The guide, a young Portuguese ecologist, was passionate. She'd quit her corporate job in Lisbon to move here. 'I was dying in an office,' she said. 'Now I wake up and breathe.'

The Unexpected Social Experiment

What surprised me most was the social dynamics. Without phones, people actually talked to each other. At dinner, we sat around a long wooden table and had real conversations. No one checked their phone. No one took photos of their food. We argued about politics, shared travel stories, and laughed at each other's bad jokes.

I became close with a guy named Mark, a 34-year-old software engineer from Berlin. He'd come because he was on the verge of burnout. He told me he'd been working 80-hour weeks for two years. 'My doctor told me if I didn't take a break, I'd have a heart attack.' He wasn't joking. On day four, he cried during a walk. 'I forgot what silence sounds like,' he said.

There was also Maria, a 67-year-old retired teacher from London. She'd been widowed the year before and said she needed 'to feel something other than grief.' She spent most of her time gardening with the locals. By day six, she was laughing again. I saw her dancing at the village festival on Saturday night.

The village itself was welcoming. Locals were curious about us. They invited us into their homes, offered us wine, told us stories about the village's history. São João das Lampas has 200 residents, most over 60. The program brings in young people, energy, and money. It's not a gimmick — it's genuinely helping these communities survive.

The Return to Reality Was Jarring

On day seven, I got my phone back. I had 1,200 unread emails. My family had sent worried texts (I'd warned them, but they still worried). My Instagram was full of notifications about things that felt completely irrelevant. I scrolled for five minutes and felt nauseous. The colors were too bright. The speed was too fast. I put it down and went for a walk.

I took the train back to Lisbon. The city felt overwhelming. Too many people. Too many screens. I saw a teenager walking while watching TikTok on his phone and nearly crashing into a lamppost. A month ago, I would have done the same. Now, it looked ridiculous.

It's been two weeks since I returned. I've tried to limit my screen time. I bought an alarm clock so I don't need my phone in the bedroom. I deleted social media apps from my home screen. I'm not perfect — I still check my phone too often. But I'm better. I notice the world more. I have longer conversations. I sleep better.

Would I recommend the program? Absolutely. But it's not for everyone. If you can't handle being alone with your thoughts, this will be painful. If you're in the middle of a work crisis, don't go — your mind will race. But if you're burned out, curious, and willing to be bored for a few days, it's transformative.

The Portuguese government plans to expand the program to 20 villages by 2027. Applications are open on the official tourism website. I'm already planning my next trip. Maybe next time, I'll go for two weeks. After all, I've got nothing but time.
