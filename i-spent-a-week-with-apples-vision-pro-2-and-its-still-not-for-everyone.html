I Spent a Week With Apple's Vision Pro 2 — And It's Still Not for Everyone

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 22, 2026 · 4 min read

When Apple announced the Vision Pro 2 in June 2026, I was cautiously optimistic. The original Vision Pro was a technical marvel, but it was also a $3,500 paperweight for most people. The sequel promised to fix the biggest complaints: too heavy, too expensive, too isolated. So when I got a chance to use one for a full week, I jumped at it. Did Apple actually deliver?

Short answer: yes and no. The Vision Pro 2 is better in almost every way, but it still feels like a product for early adopters and developers, not your average person. Here's what I learned after wearing it for work, watching movies, and even trying to cook dinner with it on.

The Hardware: Lighter, but Not Light Enough

The original Vision Pro weighed about 1.5 pounds — heavy enough to cause neck strain after an hour. The Vision Pro 2 is down to 1.1 pounds, thanks to a new magnesium alloy frame and a smaller battery pack. That's a 27% reduction, and you feel it. I could wear it for two-hour stretches without discomfort. But it's still not something you'd wear all day. The new Solo Knit Band 2.0 is more comfortable, but the weight is still front-heavy. If you wear glasses, the Zeiss inserts work well, but they add a bit of weight. Improved, yes. Perfect, no.

The Display: Still the Best in the Industry

The Vision Pro 2 keeps the micro-OLED displays with 23 million pixels per eye. Text is sharp, colors are vibrant, and there's no noticeable lag. The field of view is slightly wider — about 110 degrees versus 100 on the original. Watching movies feels like having a 100-foot IMAX screen in your living room. I watched "Dune: Part Three" (finally out!) and it was breathtaking. For media consumption, this is the best device on the market. Period.

The Price: Cheaper, but Still Expensive

Apple dropped the price from $3,499 to $2,999. That's better, but still a lot. For comparison, the Meta Quest 3 costs $499. The Vision Pro 2 is better, but six times better? Probably not for most people. Apple also offers a financing option at $125/month, which makes it more accessible, but you're still paying over three grand. If you have the money and you love tech, it's worth it. For everyone else, wait for a deeper price cut.

The Software: More Apps, Same Isolation

The biggest software update is the new Spatial OS 2.0, which adds multitasking — you can now have up to 10 windows floating around you. I used it for work: I had Slack, Safari, and a Notes app open while watching a YouTube video. It worked surprisingly well. The gesture controls are intuitive — just tap your fingers to click, pinch to zoom. But typing is still a pain. The virtual keyboard is fine for short messages, but for real work, you need a Bluetooth keyboard. Also, the isolation problem remains. When you're wearing the Vision Pro, you're cut off from the world. The EyeSight feature (which shows your eyes on the front display) is improved, but people in the room still feel like you're ignoring them. I tried cooking with it — watching a YouTube recipe while wearing the headset — and nearly burned my hand. Not recommended.

The Killer App: FaceTime in Spatial Video

The one thing that blew me away was FaceTime in spatial video. You can now have a 3D video call where the other person appears as a life-size hologram in your space. I called my sister in Chicago, and she appeared on my couch, looking like she was actually there. The depth is convincing. It's not quite "Star Wars" hologram level, but it's close. For remote work, this could be huge. But it requires the other person to have a Vision Pro too, which limits adoption.

Battery Life: Still Two Hours

This is my biggest complaint. The battery pack still only lasts about 2 hours. You can plug it into a wall, but then you're tethered. Apple says they're working on a bigger battery, but for now, you're limited to short sessions. I had to charge it three times during a movie marathon. That's not great for a $3,000 device.

Final Thoughts: Impressive, but Not Essential

The Vision Pro 2 is the best spatial computing device you can buy. The display is incredible, the software is improved, and the price is slightly more reasonable. But it's still a luxury item for enthusiasts. If you're a developer, a tech reviewer, or someone with $3,000 to burn, go for it. For everyone else, the Quest 3 is a better value, and it can do 90% of what the Vision Pro does at a fraction of the price. Apple is pushing the boundaries, but the Vision Pro hasn't found its iPhone moment yet. Maybe the third time's the charm.
