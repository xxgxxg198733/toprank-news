I Bought the Meta Quest 4 — And It's Both Amazing and Terrifying

TR
By Rachel Greene · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

I'll admit it: I was a VR skeptic. I tried the original Oculus Rift back in 2016 and spent most of the time untangling cables and feeling nauseous. The Quest 2 was better, but the graphics were blocky and the games felt like tech demos. The Quest 3 improved things, but the $499 price tag and the clunky mixed reality mode kept me away.

So when the Meta Quest 4 launched in March 2026, I rolled my eyes. Another expensive headset, another promise of "spatial computing" that would change everything. But then I started seeing the reviews. The Verge called it "the first VR headset that non-enthusiasts should buy." Digital Trends said it "makes the Apple Vision Pro look like a prototype." And then Meta dropped the price: $349 for the base model, $449 for the 512GB version. I caved.

I've been using the Quest 4 for three weeks now, and I have complicated feelings. It's the most impressive piece of consumer technology I've ever owned. It's also the most unsettling. Let me explain why.

What Makes the Quest 4 So Good?

First, the hardware is a massive leap. The Qualcomm Snapdragon XR3 Gen 2 chip delivers graphics that rival a PlayStation 5 — I played "Half-Life: Alyx" (which runs natively now, no PC required) and was stunned by the detail. The resolution is 2284x2284 per eye, with a 120Hz refresh rate. No screen-door effect, no blur, no visible pixels. It's like looking through a window into another world.

Mixed reality is the real star. The Quest 4 has two 16MP color passthrough cameras that create a high-resolution view of your real environment. You can draw virtual windows on your walls, place a floating TV in your kitchen, or turn your coffee table into a Minecraft world. The depth sensor is accurate enough to map your room in seconds — furniture, walls, even pets. I set up a virtual workspace where my monitors float in mid-air, and I actually got work done. Typing on a virtual keyboard is still awkward, but for reading, browsing, and light editing, it's surprisingly usable.

The controllers are also redesigned. They're smaller, lighter, and track your fingers individually — you can point, pinch, and gesture without holding a button. Meta also added eye-tracking and face-tracking (more on that in a moment), which makes interactions feel natural. In a game like "Horizon Worlds," my avatar's eyes and mouth move in real-time, matching my expressions. It's uncanny. In a good way, mostly.

The Games and Apps Are Finally Here

The biggest criticism of VR has always been the lack of compelling content. That's changing fast. The Quest 4 launched with over 200 titles, including blockbusters like "Call of Duty: Modern Warfare VR" (a full campaign, not a tech demo), "Grand Theft Auto VI VR" (yes, the new one, in VR), and an exclusive "Alien: Isolation" sequel that's terrifying. I've spent 20 hours in "Elder Scrolls Online: VR" — it's the full MMO in virtual reality, with motion controls for combat. It works. It's amazing.

But the killer app for me is fitness. Supernatural, the subscription-based workout app, is on the Quest 4 with new 4K environments filmed in actual locations (I did a boxing workout on a beach in Hawaii). Beat Saber is still addictive. And a new app called "Body Fit" uses the headset's cameras to track your form during exercises — it corrected my squat posture in real-time. I've lost 5 pounds in three weeks without setting foot in a gym.

Productivity is also getting serious. Microsoft released a native version of Office 365 for Quest 4 that lets you edit Word documents and Excel spreadsheets on virtual monitors. It's not perfect — typing is slow, and the resolution isn't quite retina-level — but for reviewing documents or taking notes, it's better than a laptop in a cramped airplane seat.

The Dark Side: Privacy Concerns That Should Scare You

Okay, here's where I start to feel uneasy. The Quest 4 has eye-tracking cameras that monitor where you're looking at all times. It has face-tracking cameras that capture your expressions. It has outward-facing cameras that see your room, your furniture, your pets, your messy desk. All of this data is processed on-device for privacy — Meta says — but the company also says it uses "anonymized" data to improve its AI and advertising systems.

In April 2026, a whistleblower revealed that Meta had been using Quest 4 eye-tracking data to train an ad-targeting algorithm that could detect your emotional state. If you looked longer at a product, Meta would infer you were interested and serve you more ads for it. If you looked away quickly, it would infer disinterest. The company defended the practice, saying it was "opt-in" (it was buried in a 30-page EULA). The backlash was immediate. A group of US senators called for an FTC investigation. Meta paused the program in May, but the damage was done.

Then there's the security issue. In June, a hacker group called "VoidSec" demonstrated a proof-of-concept attack that could capture the Quest 4's camera feed by exploiting a vulnerability in the Unity engine. Meta patched it within 48 hours, but it raised questions about how vulnerable these devices are. Your phone has your photos and messages. The Quest 4 has a live video feed of your home. That's a different level of risk.

Should You Buy It?

I'm not going to tell you to run out and buy a Quest 4 without thinking. The privacy concerns are real, and Meta's track record on data ethics is, to put it mildly, not great. But I also can't deny that this device is incredible. It's the first time I've felt like VR is ready for mainstream use — the hardware is polished, the content is abundant, and the price is accessible.

If you're privacy-conscious, consider these alternatives:

  • Apple Vision Pro 2: Launched in September 2025, $2,499. Better privacy (Apple doesn't sell ads), but it's 7x the price and has a smaller app library.
  • Valve Index 2: Rumored for late 2026, PC-only. Best for hardcore gamers who don't care about portability.
  • Pico 5: Chinese competitor, $299, lacks Meta's app library but has better privacy policies (no ad targeting).

For me, I'm keeping the Quest 4. The experience is too good to give up. But I've made some changes: I disabled eye-tracking and face-tracking in settings (you can use the headset without them), I put a physical cover over the cameras when I'm not using it, and I'm careful about what I do in VR. It's a trade-off — I lose some functionality, but I sleep better at night.

That's the thing about bleeding-edge tech: it always comes with compromises. The Quest 4 is amazing, but it's also a reminder that when you buy a device that sees your world, you're trusting the company that made it. And trust, as Meta has shown, is a fragile thing.
