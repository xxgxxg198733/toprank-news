The 10 Best Movies of 2026 (So Far) — and 2 That Really Disappointed

TR
By TopRank Team · June 13, 2026 · 7 min read

We're halfway through 2026, and honestly? It's been a weird year for movies. We've got the usual avalanche of sequels and reboots, but there's also been some genuinely original stuff sneaking into theaters. I'm one of those people who goes to the cinema at least twice a week — sometimes alone, sometimes dragging friends — and I've sat through 47 new releases since January. That's a lot of bad popcorn.

Let's get the disappointments out of the way first. Because I need to vent.

The Two That Just Didn't Work

1. "Galaxy's Edge: Rise of the Resistance" — Disney's big-budget adaptation of their theme park ride? It should have been a slam dunk. It had a $300 million budget, James Gunn producing, and a cast full of rising stars. But the script felt like it was written by committee — which it probably was. The plot is a mess of fan service and confusing time jumps. I saw it opening weekend, and the audience was dead silent by the second act. Not in a good way. Just... confused. It made $120 million domestically in its first week, which sounds impressive until you realize it cost more than that to make. Word of mouth has been brutal. I've talked to five different people who walked out. I stayed, but I was checking my phone.

2. "The Last Harvester" — This indie darling from Sundance got picked up by A24 for a record $25 million. Everyone was calling it the next "Hereditary." It's not. It's a slow-burn horror film about a family farm and some kind of supernatural combine harvester. The cinematography is gorgeous — I'll give it that. But the pacing is so glacial that I started counting ceiling tiles. The third act has a reveal that's supposed to be shocking, but it's telegraphed from the first ten minutes. I get that A24 likes ambiguity, but this felt less like art and more like a student film that got too much money. I'm genuinely surprised by the 82% on Rotten Tomatoes. I think critics are scared to admit they were bored.

The 10 That Made It Worth It

1. "Luminara" — I don't usually cry at movies. I'm that guy who sits stone-faced through the emotional scenes. But "Luminara" got me. It's a sci-fi romance set on a dying space station, directed by Greta Gerwig (yes, the Barbie director). The lead performances from Florence Pugh and Dev Patel are incredible. The score is haunting. But what really makes it special is the ending — it takes a risk that most studios would have vetoed. It's been out for three weeks, and I've already seen it twice. It's grossed $85 million so far, which is solid for an original sci-fi film. If you see one movie this month, make it this one.

2. "Concrete Jungle" — A documentary about the real estate wars in Miami. I know that sounds dry, but trust me — it's riveting. Director Alex Gibney follows three families: one trying to buy a $50 million penthouse, one being evicted from a rent-controlled apartment, and one of the developers behind a massive new tower. The access is insane. There's a scene in a city council meeting where a developer literally laughs at a tenant crying. I wanted to throw my phone at the screen. It's streaming on Netflix now. Watch it with someone you want to argue with afterward.

3. "Drift" — A Japanese animated film from Studio Ghibli's new generation. It's about a girl who can see the memories of objects — a teapot that remembers a wedding, a sword that remembers a war. The animation is hand-drawn and absolutely gorgeous. The story is simple but profound. It won the Palme d'Or at Cannes this year, which is rare for an animated film. My only complaint is that the English dub loses some of the subtlety in the voice acting. See it in subtitles if you can.

4. "Signal Fire" — A war film set during the Korean War, but it's not about battles. It's about two soldiers — one American, one Chinese — who get separated from their units and have to survive together in the mountains. It's based on a true story I'd never heard of. The performances from John Boyega and Zhang Ziyi are career-best. There's a 20-minute sequence with almost no dialogue that is more tense than any action movie I've seen this year. It's bombing at the box office, which is a tragedy. Go support it.

5. "Neon Nights" — A neo-noir set in a futuristic Tokyo. Think "Blade Runner" meets "Drive." The director, Park Chan-wook (Oldboy), is back in top form. The lead, Maisie Williams, plays a hacker who uncovers a conspiracy involving AI-generated politicians. The visual style is incredible — all neon reflections and rain-slicked streets. The plot gets a bit convoluted in the middle, but it sticks the landing. It's rated R for violence and language, and it earns every bit of it.

6. "The Quiet Year" — A small, quiet film about a couple dealing with infertility. It sounds depressing, and parts of it are. But it's also funny and warm and real. Florence Pugh (again — she's having a monster year) and Andrew Scott play the couple. There's a scene where they're at a dinner party and someone asks when they're having kids, and the silence that follows is so perfectly acted I felt my own stomach drop. It's not a movie for everyone — if you need explosions, skip it. But if you want to feel something real, this is it.

7. "Rover" — A found-footage horror film about a Mars rover that starts transmitting weird signals. I know, I know — found footage is played out. But this one actually works. The gimmick is that it's all shot from the rover's cameras, so you're stuck in first-person perspective. The sound design is terrifying. I saw it at a midnight screening, and people were screaming. It's only 85 minutes long, which is the perfect length for a horror movie. No fat. Just pure dread.

8. "The Ballad of Billy Two-Shoes" — A country music biopic that doesn't suck. That's rare. It's about a fictional singer, but it feels real. The songs were written by Jason Isbell, and they're genuinely great. The lead, Jeremy Allen White (The Bear), does his own singing and playing. He's not a professional musician, but he's good enough that you believe it. The story is a familiar rise-and-fall arc, but the performances elevate it. I'm not a country fan, and I loved it.

9. "Echo" — A superhero movie that actually takes risks. It's about a deaf superhero, played by Lauren Ridloff. The action scenes are choreographed in sign language, which is something I've never seen before. The villain is a corrupt tech billionaire who's using sound weapons. The social commentary is a bit on the nose, but the action is incredible. There's a fight scene in a silent library that is one of the best action sequences I've seen in years. It's streaming on Disney+, and it's way better than anything Marvel has done in the last two years.

10. "The Art of Falling" — A documentary about a group of parkour athletes in Paris. It's not what you expect. It's not just cool tricks — it's about gentrification, about being young in a city that's pushing you out. The footage is stunning. There's a sequence where they run across the rooftops of Montmartre at sunrise, and I literally gasped. The director, Ladj Ly, is known for his raw style, and this is his best work. It's on Hulu. Watch it with good speakers.

So that's my list. I'm sure I missed something you loved, or you think I'm wrong about "The Last Harvester." That's fine. That's what movies are for — arguing about them. But if you take one recommendation from this, let it be "Luminara." Trust me on this one.
