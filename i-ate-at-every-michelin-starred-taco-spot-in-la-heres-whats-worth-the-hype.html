I Ate at Every Michelin-Starred Taco Spot in LA. Here's What's Worth the Hype.

TR
By Sarah Mitchell · July 16, 2026 · 6 min read

I love tacos. I mean, I really love tacos. I've eaten them from food trucks in Mexico City, street stalls in San Diego, and fancy restaurants in New York. But something weird has happened in Los Angeles over the past few years: taco joints started getting Michelin stars. Not just one or two—five of them, as of the 2025 Michelin Guide California update. That's more Michelin-starred taco spots than any city on the planet.

So I did what any sane taco obsessive would do: I spent a week eating at all five of them. I ate over 30 tacos, gained four pounds, and had some of the best meals of my life. I also had a few that made me wonder if the Michelin inspectors were paid off by the tourism board. Here's my honest ranking, from most to least impressive.

1. Holbox: The Reigning Champion

If you only go to one place on this list, make it Holbox. Located inside a food hall in South LA, this tiny stall run by chef Gilberto Cetina Jr. earned its first Michelin star in 2024 and retained it in 2025. And unlike some starred restaurants that feel like they're showing off, Holbox is genuinely unpretentious. You order at a counter, take a seat at a communal table, and eat some of the most stunning seafood you'll ever encounter.

The star of the show is the smoked kanpán taco. Kanpán is a type of amberjack, and they smoke it over mesquite, then serve it with a habanero salsa and pickled onions. The fish is buttery, smoky, and melts in your mouth. The tortillas are made fresh in-house from heirloom corn. It's a perfect taco—simple, balanced, and unforgettable. The uni taco, topped with sea urchin from Baja California, is almost as good. It costs $12, which sounds expensive for a taco until you taste it and realize you'd pay double.

Holbox also serves a Yucatán-style cochinita pibil that's been braised for 12 hours and is explosively flavorful. The whole experience feels like a masterclass in how to let good ingredients speak for themselves. If Michelin stars mean anything, they mean this.

2. Tacos Los Cholos: The Old Guard

Tacos Los Cholos in Anaheim (technically Orange County, but close enough to LA that I'm counting it) has been a local institution for years. It got its star in 2023 and has held onto it with a menu that's both traditional and inventive. The Al Pastor is the best I've had outside of Mexico City—the pork is marinated in achiote and pineapple, then cooked on a vertical spit, shaved off in thin, crispy slices. The pineapple garnish is grilled to the point of caramelization. It's sweet, savory, and smoky all at once.

What sets Los Cholos apart is the variety. They have 15 different tacos on the menu, including a vegetarian option with huitlacoche (corn fungus) that's earthy and delicious, and a lengua (beef tongue) that's so tender it's almost creamy. The consommé they serve as a side is rich and deeply beefy—perfect for dipping.

The only downside is the wait. On a Saturday night, I waited an hour and 45 minutes for a table. The tacos are worth it, but bring a friend and a good podcast.

3. Güelaguetza: Oaxacan Soul Food

This one's a bit of a cheat because Güelaguetza is famous for its mole, not its tacos. But they do serve tacos, and they're excellent—especially the taco de chapulines (grasshoppers), which are crunchy, nutty, and surprisingly addictive. The real reason to come here is the moles: they have seven different varieties, including a mole negro that's been simmered for two days and has over 30 ingredients. It's rich, complex, and completely unlike anything you've had before.

The Michelin star here feels earned, but it's a different kind of experience. Güelaguetza is a full-service restaurant with indoor seating, not a taco stand. The vibe is warm and family-oriented. The tlayuda—a Oaxacan pizza with beans, cheese, and your choice of meat—is a must-order. But if you're coming specifically for tacos, you might be slightly underwhelmed compared to the other spots on this list.

4. Villa's Tacos: The Hype Is Real (Mostly)

Villa's Tacos in Highland Park blew up on social media before it got its star, and the hype hasn't died down. The signature is the al pastor, which comes with a slice of grilled pineapple, fresh cilantro, and a spicy salsa roja. It's excellent—crispy on the edges, juicy in the center. The tortillas are blue corn, made by a local tortilleria, and they have a wonderful earthy flavor.

But here's my hot take: Villa's is good, not great. The al pastor is the best thing on the menu. The carnitas are dry, and the birria is watery compared to what you can get at a non-starred spot like Birrieria Gonzalez down the street. The prices are also steep—$6.50 per taco, which is high even by LA standards. I think Villa's earned its star because of consistency and quality of ingredients, but it's not the life-changing experience the TikTok videos claim.

That said, the salsa bar is incredible. They have six different salsas, including a habanero-mango that's sweet and fiery. I'd go back just for that.

5. Mariscos Jalisco: The Underdog

Mariscos Jalisco is a food truck in Boyle Heights that got a Michelin star in 2024. Yes, a food truck with a Michelin star. The specialty is the taco de camarón—a crispy, deep-fried shrimp taco topped with shredded cabbage, crema, and a vinegary salsa. It's messy, it's indulgent, and it's absolutely delicious. The shrimp is juicy, the batter is light and crunchy, and the cabbage adds a fresh crunch that cuts through the richness.

The truck also serves a seafood cocktail called campechana that's loaded with shrimp, octopus, and avocado in a spicy tomato broth. It's a meal in itself. The only reason Mariscos Jalisco is at the bottom of this list is that the menu is very limited—you can get about five things total. But what they do, they do better than almost anyone.

The Verdict: Are Michelin Stars Worth It for Tacos?

After eating at all five, I'm torn. The Michelin stars have brought attention and money to taquerias that deserve it—Holbox and Los Cholos are genuinely world-class. But they've also inflated prices and created lines that make it harder for locals to enjoy the places they've loved for years. Villa's used to be a neighborhood spot; now there's a line around the block every night.

If you're a taco obsessive like me, it's absolutely worth doing the tour. Start at Holbox, end at Mariscos Jalisco, and hit the others in between. You'll eat some of the best food of your life. But don't neglect the non-starred spots—there are hundreds of incredible taquerias in LA that will never get a star but will give you a taco that changes your day. That's the beauty of this city. The stars are just the beginning.
