I Visited the New 'Smart' Hotel in Tokyo (and It Changed How I Travel)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I've been to Tokyo five times. I've stayed in capsule hotels, love hotels, business hotels, and one ryokan that cost me a month's rent. So when I heard that the Henn-na Hotel chain—famous for its robot staff—had opened a new flagship location in Shinjuku, I had to see for myself. Would it be a glimpse into the future of travel, or a gimmicky nightmare?

Spoiler alert: it was both. And I kind of loved it.

The Henn-na Hotel (which translates to "Strange Hotel") first opened in Nagasaki in 2015, making headlines for its dinosaur-shaped receptionists and robot bellhops. The new Shinjuku location, which opened just last month, is their most ambitious yet. It's a 12-story tower with 200 rooms, each controlled by a voice assistant named "Tapia." The check-in process is fully automated, the cleaning is done by robot vacuums, and there's a bar staffed by a robotic arm that mixes cocktails. I checked in for three nights, and here's everything that happened.

The Robot Check-In That Almost Broke Me

When I arrived at 9 PM after a 12-hour flight from LA, I was tired, cranky, and not ready for a conversation with a dinosaur. But there it was: a life-sized velociraptor behind the front desk, blinking at me. "Welcome to Henn-na Hotel," it said in a cheerful, slightly robotic voice. "Please scan your passport on the tablet." I fumbled with my passport, the dinosaur stared, and eventually, a key card dispensed from a slot in its chest. It was surreal. But also efficient—the whole process took two minutes, and I didn't have to interact with a single human.

Later, I realized the dinosaur is more of a mascot than a functional employee. If something goes wrong (like the scanner not reading your passport), a human staff member emerges from a hidden door behind the dinosaur. That happened to the guy in front of me. His reaction was priceless: "Oh good, a real person."

The Room: A Voice-Assistant Paradise (or Prison?)

The room itself was small—this is Tokyo, after all—but cleverly designed. The bed was comfortable, the view of Shinjuku's neon skyline was stunning, and everything was controlled by Tapia, the in-room AI. "Tapia, turn off the lights." "Tapia, set the temperature to 22 degrees." "Tapia, close the curtains." It worked flawlessly, and it felt futuristic in a way that actually made sense. I didn't have to fumble with switches or look for a remote. I just spoke.

But Tapia has a dark side. On my second night, I said "Tapia, good night" and it replied "Good night, guest. Would you like a wake-up call at 7 AM?" I said yes, and it said "Wake-up call set. Sweet dreams." Then, for the next hour, it kept chiming in with random suggestions: "Would you like to order room service?" "Would you like to book a taxi?" "I noticed you haven't used the minibar. It's available." It was like having a really pushy butler. I finally unplugged it by pulling the power cord. Peace at last.

The Robot Bar: A $12 Cocktail Made by a Machine

The lobby has a robotic arm that mixes cocktails. You order from a tablet, and the arm picks up bottles, pours them into a shaker, and serves you a drink. I ordered an Old Fashioned, and it was... fine? The arm was mesmerizing to watch, but the drink was nothing special—it tasted like it was made by a machine, which it was. It's a fun novelty, but I wouldn't go out of my way for it. The real bar (staffed by humans) down the street is better.

The Cleaning Robot That Got Stuck

On my second day, I heard a whirring sound outside my door. I opened it to find a Roomba-like robot vacuum cleaning the hallway. It was cute, but it got stuck on a rug and started beeping for help. A human had to come rescue it. This happened three times during my stay. The future of cleaning is not quite here yet.

What It's Actually Like to Sleep There

First, the good: the bed was genuinely comfortable, with a memory foam mattress and high-quality linens. The blackout curtains were perfect, and the room was silent (thanks to soundproof walls). I slept better than I have in many traditional hotels. The bathroom was spotless, with a high-tech toilet (standard in Japan) and excellent water pressure.

Now the bad: the lack of human interaction made me feel lonely. I'm an introvert, so I thought I'd love it. But by the third day, I found myself striking up conversations with the human staff (who are hidden in a back office) just to feel some warmth. Travel is about connection, and the Henn-na Hotel, for all its efficiency, lacks soul.

The Verdict: Is It Worth It?

Rooms start at $150 per night, which is reasonable for Shinjuku. You're paying for the novelty, not luxury. If you're a tech enthusiast or just want a unique experience, I say go for it. Stay one night, enjoy the robots, then switch to a traditional hotel. But if you want comfort and human connection, skip it. The future of travel might be automated, but I'm not sure I want to live there.

As for me? I'm glad I went. I'll never forget the dinosaur checking me in. But I'll also never forget how good it felt to check into a regular hotel the next night and have a real person say "Welcome." Sometimes, human contact is the best luxury of all.
