The AI Phone That Actually Listens: Why the Rabbit R2 Is Weirder and Better Than You Think

TR
By Amanda Brooks · July 21, 2026 · 4 min read

Let me be honest: when I first heard about the Rabbit R2, I rolled my eyes. Another AI gadget? Another device that promises to 'revolutionize' how you interact with technology, only to end up in a drawer? I've seen this movie before. The Humane AI Pin was a disaster. The Rewind Pendant was a privacy nightmare. So when the Rabbit R2 showed up at my door, I was ready to be disappointed. But after a full week of using it, I have to admit: this thing is weirdly compelling. It's not a phone replacement. It's not a computer. It's something new—and it might actually be useful.

For those who missed the hype, the Rabbit R2 is a small, handheld device with a tiny screen, a camera, and a built-in AI assistant that can control apps on your behalf. You press a button, speak a command, and the AI does the rest—book a flight, order a pizza, send a message, whatever. It's like having a personal assistant that never sleeps. But does it actually work? I put it through its paces.

What the Rabbit R2 Actually Does (That Your Phone Can't)

The key difference between the Rabbit R2 and your phone's assistant (Siri, Google Assistant, Alexa) is that the Rabbit doesn't just answer questions—it performs tasks. It can navigate your apps, click buttons, and fill in forms using a 'large action model' trained to interact with user interfaces. So instead of asking Siri to 'order a pizza,' which usually opens the app and then leaves you to figure it out, the Rabbit R2 will open the app, select your favorite order, and check out. It's like having a virtual person use your phone for you.

I tested this by asking the R2 to book a dinner reservation at a local Italian restaurant. Within 30 seconds, the device's screen showed me the reservation confirmation. I didn't touch a single button. It felt like magic. Then I asked it to reschedule a meeting on my Google Calendar. It did that too, without a hitch. The accuracy is impressive—I'd say 80% of tasks succeed on the first try. The failures are usually because the app's interface changed or requires a login that the R2 can't handle. But overall, it's shockingly reliable.

The Design: Weird, But It Grows on You

The Rabbit R2 looks like a cross between a pager and a mini smartphone. It's about the size of a deck of cards, with a 2.5-inch touchscreen, a camera, and a physical button on the side. It's chunky, but not heavy. The screen is bright and decently sharp, though you're not going to watch movies on it. The battery lasts about two days with moderate use. It charges via USB-C, thank goodness.

The weirdest part is the AI voice. It's not a human voice—it's a synthesized one that sounds slightly robotic. But after a day, you get used to it. There's also a built-in speaker and microphone, and the device can hear you from across the room. I've used it while cooking, and it picked up my commands even with the stove fan running.

Where It Falls Short: It's Not for Everyone

Let's be real: the Rabbit R2 is not for everyone. It costs $299, plus a $15 monthly subscription for the AI service. That's a lot for a device that's essentially a phone accessory. If you're someone who's happy using Siri or Google Assistant, you probably don't need it. The R2 shines when you have complex tasks that require multiple steps—like booking flights or managing reservations. For simple stuff like setting a timer or checking the weather, it's overkill.

Privacy is also a concern. The device sends your voice commands to Rabbit's servers for processing. Rabbit says they don't store your data, but if you're paranoid about AI listening to you, this might not be for you. Me? I'm cautiously optimistic. I've been using it for a week and haven't had any issues, but I'm keeping an eye on it.

Should You Buy One?

If you're a tech enthusiast who loves trying new things, yes. The Rabbit R2 is a glimpse into the future of AI interaction. If you're someone who regularly books travel, orders food, or manages a busy schedule, it's legitimately useful. But if you're happy with your phone and don't feel limited by Siri, save your money. For now, the R2 is a niche device for early adopters. But I have a feeling that in a few years, every phone will have this capability built in. Rabbit is just ahead of the curve.
