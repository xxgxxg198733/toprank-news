Dune: Part Three Is the Best Sci-Fi Movie of the Decade — Here's Why It Works

TR
By TopRank Team · June 22, 2026 · 4 min read

I walked out of the theater at 3 AM on June 20, 2026, and I couldn't speak. Not because I was tired — I was wired. My hands were shaking. My mind was racing. Dune: Part Three is the best sci-fi movie I've seen since Blade Runner 2049, and it might be better than that. But it's not the movie I expected.

Denis Villeneuve has done something remarkable. He's taken Frank Herbert's notoriously dense and philosophical novel Dune Messiah — the book that everyone said was unfilmable — and turned it into a blockbuster that's both intellectually challenging and emotionally devastating. It's also deeply weird. I mean, really, really weird. And I loved every second of it.

The Story: Paul Atreides Faces the Consequences

If you've seen Part One and Part Two, you know the story. Paul Atreides (Timothée Chalamet) has become the Emperor of the Known Universe after leading a Fremen revolt against the Harkonnens. But Part Three picks up 12 years later, and Paul is a different man. He's older, harder, and haunted by the atrocities committed in his name. The jihad he tried to prevent has swept across the galaxy, killing billions. He's responsible.

The movie is about power — how it corrupts, how it isolates, and how it can crush the person who wields it. Chalamet gives the best performance of his career. His Paul is a tragic figure, a man who can see every possible future but is trapped in the worst one. There's a scene where he looks at himself in a mirror and doesn't recognize the face staring back. It's heartbreaking.

But the real star is Zendaya as Chani. In the books, Chani is a relatively passive character. Here, she's the moral center. She's the one who questions Paul's choices, who reminds him of the people he's sacrificed. Her final confrontation with Paul is one of the most powerful scenes I've ever seen in a sci-fi movie. I won't spoil it, but I will say I cried.

The Visuals: Villeneuve Outdoes Himself

I saw Part Three in IMAX 70mm, and I'm glad I did. The scale is immense. The opening shot — a slow zoom across the desert of Arrakis, with spice harvesters the size of cities — had me gripping my armrest. The sandworms are back, and they're more terrifying than ever. There's a sequence where a worm breaches the surface right next to Paul, and the sound design made my entire body vibrate.

But it's the quieter moments that stuck with me. There's a scene set in a garden on Caladan, where Paul talks to the ghost of his father, Leto (Oscar Isaac, in a brief but powerful cameo). The lighting is soft, the colors are muted, and the emotion is raw. It feels like a painting. Villeneuve has always been a master of visual storytelling, but here he's operating at a level that few directors ever reach.

What Nobody's Talking About: The Weird Stuff

Dune Messiah is a strange book. It deals with clones, resurrected beings, and a conspiracy to overthrow Paul by manipulating his personal relationships. The movie leans into that weirdness. There's a character called Hayt, a ghola (clone) of Paul's dead friend Duncan Idaho, played by Jason Momoa. Momoa is excellent — he brings a tragic quality to a character who's both familiar and alien.

There's also the Tleilaxu, a sect of genetic engineers who are genuinely creepy. They're played by a mix of actors wearing prosthetics and motion-capture, and they look like something out of a David Lynch film. One of them, Scytale, speaks in riddles and has a haunting presence. The movie doesn't explain everything — it trusts the audience to figure things out. I loved that.

Does It Work as a Movie on Its Own?

This is the big question. Can you see Part Three without having seen the first two? Honestly, no. The movie assumes you know the characters, the world, and the politics. It spends almost no time on exposition. If you haven't seen Part One and Part Two, you'll be lost.

But if you've followed the story from the beginning, Part Three is a rewarding conclusion. It ends on a note that's both hopeful and tragic — the kind of ending that makes you want to sit in the theater and think. I stayed for the entire credits, and so did half the audience. We were all processing what we'd just seen.

Is it the best Dune movie? I think so. It's the most ambitious, the most emotional, and the most visually stunning. Villeneuve has cemented his place as one of the greatest directors of our time. Go see it. On the biggest screen you can find.
