After 3 Months With Apple Vision Pro 2, I Have Complicated Feelings

TR
By Andrew Foster · July 18, 2026 · 6 min read

I pre-ordered the Apple Vision Pro 2 the day it launched on April 18, 2026. I'm not a tech reviewer — I'm just a guy who loves gadgets and has a bad habit of spending too much on them. But after three months of daily use (sometimes 4-5 hours at a stretch), I have thoughts. Lots of them. And most reviews I've read don't match my experience at all.

The Hardware Is Unbelievably Good (And Unbelievably Heavy)

Let's start with what everyone's talking about: the weight. Apple says the Vision Pro 2 is 35% lighter than the original, coming in at about 480 grams. That's still heavier than a Quest 3 (515g) or a Bigscreen Beyond (185g), but the weight distribution is better thanks to a new dual-strap system. I can wear it for about 90 minutes before my neck starts complaining. The original? I couldn't make it 45 minutes.

The screens are genuinely stunning — dual micro-OLED panels at 3660×3200 per eye, with a 120Hz refresh rate. Watching a 4K movie in this thing feels like sitting in a premium theater. I watched 'Dune: Part Three' in 3D last week (the new Denis Villeneuve film that premiered at Cannes in May), and I actually forgot I was wearing a headset for a few minutes. That's never happened to me before.

But here's the thing nobody mentions: the heat. After about an hour, the front of the headset gets noticeably warm — not uncomfortable, but you can feel it. Apple says this is normal and the cooling system is working. Still, in a 75-degree room, it's distracting.

Also, the battery life. Apple promised 3.5 hours of mixed use. I get about 2 hours and 40 minutes before it dies. That's better than the original (which barely hit 2 hours), but still laughably short for a device that costs $3,499. You basically need to keep it plugged in for any real session.

visionOS 3: Finally Feels Like a Real Operating System

The software is where the Vision Pro 2 really shines. visionOS 3, which launched with the headset, fixes almost everything I hated about the original. The biggest change: you can now have up to 12 app windows floating around you instead of 5. I've had a browser, Slack, a code editor, YouTube, and a calculator all open at once, and it didn't crash once. The original would have melted down with half that.

Multitasking is actually usable now. I can drag a window from one side of my room to the other with a flick of my wrist. The eye-tracking is so good that I can type by looking at keys — it's faster than using a virtual keyboard, though still slower than a real one. I've written about 40% of this article using the headset, and while it's not my preferred method, it works.

But the killer app for me is the new 'Spatial Desktop' mode. You can connect the Vision Pro 2 to your MacBook Pro (wirelessly, no dongle) and get a 4K virtual monitor that's basically infinite in size. I've used this to work from my couch, my bed, and even a park bench. The latency is imperceptible — maybe 15ms. For coding and writing, it's transformative.

The Killer Apps Are Finally Here

When the original Vision Pro launched, the app ecosystem was pathetic. A bunch of mediocre iPad ports and a few cool demos. This time, developers actually showed up. Here are the three apps that sold me:

First, 'Spatial Cinema' by IMAX — a dedicated app that streams movies in what feels like a full IMAX theater. The screen is so big you have to turn your head to see the edges. It's $14.99/month, which is steep, but if you watch a lot of movies, it's worth it. They added a 'D-BOX Motion' feature in June that syncs seat vibrations with the action. My God, watching 'Top Gun: Maverick' with that on was almost too intense.

Second, 'Spatial Studio' — a 3D modeling app that lets you sculpt objects with your hands. I'm not an artist, but I spent three hours making a terrible-looking vase, and it was the most fun I've had with a device in years. The haptic feedback through the new 'Touch Gloves' (sold separately for $149) makes it feel like you're actually shaping clay.

Third, 'Teleport' — a social app where you can meet friends as realistic avatars in shared 3D spaces. It uses the Vision Pro 2's improved face tracking (now with eye contact simulation) to make avatars that actually look like you. I had a 45-minute conversation with a friend in Tokyo, and it felt more real than a FaceTime call. We walked through a virtual version of the Shibuya crossing together. Bizarre and wonderful.

The Social Cost Is Real

I have to be honest: wearing this thing around other people is weird. I've been called 'RoboCop' by my wife, 'Darth Vader' by my neighbor, and 'that guy from that Black Mirror episode' by a barista. The front-facing display that shows your eyes (called EyeSight) helps a little — people can see your expressions — but it's still a giant visor on your face. You look ridiculous.

And there's a deeper problem: isolation. I caught myself spending whole evenings in the headset, watching movies or 'hanging out' in virtual spaces, while my wife was reading on the couch next to me. We were in the same room but not together. After a week of that, I set a rule: no Vision Pro after 8 PM unless we're watching something together (the shared viewing mode is actually great).

Apple needs to solve this. The device is amazing for solo experiences, but it's terrible for connection with people in the same physical space. Maybe that's not Apple's problem to solve — maybe it's mine. But it's worth thinking about if you're considering buying one.

The Price: Can I Justify $3,499?

Short answer: no. Long answer: it depends on your finances and how much you'll use it. I'm lucky enough to afford it without going into debt, but I still feel guilty every time I look at my credit card statement. The truth is, you need the $499 Travel Case (the headset is fragile), the $149 Touch Gloves (they genuinely improve the experience), and maybe the $99 extra battery pack to get through a long flight. That's $4,246 total. For that money, you could buy a MacBook Pro, an iPad Air, and a pair of AirPods Pro. And those devices are more useful for most people.

But if you're a developer, a 3D artist, or just a hardcore enthusiast with disposable income, the Vision Pro 2 is the most impressive piece of consumer tech I've ever used. It's not ready for the mainstream — the weight, heat, and social awkwardness are too much. But it's the first time I've felt like I'm using the future.

My prediction: the Vision Pro 3, probably in 2028, will be the one that breaks through. By then, Apple will have cut the weight in half, doubled the battery life, and brought the price under $2,000. The Vision Pro 2 is for early adopters willing to pay for the privilege of testing tomorrow's tech today. I'm okay with being that guy, but I wouldn't recommend it to most people.

Would I buy it again? Yes. But I'd think harder about it this time.
