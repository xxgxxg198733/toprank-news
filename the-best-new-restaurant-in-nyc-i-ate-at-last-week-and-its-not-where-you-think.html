The Best New Restaurant in NYC I Ate at Last Week (And It's Not Where You Think)

TR
By Megan O'Brien · July 21, 2026 · 4 min read

I ate at 12 new restaurants last week. It was exhausting, expensive, and absolutely worth it. But the one that blew my mind wasn't the multimillion-dollar project from a celebrity chef. It wasn't the place with the three-month waiting list. It was a tiny spot in Long Island City called Lutong Pinoy, run by a couple who just moved from Manila. And I need to tell you about it before the lines get ridiculous.

How I Found This Place

I was supposed to review a new tasting menu place in the West Village. It was good — very good, actually. But the kind of good that feels like it was designed by a committee. Perfectly edible, perfectly forgettable. I left feeling full but not satisfied. So I texted a friend who works in food media and asked: "What's the most interesting thing you've eaten this week?"

She sent me a Google Maps pin for Lutong Pinoy. "It's in a basement," she said. "Cash only. Go before the Times writes about it." So I went the next day.

First Impressions: A Basement in Queens

The restaurant is in the basement of an old brick building on Vernon Boulevard. There's a handwritten sign on the door that says "Lutong Pinoy" in red marker. Inside, there are maybe 12 tables, all mismatched. The walls are covered in photos of the owners' family back in the Philippines. A karaoke machine sits in the corner, dormant. The playlist is a mix of OPM (Original Pilipino Music) and 90s pop. It's not designed for Instagram. It's designed for people who love food.

The owner, a woman named Maria, came out to greet me. She's a former nurse who retired and decided to open a restaurant with her husband, who does all the cooking. "We just wanted to share our food," she told me. "No fusion, no twists. Just what we grew up eating." That's exactly what they serve.

The Food: No Shortcuts

I ordered five dishes, which was way too much for one person, but I couldn't stop. Here's what stood out:

Chicken Adobo. This is the national dish of the Philippines, and I've had versions at dozens of restaurants. This one was different. The vinegar was sharp but not overwhelming. The soy sauce was rich without being salty. The chicken was fall-apart tender, and the sauce had a depth that could only come from hours of simmering. Maria's husband uses a secret blend of peppercorns — he wouldn't tell me what's in it, and I respect that.

Sisig. This is pork cheek and liver, chopped fine, served on a sizzling plate with an egg on top. It's a classic, but the version here is extraordinary. The pork is crispy in some bites, tender in others. The calamansi (a Filipino citrus) cuts through the richness. The egg yolk mixes into the whole thing and creates a sauce that I wanted to drink with a spoon.

Kare-Kare. This is a peanut-based stew with oxtail and tripe. I've never had a good version outside of the Philippines. This one is good. The peanut flavor is nutty and deep, not sweet like some Americanized versions. The bagoong (fermented shrimp paste) on the side is essential — it adds a funky saltiness that makes the dish sing.

Lechon Kawali. Deep-fried pork belly. It's simple, but execution matters. This version is perfect — the skin is crackling, the meat is juicy, and it's served with a vinegar dipping sauce that has just enough heat. I ate it too fast and burned my mouth. Worth it.

Halo-Halo. For dessert, this is a shaved ice dessert with evaporated milk, sweet beans, coconut strips, ube ice cream, and leche flan. It's a mess in the best way. Every spoonful is different. One bite might be creamy, the next icy, the next sweet. It's the kind of dessert that makes you forget about chocolate lava cake.

The Vibe: Real, Not Manufactured

Here's what I loved most: nothing feels performative. The servers don't recite the story of every dish. The lighting is fluorescent, not warm and dim. The chairs are uncomfortable after an hour. But the energy is electric. Every table is having a good time. People are laughing, sharing food, and ordering more. A group of Filipino grandmothers at the next table insisted I try their sinigang (sour soup). I did. It was incredible.

This is the kind of restaurant that can't exist in Manhattan anymore. The rent is too high, the expectations are too curated. But in a basement in Queens, Maria and her husband have created something real. It's not trying to impress you. It's just trying to feed you. And that's what makes it special.

The Verdict: Go Now

Lutong Pinoy is the best new restaurant I've been to this year. It's not the most polished, not the most elegant, not the most photogenic. But it's the most honest. The food is cooked with love and served without pretense. That's rare in 2026, when every new opening feels like a marketing campaign disguised as a restaurant.

Go before it gets famous. Bring cash. Order the sisig. Thank me later.
