Olive Oil Prices Are Out of Control: 5 Spanish Bottles That Won't Break Your Bank (July 2026)

TR
By Joshua Reed · July 21, 2026 · 7 min read

If you've bought olive oil recently, you've felt the sting. Prices have more than doubled since 2021. Drought in Spain, record heat in Italy, and crop diseases in Greece have created a global shortage. The average price for a liter of extra virgin olive oil hit $15 in the US this month — up from $6 in 2020. I've seen bottles of niche Italian oil going for $40. It's insane.

But here's the thing. You don't need to spend a fortune to get good olive oil. Spain, which produces 40% of the world's olive oil, still has affordable options if you know where to look. I spent a week in Madrid visiting specialty stores, supermarkets, and even a small producer outside Toledo. I bought 12 bottles under $20 and blind-tested them with a local chef. The results were surprising.

Why Spanish Olive Oil Is Your Best Bet Right Now

Spain's olive oil industry has been hammered by drought — the 2024 harvest was the worst in a decade. But here's the thing: Spain produces so much oil that even a bad harvest still leaves plenty of supply. Italy's harvest was even worse (down 30% from 2023), and Greece had its own issues. So Spanish oil is relatively more available and cheaper.

Also, Spanish olive oil regulations are strict. The 'Extra Virgin' label requires acidity below 0.8% and no defects in taste. That's the same as Italian standards. So you're not sacrificing quality by buying Spanish. You're just avoiding the 'Italy tax' — the premium people pay for the Tuscan brand name.

My advice? Look for bottles with a 'DOP' (Denominación de Origen Protegida) label. That means the olives were grown, harvested, and pressed in a specific region. These oils have more character and are worth the extra few dollars. Non-DOP oils are fine for cooking, but for dipping bread or finishing a dish, go DOP.

Best Overall: Oleoestepa DOP Estepa (Aceite de Oliva Virgen Extra)

This was my winner. It's $11 for 500ml at most Spanish grocery stores (exported versions cost around $15). It comes from the Estepa region in Andalusia, known for its intense, peppery oils. This one is made from the Hojiblanca olive variety. The flavor is grassy with a hint of almond and a strong peppery finish that tingles your throat.

I used it for three days — on salads, drizzled over grilled vegetables, and just with bread. Every dish was elevated. The pepperiness cuts through fatty foods (try it on a grilled steak) but doesn't overpower delicate flavors. My chef friend said it reminded him of the oil his grandmother used in the 1990s. High praise.

Why it won: perfect balance of fruitiness and bitterness. Not too mild, not too aggressive. And at $11, it's a steal.

Best Budget: Carbonell Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Carbonell is the generic brand you'll find in every Spanish supermarket. It's $7 for a liter. I expected it to be mediocre. I was wrong. It's not complex — it's a straightforward, mild oil with low bitterness and a soft fruity note. But for cooking, it's perfect. You can use it for sautéing, roasting, and even light frying without feeling like you're wasting money.

The downside? It's not great for finishing. The flavor is too subtle to stand out on a salad or bread. But as an everyday cooking oil, it's the best value I found. Also, Carbonell uses a blend of Picual and Arbequina olives, which gives it a more neutral profile. If you're used to Italian oils, this will taste different — less grassy, more buttery. Give it a chance.

Best for Dipping: Castillo de Canena Royal Picual

This one's $18 for 500ml — the priciest on my list, but worth it if you love dipping bread. It's made from 100% Picual olives from the Jaén region. The flavor is intensely green — think cut grass, artichoke, and a punch of pepper. It's the kind of oil that makes you want to eat an entire loaf of sourdough.

The chef I tested with said this is what he uses in his restaurant for bread service. 'Customers complain when we switch to a cheaper oil,' he told me. It's also great for finishing soups or drizzling over hummus. The bitterness is pronounced but not unpleasant — it's the good kind of bitterness that cleanses your palate.

One warning: this oil is not for cooking. Heat destroys its delicate flavors. Use it raw or at room temperature. And don't hide it in the fridge — olive oil should be stored in a cool, dark pantry, not the refrigerator.

Best Medium Option: Borges Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Borges is another Spanish staple, available for $10 for 750ml. It's a blend of Arbequina and Picual olives, giving it a balanced profile — fruity but with enough bitterness to be interesting. I found it excellent for salad dressings. A simple vinaigrette of Borges oil, sherry vinegar, and salt was perfect on a tomato and basil salad.

What I like about Borges is consistency. I've bought it multiple times over the years, and it always tastes the same. Some small-producer oils vary from batch to batch. Borges is reliable. It's not the most exciting oil, but it's never disappointing. If you're cooking for guests and want something that pleases everyone without breaking the bank, this is your pick.

Best Splurge: Oleícola del Bajo Aragón DOP Bajo Aragón

At $16 for 500ml, this is a splurge. But it's worth it if you want to impress. This oil is from the Bajo Aragón region, known for its Empeltre olives. The flavor is unique — sweet and nutty with a hint of apple. It's completely different from the grassy, peppery oils from Andalusia. Think of it as the Pinot Noir of olive oils — more subtle and elegant.

I used it on a simple caprese salad with fresh mozzarella and basil. The oil's sweetness complemented the tomatoes perfectly. It would also be amazing on grilled fish or roasted potatoes. The chef said he'd use it for desserts — drizzled over vanilla ice cream or pound cake. I haven't tried that yet, but I'm intrigued.

The downside? It's harder to find. I bought mine at a specialty shop in Madrid. In the US, you might need to order online. But if you can find it, it's a revelation.

How to Store and Use Olive Oil (So You Don't Waste Money)

Since olive oil is expensive, you want to make it last. Here are my tips. First, buy smaller bottles. A liter seems economical, but if you take two months to finish it, the quality degrades. Buy 500ml bottles and use them within a month of opening.

Second, store it correctly. Keep it away from heat and light. A dark pantry is ideal. Don't store it next to the stove — the heat accelerates oxidation. And don't refrigerate it. Cold makes the oil cloudy and thick, and the flavor suffers.

Third, use it wisely. Save your expensive extra virgin for finishing — drizzling over finished dishes, dipping bread, or making vinaigrettes. For cooking, use a cheaper oil like Carbonell. That way, you get the best of both worlds without burning money.

Finally, buy in bulk from producers if you can. Some Spanish producers offer subscription services. I found one — Oleoestepa — that ships directly to the US for $30 per liter, including shipping. That's cheaper than buying retail. Check their website. Many small producers are eager for direct customers.

The Bottom Line

Olive oil prices aren't coming down anytime soon. The drought in Spain is expected to persist through 2027. But you don't have to suffer. Spanish olive oil offers incredible value if you know what to look for. Skip the fancy Italian brands. Grab a bottle of Oleoestepa or Carbonell. Your wallet — and your taste buds — will thank you.

I'm going to keep a bottle of the Castillo de Canena on my counter for bread dipping. It's my new guilty pleasure. At $18, it's a splurge, but it's cheaper than therapy. And it makes my Monday night pasta taste like a Friday night dinner.
