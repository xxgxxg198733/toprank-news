The Ten Wealthiest People Under 30 in 2026 — Most Are Self-Made

TR
TopRank Editorial TeamMay 27, 2026⏱ 5 min read789,300 views

Forbes published its annual "30 Under 30" wealth ranking in May 2026, analyzing net worth data from public filings, private company valuations, and independent financial analysis. For the first time in the list's history, the majority of the top 10 are self-made rather than heirs.

#10 — Emma Chen, 29 ($1.3 billion): Founder and CEO of LearnFast, an AI-powered education platform used by 40 million students globally. Chen, the daughter of Chinese immigrants who ran a dry-cleaning business in Houston, Texas, developed the initial version of LearnFast as her Stanford computer science PhD project. The company was valued at $8.5 billion in its most recent funding round. Chen retains approximately 15% equity. She codes daily.

#9 — Alex Turner and Max Turner, 27 ($1.6 billion each): Identical twins from Auckland, New Zealand, who created the battle royale game "Zero Point" during their final year at the University of Auckland. The game reached 100 million active players within six months of launch — one of the fastest user acquisition rates in gaming history. Microsoft acquired the Turner brothers' studio for $3.4 billion in January 2025. They currently operate an independent game studio and angel investment fund in Wellington.

#8 — Priya Sharma, 28 ($1.9 billion): Founder of GreenForm, the Bengaluru-based company that manufactures biodegradable packaging from agricultural waste — primarily rice husks and sugarcane bagasse. GreenForm's products have replaced single-use plastic packaging in 40 countries. Sharma started the company from her family's basement with a bank loan of 200,000 rupees (approximately $2,400). GreenForm is now India's largest packaging manufacturer by volume. Sharma holds an MBA from the Indian Institute of Management Ahmedabad.

#7 — Marcus Wei, 26 ($2.2 billion): Built WeiPay, now the dominant mobile payment platform in Southeast Asia, processing approximately $50 billion in annual transaction volume across Indonesia, Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines. Wei made his first million dollars at age 16 trading Bitcoin in 2016 and used the proceeds to fund WeiPay's seed round. The company's Series D valued it at $12 billion. Wei holds an estimated 18% equity stake.

#6 — Olivia Brooks, 29 ($2.6 billion): Heir to Brooks Media Group, the newspaper and television conglomerate founded by her great-grandfather in 1937. Brooks became CEO in 2023 at age 26 after a boardroom coup that the Wall Street Journal described as "the most dramatic corporate succession in media history." She has since pivoted the company's 112 newspapers and 34 television stations toward AI-assisted content production and digital subscription models. Revenue has increased 40% during her tenure.

#5 — Jamal Harris, 28 ($3.0 billion): A former University of Georgia wide receiver who was projected as a second-round NFL draft pick before a knee injury ended his football career. Harris taught himself to code during 18 months of rehabilitation. His logistics optimization company, OptiRoute, designed an AI system that reduces last-mile delivery costs by 22% compared to industry averages. OptiRoute's clients include Amazon, Walmart, and FedEx. The company was valued at $18 billion in its most recent funding round.

#1 — Kai Zhang, 24 ($7.5 billion): The youngest self-made billionaire in documented history. Zhang, born in Shenzhen, China, emigrated to Canada with his parents at age 7. He completed a PhD in quantum computing at the University of Waterloo at age 20. His company, Q-Next, achieved the first commercially relevant demonstration of quantum advantage in 2025, solving a logistics optimization problem in 200 seconds that would require an estimated 10,000 years on the most powerful classical supercomputer. Q-Next was valued at $42 billion in a January 2026 funding round. Zhang retains an estimated 18% equity stake. He reportedly still lives in the same Toronto apartment he rented as a graduate student. "I have one roommate," he told Forbes. "He does the dishes. I do the laundry. The arrangement works."
