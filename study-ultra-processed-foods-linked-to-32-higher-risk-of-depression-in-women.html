Study: Ultra-Processed Foods Linked to 32% Higher Risk of Depression in Women

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 71,230 views

A major new study from the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, published today in JAMA Internal Medicine, has found a strong link between the consumption of ultra-processed foods (UPFs) and an increased risk of depression in women. The research, which followed over 100,000 female nurses for 15 years, adds to a growing body of evidence about the harmful effects of modern diets.

Key Findings

Women in the highest quintile of UPF consumption (averaging 8 or more servings per day) had a 32% higher risk of developing depression compared to those in the lowest quintile (3 or fewer servings per day). The association was particularly strong for artificially sweetened beverages and processed meats. 'We saw a clear dose-response relationship: the more UPFs women ate, the higher their risk,' said lead author Dr. Andrew Chan, a professor of medicine at Harvard. The study controlled for factors like age, weight, physical activity, smoking, and overall diet quality.

Potential Mechanisms

The researchers suggest several ways that UPFs may contribute to depression. First, these foods are often high in unhealthy fats, sugars, and additives that can trigger chronic low-grade inflammation, which is linked to depression. Second, artificial sweeteners, in particular, may alter the gut microbiome, disrupting the production of mood-regulating neurotransmitters like serotonin. 'The gut-brain axis is a critical pathway,' said Dr. Chan. 'When we feed our gut unhealthy food, it affects our mental health.' Third, the lack of fiber and nutrients in UPFs may impair brain function over time.

Practical Advice

The study's authors recommend that women (and men) limit their intake of ultra-processed foods, which include things like soda, packaged snacks, frozen meals, processed meats, and many breakfast cereals. Instead, they should focus on a diet rich in whole foods, such as fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats. 'This is not about perfection, but about making better choices more often,' said Dr. Chan. The findings come as a growing number of countries, including the UK and Brazil, are updating their dietary guidelines to explicitly warn against ultra-processed foods. For now, a simple rule of thumb: if it has more than five ingredients or ingredients you can't pronounce, it's probably best to leave it on the shelf.
