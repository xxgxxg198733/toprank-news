Ten Remarkable Travel Destinations That Most Tourists Overlook — Recommended by Travel Writers

TR
TopRank Editorial TeamMay 30, 2026⏱ 4 min read167,300 views

Condé Nast Traveler surveyed 40 travel journalists and photographers in early 2026, asking each to nominate one destination they believe deserves far more visitors than it receives. From 40 nominations, the editors selected 10 that represent a range of geographies, budgets, and interests.

#1 — Lofoten Islands, Norway (September): Almost all visitors to Lofoten arrive in June and July for the midnight sun. September delivers equally dramatic landscapes — jagged peaks rising directly from the Norwegian Sea, red fishing huts (rorbuer) on stilts over turquoise water — plus northern lights, empty trails, and accommodation prices 40-60% lower than peak season. The water temperature averages 12°C (54°F), cold but swimmable for the determined. Photographer Mia Karlsson, who has shot Lofoten in every season for National Geographic, told Condé Nast Traveler: "September is when Lofoten looks like Lofoten in your imagination — moody, dramatic, empty of people, full of light."

#2 — Isfahan, Iran: The Naqsh-e Jahan Square, built by Shah Abbas I between 1598 and 1629, is one of the largest public squares on Earth — 560 meters long by 160 meters wide, surrounded by the Imam Mosque, Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque, and Ali Qapu Palace, all covered in tilework that shifts color from turquoise to cobalt as the sun moves across the sky. Travel writer Rolf Potts wrote in his nomination: "Isfahan is the most beautiful city most Westerners have never heard of. The architecture rivals anything in Italy. The food — saffron rice, lamb kebabs, rosewater pastries — is extraordinary. And the hospitality culture, encoded in the Persian concept of taarof, means you may be invited to a stranger's home for dinner within hours of arriving."

#3 — The Gobi Desert, Mongolia (September-October): The Gobi is not the endless sand dunes that the word "desert" suggests — it is primarily a cold desert of rock, gravel, and sparse grassland, covering 500,000 square miles of southern Mongolia and northern China. Visitors stay in traditional ger camps operated by nomadic families. Activities include riding Bactrian camels (the two-humped kind), hiking the Flaming Cliffs (where Roy Chapman Andrews discovered the first dinosaur eggs known to science in 1923), and experiencing silence on a scale that is almost impossible to find in the developed world. A 10-day guided trip costs approximately $1,200-$1,800 including all meals and accommodations.

#4 — Salento, Colombia: While international tourists crowd into Cartagena's colonial streets, Salento — a small town in Colombia's coffee region (Eje Cafetero) — offers colorful colonial architecture, working coffee farms that offer tours for approximately $5, and immediate access to the Cocora Valley, home to the Quindío wax palm (Ceroxylon quindiuense), the tallest palm tree in the world, which can reach heights of 60 meters (200 feet). The trees grow only in this specific region of Colombia. A traditional meal of bandeja paisa in a local restaurant costs approximately $6-8.

#5 — The Albanian Riviera: The stretch of Albanian coastline between Vlorë and Sarandë features the same turquoise Ionian Sea as the Greek island of Corfu — visible across 15 miles of water — at a fraction of the price. Beachfront hotel rooms in the town of Himarë cost $30-45 per night in high season. The seafood is caught the same morning it's served. The Roman ruins at Butrint, a UNESCO World Heritage site occupied continuously from the 7th century BCE through the Ottoman period, receive perhaps 1% as many visitors as comparable sites in Italy or Greece. "Albania is what Croatia was 20 years ago, before the cruise ships found it," wrote travel journalist Elizabeth Becker.
