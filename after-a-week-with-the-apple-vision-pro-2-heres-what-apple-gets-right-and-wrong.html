After a Week With the Apple Vision Pro 2, Here's What Apple Gets Right (and Wrong)

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 24, 2026 · 5 min read

I remember the first time I tried the original Apple Vision Pro in early 2024. I put it on, looked around my living room, and felt like I was inside a sci-fi movie. Then I took it off after 30 minutes because my neck hurt and the battery died. It was incredible technology that nobody actually wanted to use for more than a demo.

Fast forward to July 2026. Apple just released the Vision Pro 2 last Monday, July 20. I got my hands on a review unit (yes, I had to sign an NDA the size of a small novel). I've been wearing it every day for a week — working, watching movies, playing games, and even trying to cook with it (don't recommend). Here's what's actually different, what's still broken, and whether you should spend $2,999.

The Weight Problem Is Mostly Solved

The original Vision Pro weighed about 650 grams. That's heavy. After an hour, you felt it. After two hours, you wanted it off. The Vision Pro 2 drops to 510 grams. That's a 21% reduction. It doesn't sound like much on paper, but on your face, it's a completely different experience.

Apple did this by switching to a magnesium frame instead of aluminum and using a thinner, lighter battery pack that sits on the back strap like a counterweight. The whole thing feels better balanced. I wore it for three hours straight on Tuesday watching Dune: Part Three (yes, it's out, and yes, it's amazing in this format). My neck was fine. My face had red marks, but that's unavoidable.

The new Solo Knit Band 2.0 is also wider and more breathable. Less sweaty. If you had the original and complained about comfort, this version fixes most of it.

The Screen Is Still Unreal

The micro-OLED displays are the same resolution — about 4K per eye — but Apple improved the brightness and contrast. HDR content looks genuinely better than on my LG OLED TV. Colors pop. Blacks are truly black. When I watched a night scene in a movie, I couldn't tell where the screen ended and the real world began.

Apple also fixed the field of view issue. The original had a narrow FOV — like looking through binoculars. The Vision Pro 2 widens it by about 15 degrees. It's not perfect. You still see slight vignetting at the edges. But it's much less distracting.

Battery Life: Still a Problem, But Less of One

The original Vision Pro got about 2 hours of mixed use. Pathetic. The Vision Pro 2 gets about 3.5 hours. Better, but still not enough. If you want to watch a full movie, you're cutting it close. If you want to work for a full day, forget it.

There's a new external battery pack that clips to your belt. It's smaller than the original but still annoying. I don't want a wire running from my face to my waist. Apple says they're working on a wireless version for the third generation, but that's probably 2-3 years away.

I've been using it plugged in most of the time. That works, but it defeats the purpose of a spatial computer you can move around with.

visionOS 3: The Software Actually Feels Finished Now

The original visionOS was impressive but buggy. Apps crashed. Window placement was janky. The keyboard was a nightmare. visionOS 3, which launched with the new hardware, fixes almost all of that.

Multitasking works now. You can have Safari, Messages, and a video playing simultaneously without lag. The virtual keyboard is still worse than a real one, but voice typing has improved dramatically. I wrote half of this article using Dictation in Vision Pro, and it got my words right about 95% of the time.

Apple also added a feature called Spatial Persona 2.0. In FaceTime calls, your avatar looks more like you — it tracks your facial expressions better and even mimics your hand gestures. I called my mom on it. She said it was creepy but also cool. That's the Vision Pro experience in a nutshell.

Apps: The Ecosystem Is Growing, Slowly

There are now over 2,000 native visionOS apps. That's up from about 1,000 at launch. Netflix finally has a native app (it didn't for the original). Microsoft Office works well. Adobe Lightroom is surprisingly usable for photo editing.

But the killer app is still immersion. Watching movies in a virtual theater — where you sit in a digital recreation of a classic cinema — is magical. I watched part of the new Apple TV+ series "Foundation: Reimagined" in a simulated IMAX theater. I forgot I was wearing a headset.

Gaming is better but not great. Apple Arcade has some spatial games, but they're casual. No AAA titles yet. No Call of Duty or Assassin's Creed. If you want to game, this isn't the device.

What Still Sucks

Let me be honest. Three things still drive me crazy.

First, the price. $2,999 is insane. You could buy a MacBook Pro, an iPad Pro, and an iPhone for that. Apple is betting on early adopters and professionals. But for regular people, this is a luxury toy.

Second, the isolation. When you're wearing the Vision Pro, you're cut off from the world. The passthrough cameras are good, but they're not perfect. You can't make eye contact. You can't share the experience easily. Apple tried to fix this with the new SharePlay feature, where another person can see what you see on their iPad. But it's not the same as being together.

Third, the setup. You need to scan your face with an iPhone, adjust the fit, calibrate the eye tracking. It takes 15 minutes to set up. If you share the headset with someone else, you have to do it all over again. That's a pain.

Should You Buy It?

If you're a developer, a designer, or someone who works in 3D modeling or architecture, yes. This is a legit productivity tool. The ability to have multiple virtual monitors anywhere in your space is amazing. I can see architects using this to walk through building plans in full scale.

If you're a movie buff with $3,000 to burn, also yes. The movie-watching experience is the best I've ever had outside a real theater.

If you're a normal person who wants to browse the web and check email, no. Buy a MacBook Air for a third of the price. The Vision Pro 2 is still a niche product. It's the best niche product I've ever used, but it's not ready for everyone.

I'm sending mine back next week. But I'll miss it. And I'll probably buy the third version.
