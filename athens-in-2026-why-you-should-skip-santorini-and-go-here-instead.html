Athens in 2026: Why You Should Skip Santorini and Go Here Instead

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 20, 2026 · 4 min read

I Love Santorini, But I Can't Recommend It Anymore

Let me start by saying I'm not a travel snob. I love a good tourist trap as much as anyone. But I visited Santorini in June 2026, and it was a nightmare. Cruise ships disgorging thousands of people. Selfie sticks everywhere. Hotels charging $1,000 a night for a room with a view of a construction site. I left after two days and fled to Athens, which I hadn't visited since 2019. And I couldn't believe what I'd been missing. Athens in 2026 is having a moment. And it's the better Greek vacation.

What's Changed in Athens?

A lot. The city has poured serious money into infrastructure. The new metro line, finished in 2024, connects the airport to the center in 30 minutes. The pedestrianized streets around the Acropolis are gorgeous—no cars, just people walking and cafés spilling onto the pavement. The archaeological museum got a massive renovation. But the biggest change is the energy. There's a new generation of young Greeks opening cocktail bars, pop-up restaurants, and art galleries. It feels like Berlin in the 2000s, but with better weather and way better food.

I stayed in the Koukaki neighborhood, right below the Acropolis. It used to be a quiet residential area. Now it's full of cool little wine bars and shops. I had a dinner at a place called Oinos (meaning "wine" in Greek) where the chef serves a seven-course tasting menu for €45. It was some of the best food I've ever eaten—fresh fish, wild greens, local cheese, and olive oil that tasted like liquid gold. The wine list was all Greek, with bottles from islands I'd never heard of. I spent three hours there.

The Crowds Are Manageable

Here's the thing about Athens in July: it's busy, but not insane. The Acropolis gets crowded in the morning, but if you go at 5 PM, just before closing, it's almost empty. I walked up there at sunset and had the place almost to myself. The Parthenon glowed gold. I sat on a rock and just stared for 20 minutes. No selfie sticks. No tour groups. Just me and 2,500 years of history.

Compare that to Santorini, where I had to wait 40 minutes to get a photo on the famous blue-domed church steps. And there was a line. For a photo. On a staircase. That's not a vacation. That's a photoshoot gig.

The Food Scene Is Unreal

I'm a food person, so this matters to me. Athens has always had good food, but the current scene is next-level. The central market (Varvakeios) is a sensory overload: fish, meat, spices, olives. I had a breakfast of fresh bread, feta, and tomatoes from a stall, and it cost €3. For dinner, I went to a place called Nolan, which does modern Greek-Asian fusion. It sounds weird, but it works. The chef uses Greek ingredients—yogurt, honey, lamb—but cooks them over Japanese charcoal. The lamb kleftiko with miso was the best thing I ate all trip.

And the street food! I had souvlaki from a place called Kostas in the Monastiraki flea market. It's been there since 1950. The pork was smoky, the pita was soft, and they put french fries inside. It cost €2.50. I ate three.

The Downsides (Because Nothing Is Perfect)

Athens isn't without issues. It's hot in July—like, 95°F hot. And it's noisy. The traffic is chaotic, though the new pedestrian zones help. Some neighborhoods feel a bit gritty, but that's also part of the charm. And if you're looking for a beach vacation, Athens isn't that. The coast has beaches, but they're not Santorini-level. You need to take a ferry to the nearby islands (Hydra, Spetses) for that.

But honestly? I'd take Athens' gritty authenticity over Santorini's polished Instagram facade any day. Athens feels real. People live there. They go to work, they argue in cafés, they laugh loudly. It's messy and alive.

My Final Verdict

Skip Santorini. At least for now. The crowds are ruining it. Go to Athens. Spend a week. Eat everything. Walk everywhere. Drink retsina at a rooftop bar. Watch the sunset from Lycabettus Hill. And if you really want island vibes, take a day trip to Hydra—it's an hour by hydrofoil, no cars allowed, and it's stunning.

I'm already planning my return. Athens in 2026 is the underdog that finally won. Don't sleep on it.
