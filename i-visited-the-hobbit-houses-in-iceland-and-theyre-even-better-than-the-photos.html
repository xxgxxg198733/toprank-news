I Visited the 'Hobbit Houses' in Iceland — And They're Even Better Than the Photos

TR
By Sarah Mitchell · July 23, 2026 · 4 min read

I first saw photos of the "Hobbit Houses" of Iceland on Reddit about a year ago. They looked like something out of a fantasy movie—turf-roofed cottages built into the hillsides, with moss growing on the roofs and steam rising from nearby hot springs. People in the comments were calling them the most unique accommodation in Europe. I had to see for myself.

So in June, I booked a three-night stay at the Þjórsárdalur Valley Lodges, a collection of twelve underground houses built by architect Sólveig Jónsdóttir. They're not actually official Hobbit houses—that's just what tourists call them because of the round doors and grassy roofs. But the experience is every bit as magical as the Lord of the Rings movies.

Getting There Is Part of the Adventure

First, a warning: these lodges are not easy to reach. They're located in the Þjórsárdalur valley, about a two-hour drive from Reykjavík on paved roads, then another 45 minutes on gravel. Our rental car handled it fine, but I wouldn't try it in a sedan without all-wheel drive. The last 10 kilometers are on a rough road that winds through lava fields and past waterfalls.

But that's part of the charm. You feel like you're discovering something secret. When we finally pulled up to the valley, the lodges were almost invisible—just grassy mounds blending into the landscape. The only giveaway was the soft glow of lantern light coming from the round wooden doors.

The Lodge Itself

I stayed in Lodge 7, which is one of the larger ones. The door is exactly like you'd imagine—a heavy, round wooden door with iron hinges. You have to duck to enter, and inside, it's surprisingly spacious. The walls are made of stone and turf, which keeps the temperature perfect—cool in the summer and warm in the winter. There's a small kitchen, a wood-burning stove, a comfortable bed, and a bathroom with a compost toilet.

No electricity. That's the thing that surprised me most. The lodges are intentionally off-grid. There's no Wi-Fi, no TV, no outlets. The only light comes from candles and a single propane lantern. At first, I thought it would be annoying. But after the first night, I realized how nice it is to disconnect completely.

At sunset, I sat outside on the wooden bench and watched the light change over the valley. There were no sounds except the wind and the distant rush of a waterfall. My phone was dead in my bag and I didn't care.

The Hot Springs

One of the biggest draws of the valley is the natural hot springs. There are three pools within walking distance of the lodges, each at a different temperature. The largest one, called Gamla Laugin (Old Pool), is about 38°C—perfect for soaking. I went every evening after hiking, and it was the most relaxing thing I've ever done.

The water is rich in silica, which leaves your skin feeling incredibly soft. A word of warning: it also stains your swimsuit a faint orange color. Don't wear anything you're attached to.

What to Do in the Area

During the day, the valley is a paradise for hikers. There are trails ranging from easy walks to challenging climbs. The most popular is the hike to Hjálparfoss, a two-tiered waterfall about 30 minutes from the lodges. It's stunning—especially in the early morning when the sun hits the mist and creates rainbows.

I also did the hike up Mount Hekla, the nearby volcano that last erupted in 2023. It's a strenuous four-hour climb, but the view from the top is worth every drop of sweat. You can see the entire valley and the glacier beyond.

If you're not into hiking, there's a farm nearby that sells local cheese and lamb. The farmer, a woman named Björk, invited us in for coffee and told us stories about living in the valley for 40 years. That kind of experience is priceless.

The Downsides

Look, I loved my stay, but I have to be honest about the downsides. First, the price. These lodges are not cheap—about $450 per night in peak season. That's steep for a place without electricity. Second, the off-grid aspect means no hot shower. The lodges have a cold-water tap and you can heat water on the stove, but it's a process. If you're used to luxury, this might not be for you.

Also, the compost toilet. It's not as bad as you'd think—it's well-ventilated and doesn't smell—but it's still a compost toilet. Something to consider if you're squeamish.

Should You Go?

If you're looking for a truly unique experience and you're willing to trade comfort for connection with nature, then yes, absolutely. The Hobbit Houses of Iceland are worth every dollar. But go in with realistic expectations. This is not a glamping resort. It's a back-to-basics experience that happens to be in one of the most beautiful places on Earth.

I left feeling rested in a way I haven't felt in years. Maybe it was the lack of screens. Maybe it was the hot springs. Maybe it was just being somewhere so quiet and beautiful. Whatever it was, I'm already planning my return trip.
