10 Things Nobody Tells You About Santorini (But You Really Need to Know)

TR
By James Rodriguez · July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Let me start with a confession: I almost didn't go to Santorini. I'd seen the photos a thousand times—those whitewashed buildings with blue domes, the sunsets that look like they're painted by a god with a really good filter. I figured it was just another overhyped tourist trap. But my girlfriend insisted, and honestly, I'm glad she did. But not for the reasons you'd expect.

I spent two weeks there in late June 2026, bouncing between Fira, Oia, and a few smaller villages that most tourists skip. And yeah, the place is stunning. But there's a lot that nobody tells you before you book that flight. So here's my list of 10 things I wish I'd known—some of them good, some of them annoying, all of them true.

1. The Sunsets Are a Circus, Not a Moment

Everyone talks about the Santorini sunset like it's a religious experience. And sure, from a certain angle, with the right lighting, it's beautiful. But here's what actually happens: about an hour before sunset, every tourist in Oia starts shuffling toward the castle ruins like they're in a zombie movie. They stand shoulder-to-shoulder, phones raised, blocking every possible view. Kids are crying. Someone's elbow is in your ribs. And the whole thing lasts maybe five minutes before everyone claps and leaves a trail of empty water bottles.

If you want a real sunset experience, skip Oia entirely. Walk to the far end of Fira, find a quiet bench near the cable car station, and watch it from there. You'll get the same colors without the crowd. Or better yet, book a dinner at a taverna in Pyrgos, which has a view that's almost as good and about 90% fewer people.

2. The Water Isn't What You're Imagining

Santorini is a volcanic island. That means the beaches are not soft white sand—they're black, red, or dark brown pebbles. And I mean pebbles. Walking into the water at Perissa Beach feels like doing a foot reflexology session with a sadist. You'll want water shoes. I'm not kidding. Buy them before you go, because the ones sold at the beach are overpriced and fall apart after two dips.

The water itself is clear and beautiful, but it gets deep fast. There's no gentle slope like you'd find in the Caribbean. You take three steps and suddenly you're swimming. Also, there's no natural shade anywhere. Bring an umbrella or rent a lounge chair, because the sun is relentless.

3. Donkeys Are Not a Fun Mode of Transport

You've seen the photos of people riding donkeys up the steps from the old port in Fira. Looks charming, right? It's not. The steps are steep, uneven, and covered in donkey droppings. The animals look miserable—and they are. There have been multiple reports of donkeys collapsing from heat exhaustion in recent summers. The local government has been talking about banning the practice, but as of 2026, it's still happening.

Just take the cable car. It costs six euros, takes three minutes, and doesn't involve any animal suffering. If the line is long (and it will be), walk down the steps but don't ride. Your conscience will thank you.

4. Fira vs. Oia: Pick Your Vibe

Everyone wants to stay in Oia because that's where the famous blue-domed churches are. But Oia is tiny, expensive, and packed from 10 AM to sunset. If you're there for luxury and don't mind crowds, sure, go for it. But I'd argue Fira is a better base. It's bigger, has more restaurants, better nightlife, and a fraction of the tourist density. Plus, the views from Fira's cliffside paths are just as good, if not better in some spots.

If you really want a unique experience, stay in Imerovigli. It's a quieter village between Fira and Oia with some of the most insane caldera views I've ever seen. Fewer crowds, more peace. That's where I'd go next time.

5. The Food Is Good, but Know Where to Eat

Santorini has some legitimately amazing food—tomato keftedes, fava me kreithraki, fresh seafood. But the restaurants with the best views are usually the worst for food. They know you're paying for the sunset, not the meal. One place in Oia charged me 28 euros for a plate of spaghetti that tasted like it came from a box. The view was incredible. The pasta was a crime.

For real food, head to the inland villages like Pyrgos, Megalochori, or even the capital of Fira's back streets. Taverna O Mple, a small spot in Akrotiri, had the best grilled octopus of my life. Cost me 12 euros. The view was of a parking lot. Zero regrets.

6. The Wine Scene Is Legit

Santorini has one of the oldest wine traditions in Europe, thanks to its volcanic soil and a unique grape called Assyrtiko. The island's wineries produce crisp, mineral-driven whites that are perfect for hot summer days. I did a tasting at Vassaltis Vineyards, which is relatively new (opened in 2015), and their Assyrtiko was genuinely world-class—bright, citrusy, with a salinity that paired perfectly with the local seafood.

The best part? Most wineries offer tastings for around 15-20 euros, and they're much less crowded than the beach spots. Book a tour with Santorini Wine Adventure, which picks you up from your hotel and drives you to three or four wineries. It was the best money I spent on the island.

7. The Crowds Are Getting Worse Every Year

In 2024, Santorini welcomed over 3.4 million visitors. The island's permanent population is about 15,000. Do the math. The main streets of Fira and Oia during peak season are essentially a human river. You can't walk at your own pace—you just shuffle along with everyone else. The Greek government has been discussing caps on cruise ship arrivals, and in 2025 they actually imposed a limit of 8,000 cruise passengers per day, but that's still a lot of people.

If you can, visit in shoulder season—May, early June, or September. The weather is still beautiful, the water is swimmable, and you won't want to throw yourself off a cliff by day three.

8. Getting Around Is a Pain

Public buses exist, but they're unreliable and packed. Taxis are scarce and expensive—a ride from the airport to Fira will cost you around 25-30 euros for a 10-minute trip. Renting a car is the best option, but the roads are narrow, parking is a nightmare, and everyone drives like they're in a Fast & Furious sequel.

I rented an ATV (quad bike) for four days and it was the perfect solution. Cost me 35 euros per day, I could park anywhere, and I could explore the island at my own pace. Just be careful—the roads can be treacherous, especially at night. And wear a helmet, even if you look like a dork.

9. The Beaches Are Overrated (Except One)

I already mentioned the pebbles, but the beaches themselves are just... fine. Perissa and Kamari are long black sand beaches with plenty of tavernas and loungers, but they're not stunning. Red Beach is famous but crowded and closed sometimes due to rockfall risk. White Beach is only accessible by boat and is tiny.

The exception is Koloumbes Beach, near Oia. It's a small cove with crystal clear water and far fewer tourists. There's no infrastructure—no loungers, no restaurants—so bring your own supplies. But it's the closest thing to a hidden gem on an island that doesn't really have secrets anymore.

10. The Magic Is Still There, But You Have to Work for It

Here's the thing about Santorini: it can feel like a theme park version of itself. The Instagram filters, the influencer crowds, the relentless commercialization—it's all real. But underneath that, there's still a genuine place with genuine beauty. If you're willing to wake up at 6 AM to watch the sunrise without the crowds, walk the less-traveled paths, and eat where the locals eat, you'll find it.

One morning, I walked from Fira to Imerovigli before dawn. The only sounds were my footsteps and the distant clanging of a church bell. The caldera was glowing pink and orange in the early light. For those twenty minutes, Santorini was exactly what the postcards promise. And that's why, despite all the headaches, I'd still recommend it—just go in with your eyes open.
