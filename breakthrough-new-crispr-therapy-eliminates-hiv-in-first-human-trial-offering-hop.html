Breakthrough: New CRISPR Therapy Eliminates HIV in First Human Trial, Offering Hope of a Cure

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 225,352 views

In a stunning medical breakthrough, a new CRISPR-based therapy has completely eliminated detectable HIV from the blood of three patients in a Phase I clinical trial. The results, published on June 3, 2026, in the New England Journal of Medicine, have been hailed as 'historic' and bring the world closer to a cure for AIDS, a disease that has claimed over 40 million lives.

How the Therapy Works

The therapy, developed by biotech firm Editas Medicine in collaboration with the University of California, San Francisco, uses CRISPR-Cas9 to cut the HIV DNA integrated into a patient's own immune cells. The treatment involves extracting T-cells from the patient, editing them to remove the virus, and then reinfusing them. Unlike standard antiretroviral therapy (ART), which suppresses the virus but never eliminates it, this approach aims to eradicate it entirely.

The Trial Results

The trial enrolled 10 patients with well-controlled HIV on ART. Three of the patients saw a complete elimination of detectable HIV in their blood and lymph nodes for 12 weeks after stopping ART—a period known as 'viral rebound.' The other seven patients experienced a rebound but at much lower levels than typical. 'We are cautiously optimistic,' said lead researcher Dr. Jennifer Wu. 'This is the first time we've seen sustained viral suppression without medication in any patient.' Side effects were limited to mild flu-like symptoms.

What This Means for the Future

While the trial is small, the implications are enormous. An estimated 38 million people live with HIV globally, most in low- and middle-income countries where ART is expensive and adherence difficult. A one-time curative therapy could transform global health. However, challenges remain: the therapy must be proven in larger trials, and it costs an estimated $500,000 per patient. Editas plans to expand the trial to 100 patients by 2027. For now, the three cured patients are a symbol of hope—and a testament to the power of gene editing.
