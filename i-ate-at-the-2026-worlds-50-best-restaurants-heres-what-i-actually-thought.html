I Ate at the 2026 World's 50 Best Restaurants – Here's What I Actually Thought

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 17, 2026 · 5 min read

Eating My Way Through the List

When the World's 50 Best Restaurants list dropped in June 2026, I knew I had to try some of them. I'm not a professional food critic – just a guy who loves good food and has a travel budget. So I planned a trip that hit four of the top restaurants: Disfrutar in Barcelona (#1), Table by Bruno Verjus in Paris (#3), Atomix in New York (#8), and a controversial pick, Noma in Copenhagen (#23). I spent about two months eating my way through them, and I have thoughts. Strong ones. Let me walk you through each experience, from the sublime to the overhyped.

First, a caveat: these are tasting menus, ranging from $250 to $600 per person. That's a lot of money for a meal. I'm aware of that privilege. But if you're curious about whether the hype is real, here's my honest take. I paid for all meals myself. No freebies.

Disfrutar (#1) – Barcelona, Spain

Disfrutar has been climbing the ranks for years, and this year they took the top spot. I was skeptical – can a three-Michelin-star restaurant in Barcelona really be better than everything else? The answer is yes, but not in the way you'd expect. The meal was a 28-course journey that lasted four hours. And I loved almost every minute. The chefs, the trio who trained under Ferran Adrià at elBulli, focus on playful, whimsical dishes that look like art. There's a course called "Olive" that looks exactly like a green olive but is actually a frozen sphere of gazpacho. It bursts in your mouth. Another course is a "truffle sandwich" that's essentially a pillowy brioche filled with liquid truffle. It's ridiculous. My favorite was a dish of smoked eel with caviar and a gel of apple – the combination of smoky, salty, and sweet was perfect.

But here's the thing: it's not for everyone. Some courses are more about concept than flavor. A few were just weird (I didn't love the fermented shrimp). The service is impeccable but formal. If you want a fun, casual night out, this is not it. But if you want to see what's possible with food, Disfrutar is a must. I'd give it a 9.5/10. The only reason it's not 10 is that I left still a bit hungry. 28 courses but tiny portions. I grabbed a burger afterwards. Don't judge me.

Table by Bruno Verjus (#3) – Paris, France

This was the biggest surprise. Table is a small restaurant near the Louvre, with only 12 seats. Bruno Verjus is a former food critic (!) turned chef, and his philosophy is simplicity. No foams, no gels, no tricks. Just perfect ingredients cooked perfectly. The menu changes daily based on what's at the market. I had a course of langoustines with a simple beurre blanc that was the best seafood I've ever eaten. The langoustines were sweet, tender, and cooked to a translucent perfection. There was a pigeon dish with a sauce made from its own juices – it was rich, gamey, and unforgettable. The meal was only 12 courses, but I was full and happy. The vibe is relaxed – Bruno himself might pour your wine. It's the antithesis of Disfrutar. Which is better? It's like comparing a symphony to a jazz trio. Both are brilliant. Table gets a 9.8/10. I'd go back tomorrow if I could.

Atomix (#8) – New York City, USA

Atomix is the darling of the New York food scene. It's a Korean-inspired tasting menu with a focus on fermentation and texture. The restaurant is sleek, with a marble counter and mood lighting. The meal started strong: a course of abalone with seaweed butter was divine. Then came a dish of beef tartare wrapped in perilla leaf – the flavors were bold and bright. But somewhere around course 8, it started to feel repetitive. Too many dishes with the same fermented notes. The dessert course was a letdown – a bland soy milk panna cotta that I didn't finish. The service was also too serious. The staff explained each dish in great detail, but it felt like a lecture. I want to enjoy my food, not take a class. Atomix is good, but I don't get the hype. I'd give it a 7.5/10. For the price ($350), I expected more. Maybe I caught them on an off night, but I left disappointed.

Noma (#23) – Copenhagen, Denmark

Noma is a legend. René Redzepi's restaurant has been called the best in the world multiple times. But in 2026, it's ranked #23, and I think that's fair. I visited during their seafood season, which focuses on shellfish and Nordic ingredients. The meal was an adventure. We started with a snack of fermented shrimp on a cracker – it was funky and challenging. Then came a course of mussels cooked in seaweed butter, which was incredible. The signature dish was a whole lobster grilled over embers, served with a dashi of its own shells. It was messy, primal, and delicious. But there were also misses. A course of raw scallops with pickled elderflower was too acidic. A dessert of berries with pine needle ice cream was weirdly bitter. The vibe at Noma is more relaxed than I expected – the staff is young and enthusiastic. But the price is insane: $600 per person, plus wine pairing. I'm glad I went, but I wouldn't go again. It's a once-in-a-lifetime experience that's not worth repeating. I'd give it an 8/10.

Final Verdict

So, which restaurant is actually the best? For me, it's Table by Bruno Verjus. It's not flashy, but the food is perfect. Disfrutar is a close second if you want a show. Atomix and Noma are good but overpriced. The World's 50 Best list is a great guide, but don't treat it as gospel. Your personal taste matters more. If you're planning a food trip, I'd say skip Noma and Atomix, and go to Disfrutar and Table. You'll save money and have a better time. Or just find a great local spot for a fraction of the price. Sometimes the best meal is the one you don't plan.
