Ten Common Health Practices That Scientific Research Suggests May Be Counterproductive

TR
TopRank Editorial TeamMay 31, 2026⏱ 5 min read453,800 views

A review of recent medical and nutritional research reveals that several widely adopted health practices may not deliver the benefits their proponents claim — and in some cases may cause unintended harm. The following assessments are based on peer-reviewed studies and meta-analyses published between 2019 and 2026.

#1 — Juicing Fruits Instead of Eating Them Whole: A 2024 meta-analysis in the British Medical Journal examined 14 cohort studies involving 187,000 participants. Fruit juice consumption was associated with a small but statistically significant increase in type 2 diabetes risk (hazard ratio: 1.08), while whole fruit consumption was associated with a decreased risk (HR: 0.92). The mechanism is straightforward: juicing removes fiber, which slows sugar absorption. A 12-ounce glass of orange juice contains approximately 34 grams of sugar — nutritionally similar to a 12-ounce Coca-Cola (39 grams). The lead author, one researcher summarized: "The epidemiological evidence consistently distinguishes between whole fruit and fruit juice. They are not nutritionally equivalent foods."

#2 — The 10,000 Steps Target: The 10,000-step daily goal originated not from medical research but from a marketing campaign for a Japanese pedometer called the "Manpo-kei" (literally "10,000-step meter") released in 1965 in the lead-up to the Tokyo Olympics. A 2019 observational study of 16,741 older women published in JAMA Internal Medicine found that mortality risk decreased with step count up to approximately 7,500 steps per day, after which benefits plateaued. Exceeding 7,500 steps did not reduce mortality further. researchers noted: "The 10,000-step target is not wrong — walking more is generally better than walking less. But there is no evidence that 10,000 is a magic threshold, and fixating on the number may discourage people who can achieve health benefits at lower step counts."

#3 — Routine Multivitamin Supplementation: The Physicians' Health Study II, a randomized controlled trial of 14,641 male physicians followed for a mean of 11.2 years, found no significant difference in cardiovascular events or cognitive decline between the multivitamin and placebo groups. A 2013 systematic review in the Annals of Internal Medicine examined 26 studies and concluded that multivitamin supplements provide "no consistent evidence" of chronic disease prevention for the general population. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) can accumulate to toxic levels when supplemented unnecessarily. The National Institutes of Health's Office of Dietary Supplements states that "the best way to get the vitamins and minerals you need is from a well-balanced diet."

#4 — Complete Carbohydrate Avoidance: The anti-carbohydrate movement of the 2010s and early 2020s conflates refined carbohydrates (white bread, added sugars) with complex carbohydrates (whole grains, legumes, vegetables). Prospective cohort studies consistently associate whole grain consumption with reduced all-cause mortality. A 2016 meta-analysis in the BMJ of 45 studies found that whole grain intake was associated with a 16-22% reduction in cardiovascular disease, cancer, and all-cause mortality. The Mediterranean diet — consistently rated the healthiest dietary pattern by U.S. News and World Report's annual panel of medical experts — derives approximately 40-50% of calories from complex carbohydrates.

#5 — Excessive Use of Alcohol-Based Hand Sanitizer: A 2022 study in the Journal of Hospital Infection found that healthcare workers who used alcohol-based hand sanitizer more than 30 times per shift had significantly higher rates of irritant contact dermatitis. The alcohol kills both pathogenic and commensal bacteria on the skin surface, disrupting the skin microbiome. The CDC continues to recommend soap and water as the preferred hand hygiene method, with alcohol-based sanitizers (minimum 60% ethanol or 70% isopropanol) as an acceptable alternative when soap is unavailable. The key word is "when soap is unavailable" — routine use in settings where handwashing is accessible may cause unnecessary skin damage.

#6 — Checking Your Phone in Bed (Even With Night Mode Enabled): A 2024 study in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine used polysomnography to measure sleep quality in 120 participants who used their phones in bed with either standard display, night mode, or no phone. Both phone conditions showed delayed sleep onset (average 23 minutes) and reduced slow-wave sleep compared to the no-phone condition. Night mode reduced blue light exposure but did not significantly change sleep metrics. The lead researcher, researchers concluded: "The content is the problem as much as the light. Social media, email, and news engage the brain's arousal systems. The screen tells your brain, 'Something is happening. Pay attention.' That is the opposite of the signal you want at bedtime."
