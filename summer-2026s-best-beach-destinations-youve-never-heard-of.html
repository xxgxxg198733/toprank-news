Summer 2026's Best Beach Destinations You've Never Heard Of

TR
By Hannah Powell · July 22, 2026 · 3 min read

The Problem With Popular Beaches

If you've been to a popular beach in July, you know the drill: crowded parking, $50 beach chairs, and loud music from a dozen speakers. I've had enough. So this summer, I decided to find beaches that aren't on every 'top 10' list. Places where you can actually relax without fighting for towel space.

I spent two weeks driving along the coast of Portugal and another week in Mexico's Baja California. I found three spots that blew me away — and they're surprisingly cheap.

1. Praia do Amado, Portugal

Most tourists head to the Algarve (Lagos, Albufeira), but the western coast of Alentejo is quieter. Praia do Amado is a beach near the village of Carrapateira. It's a surfing spot, but even if you don't surf, it's stunning: golden cliffs, turquoise water, and almost no crowds. I went on a Tuesday in June and shared the beach with maybe 20 people.

The water is cold (15°C/59°F), but that's part of the charm. I rented a surfboard from a local shop for €15 per day. The instructor, a guy named Joaquim, gave me a two-hour lesson for €30. After, I ate grilled sardines at a beachside café for €8. I stayed at a guesthouse in Carrapateira for €45 a night.

How to get there: fly to Lisbon, rent a car, drive 2.5 hours south. The drive along the coast is stunning — stop at Cabo de São Vicente, the southwesternmost point of Europe.

2. Playa Balandra, Mexico

Most people go to Cabo San Lucas for party beaches. I went to La Paz, a quieter city on the Sea of Cortez. Playa Balandra is a 15-minute drive north of La Paz. It's a shallow, calm bay with white sand and water so clear it looks like a swimming pool. The water is warm (28°C/82°F) and only waist-deep for 100 meters out.

There are no resorts — just a small parking lot and a few palapa umbrellas. I kayaked to a nearby island and saw sea lions lounging on rocks. Entry is free, and parking costs $5. I stayed at a boutique hotel in La Paz called Baja Club for $120 a night. Dinner at a local seafood spot cost $15 for fish tacos and beer.

Warning: it gets busy on weekends, so go on a weekday. I went Thursday morning and had the beach almost to myself.

3. Playa Xcacel, Mexico

This one's a hidden gem on the Riviera Maya, between Tulum and Playa del Carmen. Most tourists flock to Tulum's crowded beaches, but Xcacel is protected as a sea turtle nesting site. It's a cove with calm water, palm trees, and a cenote (natural swimming hole) right next to the beach. No hotels, no restaurants — just a small food stand selling coconut water.

I swam in the cenote, which was cooler and clearer than the ocean. The beach has no waves, making it perfect for snorkeling. I saw parrotfish and a stingray. Entry costs $5. I spent the whole day there reading a book and swimming.

How to get there: rent a car in Cancún and drive 1.5 hours south. The turnoff is marked, but the road is bumpy — a rental car is fine.

Tips for Visiting These Beaches

First, bring cash. Most places don't take cards. Second, go early — arrive by 9 AM to beat the crowds (even hidden beaches get busy). Third, pack your own food and water. These beaches are remote, and options are limited. Fourth, respect the environment. Take your trash with you.

I also recommend learning a few phrases in Portuguese or Spanish. Locals appreciate the effort. At Praia do Amado, I said 'obrigado' to the surf shop owner, and he gave me a discount on a wetsuit rental.

Why These Beaches Matter

Tourism is crushing popular destinations. Venice is charging entry fees. Barcelona is limiting cruise ships. The Hamptons are unaffordable for most. These three beaches prove you don't need to spend a fortune or fight crowds to have an amazing beach vacation. You just need to know where to look.

My advice: book a flight to one of these places next month. You'll thank me when you're floating in warm water with no one else around.
