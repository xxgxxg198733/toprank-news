Santorini in June 2026: The Honest Truth About Crowds, Prices, and Whether It’s Still Worth It

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 4 min read

I’d been dreaming of Santorini since I saw a photo of Oia’s blue-domed churches in a travel magazine ten years ago. The whitewashed buildings, the caldera views, the sunsets that look like they’re painted by a god — it’s the kind of place that feels unreal. And in June 2026, I finally went. I saved up, booked a flight, and spent a week hopping between Fira, Oia, and the quieter beaches. Here’s the honest truth: it’s stunning. But it’s also a mess.

Let me start with the good stuff, because I don’t want to sound like a cynic. The caldera is genuinely breathtaking. Standing on the edge of that volcanic crater, watching the sun dip into the Aegean Sea, is a moment I’ll never forget. But that moment came with a soundtrack of 200 other tourists taking selfies, a guy loudly Facetiming his mom, and a woman who elbowed me for a better spot. The reality of Santorini in peak season is that you’re sharing the view with half of Instagram.

The Crowd Situation: It’s Worse Than You Think

I knew Santorini was popular. I’d read the articles about cruise ships disgorging thousands of passengers. But nothing prepares you for the sheer density of people. In Oia, the main pedestrian street is a human river. You can’t walk without bumping into someone. The famous sunset viewpoint at the castle? I arrived at 5 PM for a 7:30 PM sunset and found it already packed. People had staked out spots with blankets and picnic supplies. It felt like a concert, not a tranquil evening.

Here’s a tip that actually works: skip Oia for sunset. Instead, head to the little-known spot at Skaros Rock in Imerovigli. It’s a 20-minute hike, but the view is just as good and there were maybe 15 people there. Or watch from your hotel balcony if you can afford one with a view. The crowds are manageable if you avoid the ports and main lanes between 11 AM and 4 PM, when the cruise ship crowds are thickest.

The Prices: Ouch

I’d heard Santorini was expensive. I didn’t realize it was “$12 for a mediocre gyros” expensive. A meal at a mid-range restaurant in Fira runs about €40-60 per person without wine. Cocktails are €15-20. A glass of local wine (which is excellent, by the way) is €8-10. And the hotels? I stayed at a modest boutique hotel in Fira — no private pool, no caldera view — for €220 per night. The caldera-view rooms in Oia start at €500 and go up to €2,000. In June.

Is it worth it? That depends on your budget. If you’re flexible, consider visiting in late April or early May, when prices are 30% lower and the weather is still warm. Or skip Santorini altogether for the less touristy islands like Milos or Paros. I spent two days in Paros after Santorini and paid half the price for twice the charm.

The Food: More Than Just Gyros

Greek food on Santorini is actually fantastic — if you know where to go. Avoid the restaurants with laminated menus and someone waving menus in your face. Those are tourist traps. Instead, look for places with handwritten chalkboards. I had the best meal of my trip at Metaxi Mas, a tiny taverna in Exo Gonia. The fava (yellow split pea purée) was creamy and earthy, the tomato keftedes (fritters) were crispy, and the grilled octopus was tender. Total for two: €55. That’s a steal.

Also, try the local wine. Santorini has a unique volcanic soil that produces crisp, mineral-driven Assyrtiko whites. I visited the Domaine Sigalas winery and did a tasting for €15. Worth every cent.

The Logistics: Getting Around Is a Pain

You’d think an island this famous would have good public transport. You’d be wrong. Buses are infrequent and crowded. The main bus from Fira to Oia runs every 30 minutes but fills up fast. Taxis are scarce — I tried calling a taxi once and waited 40 minutes. I rented an ATV for €50 a day, which was the best decision I made. It gave me freedom to explore the island’s quieter corners, like the Red Beach and the village of Pyrgos. Just watch out for the narrow roads and the occasional donkey.

A word of caution: don’t drive to Oia during sunset. The traffic jams are legendary. Park at the entrance and walk in.

Is It Still Worth It in 2026?

Here’s my honest answer: yes, if you manage your expectations. Santorini is a postcard come to life, but it’s also a theme park version of itself. The beauty is real, but so are the crowds and the prices. If you go, plan carefully. Book everything in advance — hotel, restaurants, even ferry tickets. Wake up early to see the empty streets at 7 AM. Find the hidden spots. And don’t let the chaos ruin the magic.

Would I go back? Probably not. There are too many other islands to explore. But I’m glad I went. It’s one of those places you have to see once, like the Eiffel Tower or Times Square. Just don’t expect to have it to yourself.

Now if you’ll excuse me, I’m going to look at my photos and forget the crowds. That’s what Instagram is for, right?
