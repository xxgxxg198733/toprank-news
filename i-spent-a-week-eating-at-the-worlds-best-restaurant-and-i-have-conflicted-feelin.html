I Spent a Week Eating at the 'World's Best Restaurant' — And I Have Conflicted Feelings

TR
By Christopher Lee · July 24, 2026 · 5 min read

Last month, the World's 50 Best Restaurants list dropped, and Disfrutar in Barcelona took the top spot. If you follow food news, you probably saw the headlines. Disfrutar — a Catalan restaurant run by three chefs who trained under Ferran Adrià at elBulli — beat out perennial favorites like Central in Lima and Noma in Copenhagen.

I've been obsessed with this list since I was in college. I've eaten at about 15 of the current top 50. So when Disfrutar was crowned number one, I did what any reasonable person with questionable financial priorities would do: I booked a flight to Barcelona and a table for two. The reservation cost €290 per person for the tasting menu, plus wine pairing at €180 each. Total for two people, with service: about €1,100. That's roughly $1,200. For one meal.

Was it worth it? I've been thinking about that question for a week, and I still don't have a simple answer. Here's what happened.

The Setup: Don't Expect a Quiet Dinner

Disfrutar is located in the Eixample district, a neighborhood full of modernist architecture and wide boulevards. The space is minimalist — white walls, pale wood, lots of natural light. It's beautiful. It's also loud. Not music loud, but conversation loud. The room buzzes. It's not romantic. It's theatrical.

Our table was by the open kitchen. We could see the chefs plating, the flames, the chaos. I loved that. My dining companion (my sister, who flew in from London) thought it was distracting. She's not wrong. If you want a quiet, intimate meal, this isn't it. If you want a show, you're in the right place.

The Food: 29 Courses of Pure Theater

The tasting menu is called "The Festival." It has 29 courses. 29. That's absurd. But somehow, it works. The pacing is impeccable. Smaller dishes come fast, larger ones slow down. You never feel rushed, but you also never feel bored. Two bites of something wild, then a pause, then something completely different.

Here are the dishes I'm still thinking about:

**The liquid olive.** This is their signature dish. It looks like a perfect green olive, but when you bite into it, it's a thin shell filled with olive juice. It explodes in your mouth. One bite. Perfect. I could have eaten 20 of them.

**The faux caviar.** They make spheres that look and taste like caviar, but it's actually a technique using seaweed and vinegar. Served on a tiny blini with crème fraîche. My sister, who hates seafood, loved this. That's how good the technique is.

**The pigeon with chocolate.** This sounds gross. I was skeptical. But it was the best course of the night. The pigeon breast was cooked medium-rare, glazed with a chocolate mole that was savory, not sweet. Served with a reduction of its own jus and a sprinkle of crushed cacao nibs. It tasted like the forest after rain. I don't know how else to describe it.

**The frozen gazpacho.** A dish that looks like a sorbet but tastes like cold tomato soup with hints of garlic and sherry. It arrives in a frozen marble bowl. You scrape it with a spoon. It's refreshing, weird, and brilliant.

Not everything worked. There was a course with fermented shrimp that smelled like a dirty sock. I ate it anyway. I regret that. And a dessert with white chocolate and wasabi that tasted like a mistake someone forgot to fix.

The Service: Warm but Stiff

The servers were professional, knowledgeable, and clearly proud of what they do. They explained every dish with enthusiasm. But there was a formality that felt out of place. At €290, I expect good service. But I also want to relax. Every time I leaned back, someone appeared to refold my napkin. It was exhausting.

Compare this to Central in Lima, where the servers joke with you and the vibe is genuinely warm. Disfrutar felt like a performance. A brilliant performance, but still a performance.

The Big Question: Is It the Best Restaurant in the World?

No. And yes. Let me explain.

If "best" means most technically impressive, most creative, most mind-bending, then yes, Disfrutar deserves the title. The techniques they use — the spherification, the foams, the textures — are at a level that few restaurants can match. Every dish is a science experiment that somehow tastes delicious.

But if "best" means the meal you'd want to have again tomorrow, the one you'd recommend to a friend who's not a food obsessive, then no. I'd send someone to Central in Lima, or to Atomix in New York, or to Den in Tokyo. Those restaurants are more welcoming, more soulful, more human.

Disfrutar is a museum. A stunning, breathtaking museum where you can eat the exhibits. But you don't live in a museum.

The Price: Let's Talk About It

€1,100 for two people is a lot. It's more than my rent in college. It's more than most people spend on groceries in a month. I'm privileged to have been able to afford it, and I'm not pretending otherwise.

Was it worth the money? For the experience, yes. I'll remember that pigeon dish for the rest of my life. But if you have €1,100 and one night in Barcelona, you could eat at six amazing restaurants for that price. You'd have more fun and eat more variety. Disfrutar is a splurge, not an everyday thing.

Final Thoughts: Go, But Go With Eyes Open

If you love food — really love it, the way some people love art or music — you should eat at Disfrutar once. It's an education. You'll learn what's possible when chefs have unlimited resources and insane talent.

But don't expect the best meal of your life. Expect the most interesting meal of your life. There's a difference.

I'm glad I went. I'm also glad I don't have to go again. And if you want my honest opinion, the best restaurant in the world is still the one that makes you happy. For me, that's a tiny ramen shop in Tokyo called Michelin-starred Nakiryu. But that's a story for another article.
