The New 'Dune: Messiah' Trailer Just Dropped: Here's What You Missed

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I've been a Dune nerd since I was 15, when I first picked up Frank Herbert's novel and got lost in the sandworms, the spice, and the political intrigue. So when the trailer for "Dune: Messiah" dropped at 9 AM today, I was ready. I had my coffee, my notebook, and a willingness to pause every single frame. And let me tell you: it's a lot. In a good way, mostly. But also in a way that makes me worried about how they're going to adapt one of the most divisive books in the series.

First, a quick recap: "Dune: Part Two" ended with Paul Atreides (Timothée Chalamet) leading the Fremen to victory, marrying Princess Irulan, and basically becoming the most powerful being in the universe. But the book "Dune Messiah" is a downer. It's about the consequences of power, the trap of prophecy, and the loneliness of being a messiah. It's my favorite book in the series because it's so human. But it's also slow, philosophical, and has a lot of talking. Can Villeneuve make it cinematic? The trailer suggests he's trying.

The Tone: Darker Than Ever

The trailer opens with a shot of Paul standing alone on Arrakis, looking at the horizon. The sky is orange, the wind is howling, and Hans Zimmer's score is pounding. Paul looks tired. He's not the confident warrior from Part Two; he's a man haunted by his own future. Chalamet's performance in just these few seconds is incredible—you can see the weight of the universe on his shoulders. Then we hear a voiceover: "The emperor is a prisoner of his own legend." It's a direct quote from the book, and it sets the tone perfectly.

The trailer cuts to quick flashes: a sandworm emerging from the desert, a Fremen ceremony, a spaceship exploding. But the most striking image is Paul and Chani (Zendaya) sitting in a tent, not speaking. The tension is palpable. If you've read the book, you know their relationship is about to get complicated. If you haven't, you're about to be heartbroken.

New Characters: Who's Who?

The trailer introduces several new characters that fans will recognize. First, we see Florence Pugh as Princess Irulan, looking regal and cold. She's in a throne room, delivering a speech about "order" and "control." She's clearly not on Paul's side, but she's not a villain either—she's a political player. I'm excited to see how Pugh portrays her, because Irulan is one of the most complex characters in the series.

Then there's Austin Butler as Feyd-Rautha. Yes, he's back. In the trailer, we see him in a gladiator arena, shirtless and covered in blood. He's smiling. It's terrifying. Butler's performance in Part Two was a highlight, and it looks like he's going to be even more unhinged in Messiah. But here's the twist: in the book, Feyd-Rautha dies in Part Two. So either Villeneuve is changing the story, or these are flashbacks. I'm betting on flashbacks, but I'm not sure.

We also get a glimpse of Christopher Walken as the Padishah Emperor Shaddam IV, looking frail and defeated. And there's a brief shot of a character I didn't recognize: a bald woman with a metal faceplate. I think it's a Tleilaxu Face Dancer, which is a deep cut from the book. If Villeneuve is including the Tleilaxu, this adaptation is going to be weirder than I expected.

The Big Change: The Time Jump

In the book, "Dune Messiah" takes place 12 years after the events of "Dune." But the trailer seems to suggest a shorter time jump. Paul still looks young, Chani is still fighting, and the Fremen are still wearing the same gear. This might be a smart move—keeping the same actors and aesthetic avoids confusion. But it also means losing some of the novel's themes about aging and legacy. I'm okay with it, but purists might be annoyed.

The Action: More Sandworms, More Chaos

The trailer doesn't skimp on spectacle. There's a scene where Paul rides a sandworm through a storm, and it's as epic as you'd imagine. There's a battle in the desert with lasguns and shields, and it looks chaotic and brutal. But the trailer is careful not to show too much—Villeneuve knows that the real drama in Messiah is psychological, not physical. The action is there to support the story, not replace it.

What's Missing: The Philosophical Stuff

Here's my concern: the trailer shows a lot of conflict, but not much of the introspection that makes Messiah special. The book is about Paul's struggle with his own prescience, his guilt over the jihad he's unleashed, and his desire to escape his fate. The trailer hints at this (Paul says "I see the path to destruction"), but it's buried under explosions and sword fights. I hope the movie takes time to breathe. Messiah is not an action story; it's a tragedy.

Release Date and What's Next

The trailer confirms a release date of November 20, 2026—just in time for Thanksgiving. That's later than I hoped, but it gives Villeneuve time to perfect the film. There's also a rumor that the movie will be split into two parts, but I think that's just speculation. At 2 hours 45 minutes, the trailer suggests a tight, focused film. We'll see.

For now, I'm cautiously optimistic. The trailer is beautiful, the cast is incredible, and Villeneuve has earned my trust after Part One and Part Two. But Messiah is a tough book to adapt. It's slow, strange, and deeply sad. If anyone can pull it off, it's Villeneuve. But I'll be holding my breath until November.
