I Spent a Week in Japan's 'Digital Detox' Island — No Phone, No Wi-Fi, No Regrets

TR
By Megan O'Brien · July 23, 2026 · 5 min read

I have a problem. I check my phone about 80 times a day. I know this because my screen time report told me. It's embarrassing. I'm a tech journalist, and I can't stop scrolling. Twitter, Instagram, news alerts, emails — it's a constant drip of dopamine that I can't seem to quit.

So when I heard about Sarushima Island — Japan's newly designated "Digital Detox Island" — I booked a trip immediately. The premise is simple: no cell service, no Wi-Fi, no screens allowed. Just nature, quiet, and your own thoughts. It sounded terrifying. And exactly what I needed.

Getting There: A Journey Back in Time

Sarushima is a small island in Tokyo Bay, about 30 minutes by ferry from Yokosuka. It's been a military fort since the 19th century, with abandoned tunnels and crumbling stone walls. The Japanese government recently opened it to tourists with a twist: you must surrender your phone upon arrival. They give you a locker key and a paper map. That's it.

I arrived on a Tuesday morning. The ferry was filled with a mix of Japanese tourists and foreign travelers, all of us clutching our phones nervously. The island is small — about 8 hectares — covered in dense forest, with rocky beaches and old military ruins. There's one small inn, a few campsites, and a single restaurant that serves simple seafood meals.

At the check-in desk, I handed over my iPhone 15 Pro. The staff member smiled and said, "You'll survive." I wasn't sure I believed her.

Day 1: The Panic

The first 24 hours were rough. I kept reaching for my pocket. I'd feel phantom vibrations. I wanted to check the news, see if anyone had messaged me, scroll through Instagram. My brain was screaming for stimulation.

I walked around the island instead. The old military tunnels are eerie — dark, damp, with graffiti from decades past. I climbed to the highest point and looked out over Tokyo Bay. The city skyline was visible in the distance, hazy and unreal. I sat there for an hour, just watching the water. It felt strange. Uncomfortable. But also peaceful.

At dinner, I ate grilled fish and rice at the restaurant. No phone to photograph my food. No distractions. I actually tasted the fish. I talked to the Japanese couple at the next table — something I never would have done if I had my phone.

Day 3: The Shift

By day three, something changed. I stopped reaching for my phone. The phantom vibrations stopped. I woke up naturally, without an alarm. I read a physical book (Kafka on the Shore, which I'd been meaning to read for years). I went swimming in the cold, clear water. I took a nap in the sun.

Time slowed down. Normally, my days feel like a blur of notifications and tabs. Here, each hour felt full. I watched ants carry crumbs across a picnic table. I noticed the way the light changed through the pine trees. I had conversations that lasted two hours — not because we had anything urgent to say, but because we had time.

Day 5: The Boredom

Day five was hard. The novelty wore off. I was bored. Really bored. There's only so much island to explore, so many rocks to skip across the water. I wanted a podcast. I wanted to Google something. I wanted to text a friend.

But boredom, I realized, is a muscle. You have to exercise it. Instead of reaching for a screen, I sat with the boredom. I wrote in a notebook. I drew a terrible sketch of a seagull. I thought about my life, my work, my relationships. It was uncomfortable, but necessary. Modern life has conditioned us to avoid boredom at all costs. But boredom is where creativity comes from. It's where you process your thoughts.

Day 7: Leaving

On the last day, I didn't want to leave. I had settled into a rhythm — waking with the sun, eating simple meals, walking, reading, sleeping. My phone felt like a foreign object when I picked it up from the locker. I turned it on and was hit by a flood of notifications: 47 emails, 23 Instagram likes, 12 texts, 3 missed calls. It felt overwhelming. Unnecessary.

I took the ferry back to Yokosuka. The city was loud, bright, chaotic. I was immediately sucked back into my phone on the train. But something had changed. I wasn't checking it compulsively anymore. I was aware of the pull. And I could resist it more easily.

The Verdict: Is It Worth It?

Sarushima Island isn't for everyone. If you're in a job that requires constant availability, you can't just disappear for a week. But if you can swing it, I highly recommend it. The experience is not about the island itself — it's about what you discover when you remove the noise.

I learned that I'm addicted to my phone. Not in a dramatic, life-ruining way, but in a subtle, insidious way. It's the first thing I reach for in the morning and the last thing I touch at night. That's not healthy. A week without it reset something in my brain.

Since returning, I've kept my phone in a drawer during meals. I read books before bed instead of scrolling. I go for walks without headphones. Small changes, but they've made a big difference.

If you're feeling burned out, overwhelmed, or just tired of the constant noise, consider a digital detox. You don't have to go to Japan. You could leave your phone at home for a weekend. But if you want the full experience — the forced disconnection, the beautiful scenery, the old military tunnels — Sarushima is waiting. And your phone will still be there when you get back.
