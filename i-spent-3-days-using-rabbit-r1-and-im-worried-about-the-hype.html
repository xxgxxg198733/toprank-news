I Spent 3 Days Using Rabbit R1 — And I'm Worried About the Hype

TR
By Andrew Foster · July 23, 2026 · 4 min read

If you follow tech news, you've heard of the Rabbit R1. It's the little orange device that went viral at CES in January 2024, promising to be an "AI assistant" that could replace your phone. The idea was simple: instead of tapping through apps, you talk to the R1, and it takes action — ordering Ubers, booking flights, sending messages — on your behalf, using a large language model and custom APIs.

It sounded amazing. People pre-ordered over 100,000 units in the first week. The hype was enormous.

I finally got my review unit last week. I've been using it for three days straight. And I have complicated feelings.

What the Rabbit R1 Gets Right

First, the hardware is charming. It's small — about the size of a deck of cards — with a bright orange shell, a scroll wheel on the side, and a rotating camera. The screen is a 2.88-inch touchscreen, but you're not supposed to use it much. The whole point is voice control. The design is retro-futuristic, like something from a 90s sci-fi movie. I like it.

Setting it up is easy. You connect it to your phone via Bluetooth, link your accounts (Uber, Spotify, Amazon, etc.), and you're ready. The Rabbit OS is a custom Android-based system that runs a large action model (LAM) to interpret your requests.

When it works, it's impressive. I said, "Order me a latte from Starbucks," and it did — without me touching a screen. I said, "Play my Discover Weekly on Spotify," and it played. The voice recognition is fast and accurate. The scroll wheel feels nice. The speaker is surprisingly good for such a small device.

Where It Falls Apart

But here's where things get frustrating. The R1 works maybe 60% of the time. The other 40%, it either misunderstands, takes too long, or gives up entirely.

I tried to book an Uber to a friend's house. The R1 asked for the address, confirmed it, and then... sat there for 30 seconds. Then it said, "I'm having trouble connecting to Uber." I tried twice more. Same result. I ended up pulling out my phone and doing it in 10 seconds.

I asked it to "set a timer for 10 minutes." It replied, "I can help with that!" and then did nothing. I waited. Nothing. I asked again. It said, "Timer set for 10 minutes." But no timer actually started. Basic stuff like this shouldn't fail.

The LAM is supposed to learn your habits over time, but in three days, I didn't see much improvement. It still struggles with multi-step requests. "Order pizza from Domino's, add pepperoni and extra cheese, deliver to my home address" — this confuses it. It often drops steps or asks for clarification on things that should be obvious.

There's also the latency issue. Every request takes 5-10 seconds to process because it's sending data to the cloud and waiting for a response. That's fine for complex tasks, but for simple things like "what's the weather?", it's slower than just glancing at your phone.

Security and Privacy Concerns

I'm not a privacy nut, but the R1 raises real questions. It's always listening for the wake word. All your requests are processed in the cloud. Rabbit's privacy policy says they don't sell your data, but they do use it to improve the model. And because the R1 integrates with third-party services, you're giving Rabbit access to your Uber history, Spotify playlists, and Amazon orders.

If Rabbit gets hacked — and every tech company eventually does — that's a lot of personal data in one place. The company hasn't released a detailed security audit. That worries me.

Who Is This For?

I've been asking myself this question all week. The R1 is $199, plus a subscription for some features after the first year. It doesn't replace your phone — you still need your phone for apps that aren't supported, for authentication, for anything sensitive. So it's an accessory. A $200 accessory that does less than your phone.

Maybe it's for people who want to reduce screen time? But the R1 has a screen, and you end up looking at it while you wait for responses. Maybe it's for older adults who find smartphones confusing? But the setup process is complicated, and the voice commands require specific phrasing.

The truth is, the Rabbit R1 feels like a beta product. A promising idea, executed too early. The hardware is lovely. The vision is exciting. But the software isn't ready for prime time.

The Future

I want Rabbit to succeed. I want a world where I can just talk to a device and get things done. But we're not there yet. The R1 needs faster processing, better reliability, and more integrations. Rabbit needs to fix the basic bugs before adding fancy features.

If you're a tech enthusiast with $200 to spare, go ahead — it's a fun toy. You'll enjoy showing it to friends. But if you're looking for a practical tool that saves you time, wait for version 2.

As for me? I'm keeping it on my desk. I'll check back in six months. Maybe by then, the software will catch up to the hardware's potential.
