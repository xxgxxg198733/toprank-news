10 Things I Wish I Knew Before Visiting Santorini in 2026

TR
By Lauren Davis · July 24, 2026 · 4 min read

I finally made it to Santorini last month, and let me tell you – it's every bit as beautiful as the pictures. But it's also crowded, expensive, and full of hidden surprises that no guidebook prepares you for. I spent two weeks exploring every corner of this volcanic island, from the tourist-packed streets of Oia to the quiet vineyards of Megalochori. Here are the 10 things I genuinely wish someone had told me before I booked my flight.

1. The Crowds Are No Joke – But They're Manageable

I'd read about the crowds, but nothing prepares you for the reality of trying to snap a photo of the famous blue domes in Oia at sunset. There were literally hundreds of people jostling for position. But here's the hack: go at sunrise. I had the entire spot to myself at 6 AM. Also, skip the main pedestrian street during peak hours (11 AM to 4 PM) and explore the side alleys – they're just as pretty and almost empty.

2. The Food Scene Is Way Better Than I Expected

I thought Santorini would be all overpriced tourist traps, but the food blew me away. The cherry tomatoes and white eggplants grown in the volcanic soil are unlike anything I've tasted. Don't miss the tomato fritters (tomatokeftedes) at a little family-run place called Metaxi Mas in Exo Gonia. It's not fancy, but it's the best meal I had on the island.

3. Renting an ATV Is the Best Decision You'll Make

The bus system is unreliable, taxis are scarce and expensive, and rental cars are fine but parking is a nightmare. I rented a small ATV from a place near the airport for €35 a day, and it was the best decision. I could park anywhere, zip through traffic, and take dirt roads to hidden beaches. Just bring a bandana for dust.

4. The Beaches Are Not What You Expect

If you're dreaming of soft white sand, Santorini will disappoint. The beaches are mostly black, red, or volcanic pebbles. But that's not a bad thing – Red Beach is stunning with its dramatic cliffs, and Perissa's black sand is striking. Just bring water shoes because those pebbles get hot.

5. Fira Is Underrated Compared to Oia

Everyone obsesses over Oia, but I actually preferred Fira. It's more lively, has better nightlife, and the views from the cliffside walkway are just as spectacular. Plus, the cable car ride down to the old port is a thrill. Oia is beautiful but feels like a curated museum; Fira feels like a real town.

6. The Wine Tour Is Worth Every Euro

I'm not usually a wine tour person, but Santorini's volcanic soil produces unique Assyrtiko grapes that make crisp, mineral-driven whites. I did a tour with Santo Wines, and the tasting included 6 wines paired with local cheeses. The sunset views from their terrace were better than anything in Oia, and it was less crowded.

7. You Need to Book Restaurants in Advance

This one caught me off guard. During peak season, the popular spots like Selene and Ambrosia are booked weeks ahead. I walked into a place that looked decent and was told there's a 2-hour wait. My advice: book everything at least a week in advance, especially if you want a table with a view.

8. The Volcano Boat Tour Is a Must-Do

I almost skipped this because I thought it would be cheesy, but I'm so glad I didn't. The boat takes you to Nea Kameni, where you can hike up the active volcano crater – yes, you can feel the heat rising from the ground. Then you get to swim in the hot springs. It's a full day, but it gives you perspective on how this island was formed.

9. Bring Layers, Even in Summer

I visited in July and still needed a light jacket for evenings. The meltemi wind can be fierce, especially on the eastern side of the island. One night I was shivering in a sundress at a cliffside restaurant. Pack a pashmina or a denim jacket – you'll thank me.

10. The People Are Incredibly Welcoming

Despite the crowds and the tourist economy, I found the locals to be genuinely warm and helpful. A shopkeeper in Pyrgos spent 20 minutes helping me find directions to a hidden beach. Another family invited me to join their table for Easter celebrations. Santorini is beautiful, but the people make it special.

So there you have it – my honest take on Santorini in 2026. It's crowded, yes. It's expensive, yes. But if you go prepared, it's still one of the most magical places I've ever been. Would I go back? In a heartbeat. But I'll definitely book those restaurants in advance next time.
