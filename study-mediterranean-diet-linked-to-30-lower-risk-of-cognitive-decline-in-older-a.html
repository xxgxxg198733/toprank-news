Study: Mediterranean Diet Linked to 30% Lower Risk of Cognitive Decline in Older Adults

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 03, 2026 ⏱ 1 min read 64,790 views

Yet another reason to eat like you're in Greece. A large-scale study published in JAMA Network Open on June 1, 2026, has found that older adults who closely follow a Mediterranean diet have a 30% lower risk of developing cognitive decline over a 12-year period. The findings add to a growing body of evidence linking diet to brain health.

The Study

Researchers at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health analyzed data from 65,000 participants aged 65 and older, drawn from the Nurses' Health Study and the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study. Participants were surveyed on their eating habits every four years and underwent cognitive tests. 'The results were striking,' said lead author Dr. Olivia Chen. 'Those with the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet showed significantly slower rates of memory decline.'

— Advertisement —

Key Components

The Mediterranean diet emphasizes vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds, with olive oil as the primary fat source. It includes moderate consumption of fish and poultry, and limited red meat and sweets. The study found that specific components—particularly green leafy vegetables, berries, and nuts—had the strongest protective effect. 'The anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties are key,' Chen added.

Practical Advice

For those looking to protect their brain health, the study recommends starting dietary changes early. 'It's never too late, but earlier is better,' said Dr. Richard Isaacson, a neurologist at the Alzheimer's Prevention Clinic in New York. Simple swaps like using olive oil instead of butter, eating fish twice a week, and snacking on nuts can make a difference. The study also noted that even moderate adherence (about 60% of the diet) offered some benefit.

With dementia rates projected to triple by 2050, lifestyle interventions like diet offer a promising, low-cost strategy. The Mediterranean diet isn't just good for your heart—it's good for your mind too.
