World's First 'Living Robot' Created from Frog Cells Reproduces, Scientists Stunned

TR
By TopRank Team June 06, 2026 ⏱ 1 min read

In a breakthrough that sounds like science fiction, researchers at the University of Vermont and Tufts University have announced that their 'living robots,' known as Xenobots, can now reproduce. The study, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) on June 6, 2026, describes how these millimeter-wide robots made from frog stem cells can find loose cells in their environment and assemble them into new Xenobots.

What Are Xenobots?

First created in 2020, Xenobots are not traditional robots with metal and wires. They are living organisms—tiny blobs of cells derived from the African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis). They were initially designed to move through fluid, heal themselves, and perform simple tasks like cleaning up microplastics. The new discovery is that they can spontaneously reproduce in a process called 'kinetic replication,' which has never been observed in any organism before.

How Does the Reproduction Work?

Unlike sexual or asexual reproduction in animals, Xenobots gather individual cells floating in a petri dish and assemble them into a new, functional Xenobot. This process happens without any external manipulation. The new 'offspring' then start moving and can themselves reproduce. The researchers used a supercomputer to simulate the optimal shape for replication, resulting in a 'C' shape that functions like a tiny Pac-Man gathering cells.

Ethical Concerns and Future Potential

The announcement has sparked debate. Some ethicists worry about the implications of self-replicating biological machines. However, the researchers emphasize that the Xenobots are fully biodegradable and contained in lab environments, with no risk of escaping. Potential applications include targeted drug delivery, environmental cleanup, and even biological computing. As one scientist put it, 'We are witnessing the birth of a new form of life.'
