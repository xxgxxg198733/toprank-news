I Switched to a Standing Desk for 30 Days — Here's What Actually Happened to My Body

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I have a confession: I'm a serial sitter. I work from home, and my default posture is a human question mark hunched over a laptop. My back hurts. My neck hurts. My chiropractor has named his boat after my co-pay.

So when Varidesk sent me their ProPlus 36 standing desk converter to test, I thought, 'Sure, why not? What's 30 days?'

Spoiler: it's not that simple. The standing desk industry is built on promises of better health, more energy, and higher productivity. But after a month of tracking my habits, my pain levels, and my actual output, I have some thoughts that might surprise you.

The First Week: The Honeymoon Phase

Day one was great. I set up the Varidesk on my existing desk, adjusted the height to match my elbows at a 90-degree angle, and stood proudly like some sort of productivity guru. I lasted 45 minutes before my feet started hurting.

But I pushed through. By day three, I was standing for two hours at a stretch. I felt virtuous. I was burning calories! I was improving my posture! I was — let's be honest — mostly just thinking about how much my legs hurt.

The Varidesk itself is solid. It's a gas-spring lift mechanism, so raising and lowering it is smooth. The work surface is spacious — 36 inches wide, enough for my monitor, laptop, and coffee. The build quality is excellent. No wobble, even at full height.

But the honeymoon didn't last. By day five, the novelty wore off, and reality set in.

The Reality: Standing Isn't a Miracle Cure

Here's what nobody tells you about standing desks: standing still is also bad for you. Prolonged standing puts stress on your lower back, knees, and feet. It can lead to varicose veins, joint pain, and circulatory issues. The research is clear — the ideal is to alternate between sitting and standing throughout the day.

I learned this the hard way. By day seven, my lower back was actually worse than when I was sitting. I'd been standing with locked knees, which pinches nerves. My feet ached constantly. I bought an anti-fatigue mat (the Varidesk brand one, which is fine but overpriced). It helped, but didn't solve the problem.

I texted my friend Dr. Sarah Mitchell, a physical therapist in Portland. She said: 'Standing desks aren't magic. They're a tool. The real benefit comes from movement — changing positions, walking around, stretching. If you just stand still for eight hours, you're trading one problem for another.'

The Productivity Question

A lot of standing desk advocates claim they're more productive when standing. I didn't find that to be true. In fact, during the first two weeks, my typing speed dropped slightly. I was more distracted by the discomfort. I took more breaks — not the good kind, where I walk around, but the bad kind, where I check my phone while sitting down because my feet hurt.

But something interesting happened around week three. I stopped thinking about it. The desk became background noise. I'd stand for 30-45 minutes, then sit for 20. The transition became automatic. And when I was standing, I noticed I was more alert — not superhuman, but less prone to the 2 PM slump.

Productivity-wise, it's a wash. I didn't get more done. But I didn't get less done either. The real benefit was how I felt after work — less achy, less stiff, less like I needed to lie down for an hour before dinner.

The Health Data (What I Actually Measured)

I wear an Apple Watch Series 9, so I tracked my activity for the full 30 days. Here are the numbers:

Before the standing desk: average 4,200 steps/day. After: 6,100 steps/day. That's a 45% increase, mostly from pacing while on calls or thinking. Standing burns about 0.15 more calories per minute than sitting — so over a 4-hour standing session, that's about 36 extra calories. Not nothing, but not a weight loss solution.

My heart rate variability (HRV) improved slightly. My resting heart rate stayed the same. My posture? Hard to measure, but photos show my shoulders are less rounded. Progress, but not transformation.

The Cost Question

The Varidesk ProPlus 36 retails for $450. Is it worth it? I think it depends on your situation.

If you have an existing desk that you like and just want the ability to stand, a converter is a good option. You can find cheaper ones on Amazon for $200-300. The Varidesk is well-made and has a 10-year warranty, but you're paying for the brand.

If you're buying a new desk entirely, I'd recommend a full electric standing desk from companies like Fully (now owned by Herman Miller) or Uplift. They're more expensive ($600-1,200) but better integrated and more stable at standing height.

Or — and this is the controversial take — you could just get a $30 lap desk for your couch and change positions every 30 minutes. Most of the benefit comes from not being static, not from the specific equipment.

The Verdict After 30 Days

I'm keeping the Varidesk. But I've adjusted my expectations. It's not a wellness miracle. It's a tool that, used correctly, can make a workday slightly less punishing.

My routine now: stand for 30-45 minutes, sit for 20, stand for 30. I take a 5-minute walking break every hour. I do a 2-minute stretch routine (youtube stretches from 'Ask Doctor Jo') at lunch. And I actually move around while standing — shifting weight, stepping side to side, walking in place.

Would I recommend a standing desk? Yes, but only if you're willing to change your habits, not just your furniture. The desk doesn't fix you. You have to fix you.

And maybe buy a better anti-fatigue mat than the Varidesk one.
