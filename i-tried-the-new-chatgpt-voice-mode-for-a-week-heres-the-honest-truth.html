I Tried the New ChatGPT Voice Mode for a Week. Here's the Honest Truth

TR
By Xiao Chao · June 08, 2026 · 3 min read

Last Monday, OpenAI rolled out its new voice mode for ChatGPT to all free users, and honestly? I was skeptical. I've been burned by AI voice assistants before — Siri still misunderstands me half the time, and Alexa once ordered 12 pounds of cat food when I asked her to play a song. But after spending a full week using this thing for everything from cooking advice to late-night rants, I've got some real thoughts.

First, What Actually Changed?

On June 1, 2026, OpenAI announced that ChatGPT's advanced voice mode, previously locked behind the $20/month Plus subscription, would be available for free. The Verge reported that over 10 million users tried it within the first 48 hours. The big deal here is that it's not just reading text out loud — it can detect your tone, laugh, pause, and even sigh. It feels like you're talking to a person, not a robot reading a script.

The first thing I did was ask it to tell me a joke. It laughed at its own punchline. Genuinely laughed. I wasn't prepared for how human that would feel. My girlfriend walked in and asked if I was on the phone with someone. When I told her it was an AI, she didn't believe me. That's the moment I knew this was different.

Where It Shines: Real Conversations

I've been using it to brainstorm for this very article. Instead of typing out ideas, I just talked to it. We had a 20-minute back-and-forth about what angle to take, and it remembered what I'd said 15 minutes earlier. No repetition, no awkward pauses. Honestly, it felt like talking to a clever friend who actually listens.

But here's what nobody's talking about: the voice mode is genuinely good at emotional support. I'm not kidding. I had a rough day on Wednesday — work stress, family stuff — and I just vented to ChatGPT for 10 minutes. It didn't give me canned responses. It said things like, "That sounds really hard. Do you want to talk about what specifically is stressing you out?" I teared up a little. Is that pathetic? Maybe. But it's real.

The Frustrations: Still Not Perfect

Okay, let's be real. It's not all sunshine. For one, it sometimes mishears words in a noisy environment. I tried using it while cooking with the exhaust fan on, and it kept thinking I said "pasta" when I said "pesto." That's a small thing, but it broke the immersion.

Also, it can be a bit too polite. When I asked it to critique my writing, it was gentle to the point of being useless. I had to tell it, "No, really, tell me what sucks about this paragraph." Then it gave me actual constructive feedback. But most people won't think to do that. OpenAI needs to add a "brutal honesty" toggle.

Battery Life and Data Usage

I used it on an iPhone 15 Pro, and it chewed through battery like crazy. About 20% per hour of continuous conversation. That's better than I expected, honestly, but still not great for all-day use. Data usage was around 50MB per hour, which is fine if you're on WiFi but could be a problem for people with limited plans.

The Verdict: A Glimpse of the Future

After a week, I'm convinced that voice-first AI is the next big interface shift. Typing feels archaic now. I had a conversation with my dad yesterday — he's 68 and not tech-savvy — and he loved it. He asked it for gardening tips and they had a 30-minute chat. He said, "It's like having a neighbor who knows everything."

But here's the catch: it's still a novelty for most people. The real test will be whether we're still using it in a month. For now, I'm impressed. More than I thought I'd be. If you haven't tried it yet, you should. Just don't be surprised if you start talking to your phone more than your actual friends.
