10 Things I Actually Learned From That Wild 2026 Summer Travel Season (So Far)

TR
By Nicole Barnes · July 23, 2026 · 6 min read

It's July 23, 2026, and I'm sitting in a cramped airport lounge in Atlanta after my third delayed flight in two weeks. The girl next to me is crying on the phone about a missed connection to Seattle. The guy across the way is loudly complaining about his rental car reservation getting ‘lost in the system.’ This is summer travel now—and honestly? It's been a wild ride.

I've traveled to 14 states and three countries since Memorial Day. I've slept in a car twice, eaten gas station sandwiches more times than I want to admit, and discovered that the best travel advice comes from strangers in airport bars. So here's my list of 10 things I actually learned—not the glossy Instagram version, but the real, sweaty, annoying, occasionally magical truth about traveling in the summer of 2026.

1. Google Flights Is Still King, But You Need Patience

I've been using Google Flights for years, but this summer the pricing has been absurd. I found a $69 one-way from Chicago to Denver—but only if I left at 5 AM on a Tuesday. The algorithm is brutal but fair. If you're flexible on time, you can still find deals. I saved $400 on a round trip to Portland by shifting my departure by one day. But you have to check it obsessively. I mean, I checked it three times a day for a week before booking.

The real trick? Use the 'explore' map feature. I randomly found a $55 flight to Nashville because I was just scrolling. That never would've happened if I'd stuck to a specific destination.

2. Airbnb Is Not What It Used to Be—But There's a Silver Lining

Let's be real: Airbnb has become a mess. Cleaning fees that cost more than the room itself, hosts who ghost you, and properties that look nothing like the photos. I booked a 'cozy studio' in Austin that turned out to be a closet with a mattress. I'm not even kidding—the door barely opened past the bed.

But here's what I learned: VRBO is actually making a comeback. I found a legit cabin in the Smoky Mountains for less than a hotel room, and the host left me fresh-baked cookies. Also, look at smaller platforms like Vacasa or even booking directly with property management companies. Some of my best stays were through local rental agencies that have zero app presence.

3. The TSA PreCheck Line Is Longer Than Ever—But Still Worth It

I got TSA PreCheck in 2024, and this summer I've used it maybe 20 times. The lines have grown—sometimes 30 minutes at major airports like LAX or JFK—but compared to the regular line that snakes around the terminal? It's still a godsend. But here's the trick: sign up for Clear if you can. I finally caved after a two-hour wait in Denver, and now I breeze through in under five minutes. The combo of PreCheck + Clear is the only way to survive peak season.

One thing nobody tells you: if your flight is super early (like 5 AM), the regular line is often empty anyway. So don't waste money if you're a dawn traveler.

4. Europe Is Overrun—But Southern Europe Is Surprisingly Chill

I spent two weeks in Italy and Greece in June. Rome was a nightmare. The Colosseum? Surrounded by selfie sticks and tour groups that move like a herd of cattle. I waited 45 minutes for a gelato that was okay. But then I took a train to Puglia, and it was like a different world. Empty beaches, affordable seafood, and locals who actually smiled at me. The same with Sicily—nobody told me that the real Italy is in the south.

If you're planning a European trip right now, skip the capitals. Go to places like Matera, Lecce, or the Albanian Riviera. I met a guy who spent $40 a night on a beachfront room in Saranda. That's insane.

5. The Heatwave Is Real—And It's Changing How We Travel

July 2026 has been brutal. Record temperatures in Spain, France, and even the UK hit 40°C (104°F) last week. I was in Madrid when it hit 43°C, and the city basically shut down from noon to 5 PM. I learned to do what locals do: siesta. I started scheduling my sightseeing for early morning and late evening, and spent the hot afternoons in air-conditioned museums or, honestly, napping in my hotel room.

Also: buy a portable fan. I got one that clips onto my belt loop, and it saved me in Barcelona. Don't laugh—it works.

6. Road Trips Are the Real MVP of 2026

With flight delays and cancellations at an all-time high (I had three flights canceled in one week), road trips have become my go-to. I drove from Nashville to New Orleans, then from Denver to Moab, and it was glorious. No security lines, no baggage fees, no crying babies (except my own when I missed an exit).

But you need a good playlist. I made a 'Summer 2026' mix with Chappell Roan, Sabrina Carpenter, and some indie stuff. Also, download offline maps—cell service dies in the middle of nowhere faster than you'd think.

7. Booking Direct With Hotels Pays Off

I used to use Expedia or Hotels.com for everything. But this summer, I started calling hotels directly. I got a free upgrade in Chicago just by asking nicely. Another hotel in Savannah gave me a 15% discount because I mentioned I saw their room rate on Booking.com. They'd rather cut out the middleman.

The best move? Join hotel loyalty programs. I'm now a Hilton Honors member, and even though I'm not rich, I've earned two free nights this year. It's not glamorous, but it's real savings.

8. Travel Insurance Is Non-Negotiable

I never bought travel insurance before. Then my friend got stuck in Puerto Rico when Hurricane Bertha hit in June, and she was out $1,200. I now use World Nomads—it's cheap ($50 for a week trip) and covers everything from flight cancellations to lost luggage. I actually filed a claim after my bag got lost on a United flight, and they reimbursed me for clothes within three days. Worth every penny.

9. The Best Food Is Off the Tourist Path

In every city I visited, the best meal I had was at a place with no Yelp reviews and a handwritten sign. A taco stand in Austin, a pho shop in Portland's suburbs, a tiny bakery in Paris that only opens at 6 AM. I learned to ask locals: 'Where do you eat?' Not 'What's the best restaurant?' That makes all the difference.

Also: never trust a line. Long lines often mean overpriced mediocrity. Short lines with locals? That's gold.

10. Traveling Alone Is Better Than You Think

I spent a week solo in Colorado, and it was the best decision I made all summer. No arguments about where to eat, no compromise on hiking trails, no waiting for anyone to get ready. I met other solo travelers at hostel bars, and we ended up road-tripping together for two days. The loneliness lasted about 10 minutes. The freedom lasted the whole week.

If you're on the fence about solo travel, just do it. Book a hostel, bring a book, and talk to strangers. It's scary for the first hour, then it's the best thing ever.

So there you have it—my honest, unfiltered summer travel lessons. It's been chaotic, sweaty, and expensive. But I'd do it again tomorrow. Just maybe not through Atlanta.
