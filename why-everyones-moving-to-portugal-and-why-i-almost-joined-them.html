Why Everyone's Moving to Portugal (and Why I Almost Joined Them)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I have a friend named Jake who sold everything he owned in 2024 and moved to Lisbon. He works remotely for a US tech company, pays half the rent he did in San Francisco, and spends his weekends surfing in Cascais. His Instagram is a highlight reel of pastel de nata, sunset views from Miradouro da Graça, and pictures of his cat, who apparently loves Portugal as much as he does.

I was jealous. So I decided to spend a month in Portugal — split between Lisbon and Porto — to see if the dream is real.

Spoiler: it's complicated.

The Portugal Dream: What's Actually True

Let's start with what's real. Portugal is beautiful. The light in Lisbon is different from anywhere else — golden, warm, forgiving. The architecture is stunning, from the Pombaline downtown to the azulejo-tiled facades. The food is simple but excellent: grilled sardines, caldo verde, pastéis de nata warm from the oven.

The cost of living is lower than most of Western Europe and dramatically lower than the US or UK. Rent in Lisbon has gone up — a lot — but you can still find a one-bedroom apartment in a central neighborhood for €1,200-1,500. In Porto, it's €800-1,000. Compare that to San Francisco ($3,500) or London (£2,000), and it's a bargain.

The healthcare is good and affordable. The public transport works. The internet is fast. The people are genuinely friendly — the Portuguese have a warmth that isn't performative.

And the D7 passive income visa is a game-changer. If you have a steady income (around €1,000/month), you can get residency. No job offer required. No massive investment. Just proof of income and a clean record.

No wonder everyone's moving here.

The Problem Nobody Talks About

But here's the thing nobody on Instagram will tell you: Portugal has a housing crisis. And foreigners — especially digital nomads and retirees — are making it worse.

Lisbon rents have increased by 60% since 2020. Locals are being pushed out of neighborhoods they've lived in for generations. In Alfama, the historic heart of Lisbon, you see fewer Portuguese families and more Airbnbs and co-living spaces for remote workers. The resentment is real. There have been protests against gentrification. Signs in windows reading 'Não é turismo, é habitação' — 'It's not tourism, it's housing.'

I felt this tension during my stay. At a café in Príncipe Real, I chatted with a local barista named Mariana. She's 28, has a degree in graphic design, and works two jobs to afford a tiny apartment in Marvila. She told me, 'I'm happy you're here. But I'm sad about what's happening to my city. I can't afford to live where I grew up.'

I didn't know what to say. She was right.

The Tax Question: NHR 2.0

Portugal's Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) tax regime was one of the biggest draws for expats. It offered a 10-year tax holiday on foreign income — no tax on pensions, dividends, or capital gains. For retirees and remote workers, it was a golden ticket.

But the Portuguese government ended the NHR regime in 2023. There's a new version — often called NHR 2.0 — but it's more restrictive. You need to be a qualified professional (like a doctor, engineer, or artist) or have a local job. The days of moving to Portugal and paying zero tax on your US salary are over.

I talked to a tax lawyer in Lisbon who said the new rules are still being clarified. 'The government wants to attract talent, not just tax avoiders,' he told me. 'They want people who will contribute to the economy, not just take advantage of loopholes.'

Fair enough. But it means the Portugal dream is less accessible than it was two years ago.

The Bureaucracy: A Necessary Evil

I have never experienced bureaucracy like Portugal's. And I'm saying this as someone who has dealt with Indian bureaucracy, Italian bureaucracy, and the DMV.

Getting a NIF (tax number) requires an appointment that takes weeks to book. Opening a bank account requires documents that require other documents that require time travel. The golden visa process is infamous for delays. I met a British couple who had been waiting 18 months for their residency cards.

The Portuguese are aware of this. The government has been trying to digitize services, but it's slow. My advice: hire a lawyer. It costs €1,000-2,000, but it saves months of frustration.

The Weather: More Realistic Than You'd Think

Portugal is known for its sunny climate. And it's true — Lisbon gets 300 days of sun a year. Porto, less so. I spent two weeks in Porto in April and it rained nine days. Not a drizzle — proper rain. The kind that seeps into your bones.

But the sun, when it comes, is glorious. The summers are hot but not oppressive. The winters are mild. The Algarve in the south is a different world — warm beaches, dramatic cliffs, and a slower pace of life.

If you're moving from Northern Europe or the US Northeast, the weather will feel like a upgrade. If you're coming from California or the Mediterranean, it might feel like a sideways move.

The Verdict: Should You Move to Portugal?

After a month, I almost applied for a D7 visa. I had the paperwork ready. I found an apartment in Porto that I loved. I had a plan.

But I didn't do it. Not because Portugal isn't great — it is. But because I realized I was chasing a fantasy. The Instagram version of Portugal — the pastel de nata, the sunsets, the casual surfer vibe — is real, but it's not the whole story.

The real Portugal is complex. It's affordable but changing. It's beautiful but crowded. It's welcoming but burdened by bureaucratic inertia. It's a country that has opened its doors to the world, but isn't sure it likes what's walking through them.

If you're thinking about moving, do it for the right reasons. Not because it's trendy. Not because your friend did it. But because you've spent time there, you understand the trade-offs, and you're ready to be part of the solution, not part of the problem.

Portugal deserves that. And so do you.
