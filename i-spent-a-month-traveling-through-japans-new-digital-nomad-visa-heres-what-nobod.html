I Spent a Month Traveling Through Japan's New Digital Nomad Visa — Here's What Nobody Tells You

TR
By Jessica Thompson · July 21, 2026 · 6 min read

Japan's Digital Nomad Visa launched officially on April 1, 2026 — no joke. It allows remote workers from 49 countries to stay for up to six months with a valid remote job. I applied on day one, got approved in three weeks, and spent the entire month of June in Japan. I worked from Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, and a tiny town in the Japanese Alps that I'd never heard of before. It was one of the best months of my life. It was also one of the most frustrating. Here's what actually happens when you try to live and work in Japan as a digital nomad.

The Application Process: Easier Than I Expected

Everyone told me the Japanese bureaucracy would be a nightmare. It wasn't. The application is online through the Ministry of Foreign Affairs website. You need a valid passport, proof of remote employment (pay stubs or a letter from your employer), and proof of income — at least $50,000 per year. That's it. No interview, no mountains of paperwork.

The trick is the health insurance requirement. You need to prove you have coverage for the entire stay. My US insurance doesn't cover me abroad, so I had to buy a travel insurance policy from a Japanese provider recommended by the embassy. That cost about $300 for six months. Not bad. The whole process took exactly 19 days from submission to approval. I got a sticker in my passport at the airport in Narita.

The Reality of Working from Japan

Here's the part nobody on Instagram shows: the time zone difference is brutal. I'm based in New York, which is 13 hours behind Tokyo. My team's workday started at 9 AM Eastern, which is 10 PM in Tokyo. I spent the first week working from 10 PM to 6 AM. It was miserable. I was tired, my sleep schedule was wrecked, and I was grumpy in every meeting.

I eventually switched to a split schedule: work from 6 AM to 9 AM (catching the tail end of the US day), then take a break, then work again from 9 PM to midnight (the start of the US day). It wasn't perfect, but it was manageable. If you're from Europe or Australia, the time zone is much friendlier. If you're from the Americas, prepare for some serious schedule adjustments.

Where I Stayed: The Good, the Bad, the Surprising

I spent the first week in Tokyo at a co-living space called Shibuya Hikarie. It was fine — clean, fast Wi-Fi, a desk in my room. But it was also expensive ($2,500 for the month) and sterile. I felt like I was in a WeWork with a bed. Not my vibe.

Week two was in Kyoto, where I rented a traditional machiya townhouse on Airbnb. Beautiful. Wooden floors, sliding doors, a tiny garden. The Wi-Fi was surprisingly fast — 200 Mbps. But the walls were paper-thin, and I could hear my neighbor's TV through the wall. Working from a low table (zabuton seating) killed my back. After three days, I bought a portable laptop stand and sat on a cushion. Do not try to work from a traditional Japanese home without proper ergonomics.

Week three was Osaka, where I stayed in a modern apartment near Namba. This was the sweet spot. Western-style furniture, fast internet, close to great food. I paid $1,200 for the month. The neighborhood was lively but not overwhelming. I found a coworking space called Namba Hatch that had a quiet room, free coffee, and a view of the canal. I went there every day.

Week four was my favorite: a tiny town called Kamikochi in the Japanese Alps. I rented a room in a minshuku (family-run guesthouse) for $800 for the week. The internet was slow — 20 Mbps on a good day — but the scenery was unreal. Mountains, rivers, zero tourists. I hiked every morning, worked for a few hours, and ate dinner with the family who ran the place. They didn't speak English, I didn't speak Japanese, but we communicated through Google Translate and shared meals. It was magical.

The Internet: Better Than You Think, Worse Than You Need

Japan is famous for fast internet, and in cities, it's true. I got 500+ Mbps in my Tokyo hotel. 200+ in Kyoto. 400+ in Osaka. But in rural areas, it drops dramatically. Kamikochi was borderline unusable for video calls. I had to use my phone as a hotspot, which ate through my data plan. I recommend buying a pocket Wi-Fi device from a company like Japan Wireless before you arrive. They're about $100 for a month and give you reliable 4G (5G is still spotty outside cities).

The Social Life: Loneliness Is Real

Here's the thing nobody talks about: Japan can be incredibly lonely for digital nomads. The country isn't set up for casual socializing the way Southeast Asia is. There are no beach bars where you'll meet other travelers. Coworking spaces in Tokyo are expensive and full of locals who keep to themselves. I joined a few Facebook groups for digital nomads in Japan, and the meetups were sporadic.

I coped by staying in hostels for a few nights (even though I'm too old for that) and by using the app Meetup. I found a weekly language exchange in Osaka that was full of friendly locals and expats. I also joined a running club in Tokyo that met on Saturday mornings. It wasn't easy, but the connections I made were deeper than the ones I've made in more "social" destinations. Quality over quantity.

The Food: The Ultimate Perk

I can't lie — the food alone makes this worth it. I ate ramen at 10 AM, sushi at 2 PM, okonomiyaki at midnight. I never had a bad meal. My favorite discovery was a tiny soba shop in Kyoto run by an 80-year-old woman. No English menu, no photos. I pointed at something, and she brought out a bowl of soba noodles in a cold broth with a raw quail egg on top. It cost $5. It was the best thing I ate all month.

For budget, I spent about $40 per day on food. That's eating well — three meals, snacks, and the occasional drink. If you cook your own meals, you can do it for half that. Groceries in Japan are surprisingly affordable, and convenience stores (konbini) have decent options for cheap.

The Verdict: Should You Do It?

Yes, absolutely. But go in with your eyes open. The time zone is hard. The loneliness is real. The internet in rural areas is unreliable. But the experience is unmatched. I got to hike in the Alps, eat some of the best food on earth, and work from a country that's simultaneously ancient and futuristic.

Would I do it for six months? Probably not — I missed my friends and my routine. But a month was perfect. I came back refreshed, inspired, and with a new appreciation for both Japan and my own home. If you're thinking about applying, do it. Just don't believe the hype. Believe the reality. It's better.
