I Tried the Viral 'Sleepy Girl Mocktail' Trend. Here's What Actually Happened.

TR
By Jessica Thompson · July 23, 2026 · 5 min read

If you've been on TikTok anytime in the past month, you've probably seen the "Sleepy Girl Mocktail." It's a drink that claims to fix your sleep: tart cherry juice, magnesium powder, and soda water. Millions of people swear by it. I was skeptical. I have trouble sleeping — not insomnia, but that restless feeling where your brain won't shut up about tomorrow's to-do list. So I decided to try it for a full week. Here's what happened.

What's In the Drink?

The basic recipe is simple: 2 tablespoons of tart cherry juice (not sweetened), 1 teaspoon of magnesium glycinate powder (the kind that's easy on the stomach), and about 6 ounces of sparkling water. You stir it together and drink it 30 minutes before bed.

Some people add a splash of lemon or lime juice for flavor, but I kept it plain. The taste is interesting — tart and slightly sour from the cherry juice, with a fizzy kick from the soda water. It's not bad, but it's not delicious either. Think of it as a health drink, not a treat.

The science behind it is plausible. Tart cherry juice is a natural source of melatonin, the hormone that regulates sleep. Magnesium helps relax your muscles and calm your nervous system. So in theory, combining them should make you drowsy. But does it actually work in real life? I tracked my sleep with an Oura Ring and a journal for a baseline week, then did the mocktail experiment.

Night 1: Hopeful but Underwhelming

First night, I drank it at 9 PM, went to bed at 9:30. I fell asleep within 15 minutes — which is normal for me. But I woke up at 2 AM and couldn't get back to sleep for an hour. My Oura ring said I had "restless sleep" with low REM. So much for the magic potion.

Maybe it needs time to build up in your system, I thought. Plenty of people online said it took a few nights to work.

Nights 2-4: The Sweet Spot

By the third night, something changed. I started feeling drowsy about 20 minutes after drinking the mocktail. Not the kind of drowsy where you're fighting sleep, but a gentle relaxation. My jaw unclenched (I realized I clench it at night), and my shoulders dropped. I fell asleep faster — 7 minutes vs my usual 15. And I stayed asleep. According to my Oura ring, my deep sleep increased from 1.5 hours to 2.1 hours. That's a 40% improvement.

I also noticed I had fewer vivid dreams. That's a plus for me because my dreams are usually stressful (I dream about missing deadlines or losing my passport). The mocktail nights were calm. I woke up feeling more refreshed, even with fewer total hours of sleep.

Was it the mocktail or a placebo? I can't say for sure, but the timing was consistent. I drank it, I relaxed, I slept better. That's good enough for me.

Nights 5-7: The Honeymoon Ends

By night five, the effect started to fade. I still fell asleep quickly, but I woke up at 4 AM with a racing heart. I don't know why — maybe it was stress, maybe it was the drink. Night six was similar: I slept through, but my sleep score was back to baseline. Night seven was the worst: I had a headache the next morning, and I felt groggy all day.

I looked into it and found that too much magnesium can cause headaches and drowsiness. I had been using a tablespoon of powder instead of a teaspoon (I misread the label). Oops. Once I corrected the dosage, the headache went away. But it made me realize that this is a real supplement, not a harmless drink. You have to be careful with the amount.

The Verdict: Does It Work?

Yes, but with caveats. The Sleepy Girl Mocktail helped me fall asleep faster and increased my deep sleep — but only after a few days of consistent use. It wasn't a magic bullet, and one night of proper sleep didn't fix my chronic sleep quality. You probably need to use it for at least a week to see results.

The biggest downside is the sugar content. Tart cherry juice has natural sugar — about 15 grams per serving. If you drink this every night, that's 105 grams of sugar per week. For context, the American Heart Association recommends no more than 25 grams of added sugar per day for women. The mocktail's sugar is natural, but it's still sugar. If you're diabetic or watching your sugar intake, this isn't for you.

Also, the taste grows old quickly. By day four, I was tired of the sour fizz. I started mixing it with chamomile tea instead of soda water, which helped. But it's not a pleasure drink — it's a functional one.

Should You Try It?

If you have mild sleep issues — like trouble falling asleep or restless nights — it's worth a shot. It's cheap (tart cherry juice is about $8 per bottle, and magnesium powder is $12 for a month's supply), and the ingredients are natural. Just start with a lower dose of magnesium (300 mg is plenty) and see how you feel.

But if you have serious insomnia or sleep disorders, don't expect this to solve your problems. See a doctor. The mocktail is a supplement, not a medication. I'm going to keep using it a few times a week, but I won't rely on it every night. Variety is probably better for your body.

Final score: 7/10. It works, but it's not the magical fix TikTok makes it out to be. Then again, nothing ever is.
