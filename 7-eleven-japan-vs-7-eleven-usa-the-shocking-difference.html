7-Eleven Japan vs 7-Eleven USA: The Shocking Difference

TR
By Michael Chen · June 25, 2026 · 1 min read

I grew up on American 7-Elevens. Slurpees, hot dogs, and questionable taquitos. They’re fine, but they’re not special. Then I went to Japan. The convenience stores there—konbini—are a whole different universe. I spent a week visiting 7-Elevens in both cities to compare. The results shocked me.

Food Quality

Japanese 7-Elevens sell fresh sushi, onigiri (rice balls), bento boxes, and even ramen. Everything is made daily. I bought a chicken katsu sandwich that tasted like it came from a restaurant. The American version? A sad, soggy sandwich that’s been under a heat lamp for hours. No contest.

Hot Food Options

In Japan, you can get fried chicken, croquettes, and steamed buns. They keep them warm in heated cases. In the US, you get roller grill items—hot dogs, taquitos, and nachos with cheese sauce. They’re fine for drunk nights, but not comparable.

Drinks

Japan has a ridiculous variety of teas, coffees, and sodas. Cold matcha lattes, canned coffee that’s actually good, and yuzu lemonades. America has Coke, Pepsi, and Gatorade. Boring.

Services

Japanese 7-Elevens have ATMs, bill payment, and even ticket sales for events. The staff is insanely polite. In the US, it’s a lottery whether the cashier will acknowledge you.

Winner

Japan wins by a landslide. It’s not even close. But to be fair, American 7-Elevens are improving. Some now have fresh sandwiches and coffee bars. Still, if you ever visit Japan, spend an hour in a konbini. It’s a cultural experience.
