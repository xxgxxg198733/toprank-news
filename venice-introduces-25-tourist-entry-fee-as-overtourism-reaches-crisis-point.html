Venice Introduces €25 Tourist Entry Fee as Overtourism Reaches Crisis Point

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 496,820 views

Venice has announced its most aggressive measure yet to combat overtourism: a €25 entry fee for all day-trippers, mandatory pre-booking, and a cap of 50,000 visitors per day. The new rules, approved by the city council on June 2, 2026, will take effect on July 1, just as the peak summer season begins. The move follows years of protests from residents who say the city is being 'loved to death.'

Why the Fee Is Needed

Venice, a UNESCO World Heritage site, receives an estimated 30 million tourists annually, with up to 100,000 visitors on peak days—dwarfing its resident population of just 50,000. The crush of people has strained infrastructure, eroded historic buildings, and driven up housing costs. Cruise ships have been banned from the lagoon since 2021, but day-trippers still clog the narrow alleys and piazzas. 'Venice is not a theme park,' said Mayor Luigi Brugnaro. 'It is a living city, and it must be protected.'

How the System Works

All visitors over the age of 14 who are not staying overnight in a hotel will need to purchase a 'Venice Access Pass' online at least 48 hours in advance. The pass costs €25 and includes a QR code that is scanned at entry points like the train station and Piazzale Roma. Those staying overnight will have the fee waived but must register their accommodation. Fines for entering without a pass start at €300. The system is designed to spread visitors more evenly throughout the day and year.

Reactions and Outlook

Reactions are mixed. Many tourists have expressed frustration, but some organizers welcome the change. 'I'd rather pay €25 than queue for hours,' said Japanese tourist Sayuri Tanaka. Local activists are skeptical, arguing that the fee is not high enough. '€25 is a small price for a day in Venice,' said activist group 'Venice Against Tourism.' 'We need to see real limits on numbers, not just a tax.' The city will monitor the system over the summer, with the possibility of raising the fee or lowering the cap if needed. For now, Venice is placing a price on its own beauty.
