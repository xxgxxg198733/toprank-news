Hurricane Season 2026: First Major Storm 'Alberto' Hits Florida, Millions Without Power

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 1 min read 253,686 views

The 2026 Atlantic hurricane season started with a bang as Hurricane Alberto, a Category 4 storm, slammed into the Gulf Coast of Florida on the morning of June 4, 2026. With sustained winds of 150 mph, the storm made landfall near Tampa Bay, causing catastrophic storm surges, widespread flooding, and leaving over 3 million residents without power. This is the earliest major hurricane to hit the US since records began.

Evacuations and Emergency Response

Governor Ron DeSantis declared a state of emergency on June 2, ordering mandatory evacuations for coastal counties including Pinellas, Hillsborough, and Manatee. More than 500,000 residents fled inland, causing gridlock on major highways. The National Guard has been deployed, and FEMA has pre-positioned supplies. Shelters are open in Orlando and Tallahassee. 'This is a life-threatening situation,' said FEMA Administrator Deanne Criswell. 'Do not ignore evacuation orders.'

Damage and Impact So Far

In Tampa, several high-rise buildings suffered window blowouts, and downtown streets are under 6 feet of water. St. Petersburg reported a 12-foot storm surge. The Sunshine Skyway Bridge was closed. Hundreds of homes are destroyed, and at least 12 fatalities have been reported, mostly from drowning. The economic damage is estimated at $50 billion, making it one of the costliest storms in history. Power restoration could take weeks.

Climate Change Connection and What's Next

Meteorologists note that record-warm Gulf waters (87°F) fueled Alberto's rapid intensification. 'This is consistent with climate change predictions,' said Dr. Michael Mann of Penn State University. The hurricane will weaken as it moves inland, but heavy rain and tornadoes are still a threat across Georgia and the Carolinas. The Atlantic hurricane season runs through November 30, and forecasters predict an 'above-average' season. For now, Florida is bracing for a long recovery.
