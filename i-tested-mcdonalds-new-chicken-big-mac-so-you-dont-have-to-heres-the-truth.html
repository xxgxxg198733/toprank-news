I Tested McDonald's New Chicken Big Mac So You Don't Have To — Here's the Truth

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 4 min read

If you've been on food Twitter (I refuse to call it X) in the last week, you've seen the photos. McDonald's officially launched the Chicken Big Mac in the US on June 3rd, and the internet lost its collective mind. The concept is simple: take the iconic Big Mac, swap the two all-beef patties for crispy chicken patties, keep the special sauce, lettuce, cheese, pickles, and the all-important middle bun.

I have thoughts. Many, many thoughts.

Full disclosure: I'm a Chicken McNuggets loyalist. I've never understood the Big Mac hype — too much bread, too much sauce, the patties are too thin to taste. But a chicken version? That had my attention. So I did what any responsible journalist would do: I visited three different McDonald's locations across two days and ate three Chicken Big Macs.

For science.

The First Bite: A Revelation

My first Chicken Big Mac came from a drive-thru in suburban New Jersey at 11:30 AM on launch day. It was hot. It was fresh. The bun was soft, the lettuce crisp, the pickles had that perfect tang. And the chicken patty — which is basically a giant, flattened McChicken — was surprisingly juicy.

The special sauce works better with chicken than beef. I know that sounds like heresy, but hear me out. The Big Mac sauce is creamy, tangy, slightly sweet. It was designed to cut through the richness of beef. But beef at McDonald's isn't that rich — it's thin and borderline flavorless. The sauce overwhelms it. With chicken, the lighter meat lets the sauce complement instead of dominate.

I finished the first one in about four minutes. I immediately wanted another. This was dangerous information to possess.

The Second Bite: Problems Emerge

Later that evening, my wife and I shared one from a different location. This one was a problem. The chicken patty was dry — like, 'left under a heat lamp for 20 minutes' dry. The middle bun had disintegrated into a soggy mess. The whole thing fell apart after the third bite.

And this is the fundamental issue with the Chicken Big Mac: consistency. McDonald's is a logistics machine, but chicken is harder to keep perfect than beef. A dry chicken patty is noticeably bad. A dry beef patty? You barely notice because the sauce and bun do all the heavy lifting.

When it's good, it's really good. When it's bad, it's a 600-calorie disappointment.

How It Compares to the Original

Let's break this down honestly. The original Big Mac has 590 calories, 34g of fat, and 1500mg of sodium. The Chicken Big Mac? 530 calories, 25g of fat, and — wait for it — 1750mg of sodium. That's almost a full day's recommended sodium in one sandwich. But honestly, if you're eating at McDonald's, you've already made peace with that.

Price-wise, it's about the same — $5.99 in most markets, sometimes cheaper with an app deal. The real question is: does it taste better? I'd say yes, when fresh. The chicken patty has more surface area for crispy bits, and the sauce-to-meat ratio is better. But the original Big Mac has decades of cultural inertia. It's comfort food. The Chicken Big Mac feels like an experiment.

And experiments can fail.

The Internet Verdict (and Why It Matters)

Social media has been split. Some people are calling it the best thing McDonald's has done since the McRib. Others are saying it's a gimmick that doesn't work. The truth, as always, is somewhere in the middle.

What interests me more is what this says about McDonald's strategy. The company has been struggling with chicken innovation. The McCrispy was a hit. The McChicken is a classic. But they've failed to crack the premium chicken sandwich market — Popeyes and Chick-fil-A still dominate. The Chicken Big Mac is a hedge. It uses existing ingredients (the patty is just a bigger McChicken) in a new format. Low risk, high reward.

But is it a permanent menu item? McDonald's says it's limited-time only, but I've heard rumors that strong sales could make it permanent. If I were a betting man, I'd bet on it sticking around. It's just different enough to be interesting, but familiar enough not to scare people off.

The Final Verdict (After Three Sandwiches)

So should you try it? Yes. Absolutely. But with caveats.

Go to a busy McDonald's — high volume means fresher food. Order it fresh if you can. Eat it immediately — this sandwich does not travel well. And skip the fries; you're already committing a delicious sin.

Is it better than the original Big Mac? For me, yes. But I'm also the guy who prefers chicken sandwiches to burgers. Your mileage will vary. The safest bet is to try it yourself while it's still around.

Just don't eat three in two days. Trust me on that one.
