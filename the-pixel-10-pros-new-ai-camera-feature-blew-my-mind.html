The Pixel 10 Pro's New AI Camera Feature Blew My Mind

TR
By Amanda Brooks · July 22, 2026 · 3 min read

I Didn't Believe the Hype

When Google announced the Pixel 10 Pro at their hardware event last week, they showed off a feature called 'Reflection Eraser' — an AI tool that removes reflections from photos taken through glass. I rolled my eyes. Every year, smartphone companies promise AI magic that never works as well as the demo. So I bought a Pixel 10 Pro (after returning my iPhone for a refund) and tested it in the real world.

I'm a skeptic by nature. I've been burned by 'AI breakthroughs' before: Samsung's Galaxy S24 'Magic Eraser' left smudges, and Apple's 'Clean Up' tool in iOS 18.1 was decent but inconsistent. But the Pixel 10 Pro's Reflection Eraser? It's genuinely impressive.

How I Tested It

I took the phone to a busy street in downtown San Francisco. I found a coffee shop with large windows reflecting the sky and buildings. I snapped a photo of a friend inside — through the window, with her face partially obscured by a reflection of a passing bus. The reflection was strong: you could see the bus's red color and text over her face.

I opened the photo in Google Photos and tapped 'Edit,' then 'Reflection Eraser.' The processing took about 10 seconds. When the result appeared, I actually said 'whoa' out loud. The bus reflection was gone. Her face was clear, with natural skin tones. The window glass itself looked clean, like there was no reflection at all.

But it wasn't perfect. The AI left a slight blur in one area where the reflection had been — like a Photoshop smudge tool was used. Also, it couldn't remove reflections from shiny surfaces like a glass table. But for window reflections, it's better than I expected.

The Tech Behind It

I'm no engineer, but I did some digging. Google's Tensor G6 chip (their custom silicon) uses a neural network trained on millions of photos with reflections. The AI identifies the reflection by analyzing the angle of light and the depth of the scene. Then it reconstructs the pixels behind the reflection using surrounding data. It's similar to how they do 'Magic Eraser' for objects, but optimized for glass.

It's not just reflection removal. The Pixel 10 Pro also has 'Night Sight' improvements that reduce noise in low light by 40% (based on my tests). I took a photo of a dark alley at 10 PM — the result was brighter than what my eyes saw, but colors looked natural, not oversaturated like some Samsung phones.

What About Video?

Google also added 'Cinematic Blur' for video, which creates a depth-of-field effect in real-time. I tested it by filming my cat walking across a room. The background blurred smoothly, and the camera tracked my cat's face without lag. It's not as good as a DSLR, but it's the best phone video bokeh I've seen. My previous phone (iPhone 15 Pro) struggled with this in low light.

The catch? Video reflection removal isn't available yet. Google says it's coming in a software update later this year. So for now, the feature is still for photos.

Battery Life and Other Features

The Pixel 10 Pro has a 5,000mAh battery that lasted me a full day with heavy use (camera, maps, streaming). The display is a 6.8-inch LTPO OLED with 144Hz refresh rate — smoother than my iPhone's 120Hz. But the phone runs warm after extended camera use. Not hot, but noticeable.

The new 'Pixel Studio' app lets you generate AI images from text prompts, like ChatGPT for images. I typed 'a cat wearing a astronaut helmet on Mars' and got a decent image in 15 seconds. It's fun, but not something I'll use daily.

Should You Buy It?

If you're a photography enthusiast who takes photos through windows (museums, zoos, cityscapes), the Reflection Eraser is a game-changer. For everyone else, the Pixel 10 Pro is a solid Android phone with great camera software. But it's not a must-buy if you already have a Pixel 9 Pro or iPhone 16 Pro.

My honest take: the AI features are impressive, but they're still in the 'cool party trick' phase. I'd wait for reviews of the software update before committing. That said, Google's commitment to AI in cameras is pushing the industry forward, and that's exciting.
