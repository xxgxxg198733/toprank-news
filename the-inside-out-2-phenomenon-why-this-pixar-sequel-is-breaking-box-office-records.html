The 'Inside Out 2' Phenomenon: Why This Pixar Sequel Is Breaking Box Office Records

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I cried during a movie about emotions. Twice. I'm not ashamed to admit it. When I saw "Inside Out 2" on opening weekend, I was expecting a fun, nostalgic trip back to the mind of Riley. What I got was a gut-punch of a film that made me think about my own emotions in a way I hadn't since I was a teenager. And apparently, I'm not alone. The film has grossed over $1.5 billion worldwide as of this week, making it the highest-grossing film of 2026 and the most successful Pixar sequel since "Finding Dory." How did this happen?

Let's rewind. The original "Inside Out" came out in 2015, and it was a masterpiece. It took a complex concept—the emotions inside an 11-year-old girl's head—and turned it into a funny, heartbreaking, and insightful story. It won an Oscar and became a cultural touchstone. A sequel seemed unnecessary. But director Kelsey Mann, who took over from Pete Docter, had a vision: what if Riley became a teenager? What if new emotions showed up? And what if the film tackled the chaos of puberty?

The New Emotions: Anxiety, Envy, Embarrassment, and Ennui

The marketing has been all about the new emotions. Anxiety (voiced by Maya Hawke) is the star—a jittery, orange, wide-eyed creature who takes over headquarters and pushes Joy aside. Envy (Ayo Edebiri) is a tiny, green thing that's always jealous of others. Embarrassment (Paul Walter Hauser) is a huge, pink blob that hides in a hoodie. And Ennui (Adèle Exarchopoulos) is a bored, French-accented sloth who can't be bothered. They're all brilliant, but Anxiety is the showstopper. Hawke's performance is so good that you feel anxious just watching her.

But here's what's surprising: the film doesn't demonize Anxiety. It shows that anxiety can be useful—it helps Riley prepare for a hockey tryout and stay focused. The problem is when anxiety takes over, pushing out Joy, Sadness, and the others. It's a nuanced take on mental health that feels both honest and hopeful. I've struggled with anxiety for years, and seeing it portrayed as something that can be managed (not eliminated) was deeply moving.

The Story: Growing Up Is Messy

Riley is now 13, and she's trying to fit in with a new group of friends at school. The plot involves her trying to impress her idol, a cool older girl named Valentina. But the real story is inside her head, where Joy and the others are exiled to the back of the mind while Anxiety runs the show. The film explores themes of identity, self-doubt, and the pressure to be perfect. There's a scene where Riley's sense of self (a beautiful, glowing tree) is replaced by a tangled, thorny bush of self-criticism. It's devastating. I heard sniffles throughout the theater.

The film also introduces a new concept: "Beliefs." Each emotion can plant a belief in Riley's mind, shaping how she sees herself. Joy plants "I am a good person." Anxiety plants "I am not good enough." The climax involves Riley realizing that she can hold both beliefs at once—that she can be flawed and still worthy of love. It's a message that resonates with adults as much as kids. I'm 32, and I needed to hear it.

Why It's a Box Office Phenomenon

Part of it is timing. After a string of mediocre sequels ("Lightyear," "Elemental"), Pixar needed a win. But also, the film is tapping into a cultural moment. We're all more aware of mental health than we were a decade ago. The conversation around anxiety, especially among young people, is everywhere. "Inside Out 2" gives families a way to talk about these issues without being preachy or scary. It's a door-opener.

Also, the animation is stunning. The team at Pixar outdid themselves—the mindscape is more detailed than ever, with new locations like the "Stream of Consciousness" (a literal river of thoughts) and the "Back of the Mind" (a dusty archive of forgotten memories). The music by Andrea Datzman is beautiful, with a theme that's instantly memorable. And the voice cast is perfect: Amy Poehler is still the heart of the film as Joy, and Phyllis Smith returns as Sadness, who gets some of the best lines.

The Criticisms: Is It Too Heavy for Kids?

Some parents have complained that the film is too intense for young children. There's a scene where Anxiety induces a panic attack, complete with a whirlwind of negative thoughts and a racing heartbeat. My eight-year-old nephew was scared during that scene. I think it's fine for kids 8 and up, but younger children might struggle. Pixar has always walked the line between kid-friendly and adult-emotional, and this film leans into the latter.

Others have said the film is too similar to the first one—the emotions get separated, they go on a journey, they learn a lesson. That's fair. The structure is familiar. But the emotional beats are fresh enough that I didn't mind. It's like a good song with a familiar chorus—it still hits.

The Verdict: Should You See It?

Yes. Absolutely. Go see it in theaters, with a box of tissues. It's not just a great Pixar movie; it's a great movie, period. It made me reflect on my own inner voices, the ones that tell me I'm not good enough, and the ones that remind me I'm doing okay. It's a film that understands that growing up doesn't end at 18. We're all still figuring it out.

And hey, if you need an excuse to cry, this is a good one.
