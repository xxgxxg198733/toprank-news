5 Movies You Missed in Theaters That Are Now Streaming (And Worth Your Time)

TR
By Xiao Chao · June 11, 2026 · 7 min read

I'm a movie snob. I'll admit it. I subscribe to three film newsletters, I have a Letterboxd account with 1,400 logged films, and I've been known to argue about the merits of the long take at parties. But even I can't keep up with every release. Theaters are expensive. Streaming libraries are bloated. And the marketing machines only promote the biggest blockbusters.

So I decided to do a deep dive. I combed through the streaming catalogs of Netflix, Max, Hulu, Amazon Prime, and Apple TV+ to find films from the past year that flew under the radar but deserve your attention. These are not obscure arthouse films (though I love those too). These are accessible, entertaining, well-made movies that got lost in the shuffle. Here are five gems you probably missed.

1. "The Last Signal" (2025) — Streaming on Max

Let me start with one that genuinely surprised me. "The Last Signal" is a low-budget sci-fi film directed by a relative unknown named Lina Petrova. The premise: a radio astronomer at a remote observatory picks up a signal from deep space. But unlike "Contact" or "Arrival," the signal isn't a message — it's a countdown. The film follows the astronomer (played by Jessie Buckley in a career-best performance) as she races to understand what the signal means before time runs out.

What makes this movie special is how it uses its budget constraints as a strength. There are no giant alien ships or flashy visual effects. The tension comes from small details: a clock ticking in the background, the static on a radio, the look on Buckley's face as she realizes the implications. The script is tight — no wasted scenes, no unnecessary exposition. It's 98 minutes of pure, escalating dread.

The film premiered at Sundance 2025 to strong reviews (85% on Rotten Tomatoes) but got a limited theatrical release and disappeared quickly. It's now on Max, and I can't recommend it enough. If you liked "Ex Machina" or "Annihilation," you'll love this. It's smart, tense, and emotionally resonant. Buckley should be in the conversation for best actress of the decade.

2. "Beneath the Cypress" (2025) — Streaming on Hulu

This is a quiet, character-driven drama set in the Louisiana bayou. The story follows a young woman named Elodie (newcomer Aisha Thomas) who returns to her childhood home after her grandmother's death. She discovers that her grandmother had been secretly maintaining a network of underground tunnels used during the Civil War for the Underground Railroad. The film weaves together present-day grief with historical memory, and it's one of the most beautiful depictions of place I've seen in years.

The cinematography is stunning. The bayou is shot at golden hour, with Spanish moss hanging from cypress trees and sunlight filtering through the canopy. The director, Amélie Rousseau, is a French filmmaker who studied in Louisiana, and her outsider's perspective gives the film a fresh eye. She captures the humidity, the sounds of insects, the way the water moves — it's immersive in a way most movies aren't.

The performances are uniformly excellent. Thomas carries the film with a quiet intensity. She doesn't have long monologues; she expresses everything through her eyes and body language. The supporting cast, including a late-career turn from veteran actor Glynn Turman, adds depth. It's a slow burn, but if you let yourself sink into it, it's deeply rewarding.

Released in April 2025, it grossed only $2 million at the box office. It's now on Hulu. Give it a watch on a rainy Sunday afternoon.

3. "Dust to Dust" (2025) — Streaming on Amazon Prime

I almost skipped this one because the poster looked generic — a man in a wide-brimmed hat walking through a desert. It looked like a hundred other Westerns. But I'm glad I gave it a chance. "Dust to Dust" is a revisionist Western set in the 1880s, but with a twist: the protagonist, a former Buffalo Soldier named Ezekiel (played by the magnetic John Boyega), is searching for his brother who was kidnapped by a band of outlaws. The film follows his journey across the Southwest, where he encounters hostile terrain, corrupt lawmen, and his own traumatic past.

What sets this apart is its attention to historical detail. The film doesn't romanticize the West. It shows the brutal reality of life for Black Americans after the Civil War. The towns are dirty, the people are desperate, and violence is never glamorous. Boyega gives a performance of quiet dignity and repressed rage. There's a scene where he's denied service at a saloon and just... stands there. The camera holds on his face for a full minute. It's devastating.

The director, Deon Taylor, known for genre films like "The Intruder," shows a surprising range here. The pacing is deliberate, almost meditative. The score, by composer Tamar-kali, is haunting — a mix of blues, gospel, and ambient sounds. It's a film that stays with you.

"Dust to Dust" premiered on Amazon Prime directly (no theatrical release) in August 2025 and got lost in the algorithm. It deserves a wider audience. If you think Westerns are played out, this one will change your mind.

4. "Nocturne" (2026) — Streaming on Netflix

This is the most recent film on the list, released in February 2026. "Nocturne" is a psychological horror film about a concert pianist (Florence Pugh) who discovers that a mysterious piece of music has the power to alter reality. She becomes obsessed with perfecting it, at the cost of her relationships and her sanity.

Horror fans might be skeptical — another movie about a cursed piece of music? But trust me, this is different. The horror is not jump scares; it's atmospheric and psychological. The film uses sound design brilliantly. The music itself is a character, and the composer, Hildur Guðnadóttir (who won an Oscar for "Joker"), wrote an original piano concerto for the film that is genuinely unsettling. I had to pause the movie halfway through because I felt my heart racing.

Pugh is phenomenal. She's one of the best actors working today, and she brings a raw vulnerability to the role. You watch her descent into obsession and you can't look away. The supporting cast includes Jonathan Pryce as her cold mentor and Kaitlyn Dever as her concerned sister.

"Nocturne" got a limited theatrical release and made about $8 million. It's now on Netflix, where it's been sitting in the top 10 but not getting the critical attention it deserves. If you liked "Black Swan" or "The Perfection," watch this. It's elegant, terrifying, and beautifully made.

5. "The Quiet Year" (2025) — Streaming on Apple TV+

I'll end with a film that made me cry, which is rare. "The Quiet Year" is a drama about a married couple (Adam Driver and Greta Gerwig) living in rural Vermont who decide to spend a year without speaking to each other. It sounds like a gimmick, but it's not. The film is an exploration of communication, intimacy, and the ways we hurt the people we love without meaning to.

The year-long silence is a self-imposed experiment. The couple, both professors, are studying the psychology of nonverbal communication. But as the months pass, the silence becomes a mirror, reflecting their insecurities, resentments, and unspoken love. Driver and Gerwig are extraordinary. They act without words, using only facial expressions, gestures, and body language. There's a scene where Driver's character cooks dinner while Gerwig's character watches from the doorway. Nothing happens. It's devastating.

The director, James Ponsoldt ("The End of the Tour," "The Spectacular Now"), has a gift for capturing small moments with emotional weight. The film is shot in natural light, with long takes that let the actors inhabit the space. The Vermont setting is gorgeous — snow-covered fields, cozy farmhouse interiors, the contrast between winter isolation and summer warmth.

"The Quiet Year" premiered at TIFF 2025 to strong reviews but struggled to find an audience in theaters. It's now on Apple TV+. I recommend watching it alone, without distractions. It demands your full attention, and it rewards it.

Why These Movies Matter

I'm not saying these are the five best films of the past year. I'm saying they're the ones that deserve more eyes. The streaming landscape is overwhelming, and good movies get buried every day. The algorithm favors what's popular, not what's good. Theatrical distribution is a gamble.

But films like these — made with passion, craftsmanship, and genuine artistic intent — are worth seeking out. They remind us why we love movies in the first place. Not for the spectacle, but for the connection. The feeling of being seen. The stories that linger long after the credits roll.

So tonight, skip the latest true crime documentary or the reality show you've already seen. Try one of these. You might discover your new favorite film.
