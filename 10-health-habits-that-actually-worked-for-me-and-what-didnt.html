10 Health Habits That Actually Worked for Me (And What Didn't)

TR
By Joshua Reed · July 19, 2026 · 5 min read

Let me start with a confession: I used to hate health advice. Every influencer with a green smoothie and a sunrise photo made me roll my eyes. But last January, after my annual checkup showed my blood pressure creeping into 'high normal' territory, my doctor basically said, 'Please stop ignoring your body.' So I did something I never thought I'd do: I started actually trying things.

This list isn't about miracle cures or Instagram aesthetics. It's about what I tested, what I ditched, and what genuinely made a difference over the past 18 months. Some of this stuff surprised me. Some of it I hated. All of it is real.

1. Morning Sunlight Exposure (The One That Changed Everything)

I know, I know – every wellness guru has been shouting about this for years. But here's the thing: it actually works. Dr. Andrew Huberman, a neuroscientist at Stanford, has been the main evangelist, and I finally caved. Starting in February, I forced myself outside within 30 minutes of waking, no sunglasses, for 10 minutes on sunny days and 20 on cloudy ones.

The result? My sleep quality improved dramatically within two weeks. I fell asleep faster, woke up less during the night, and my 8 AM grogginess vanished. The science is solid: morning light sets your circadian clock. I'm not a morning person, but this one stuck. It's free, it's easy, and it works.

2. Cold Showers (Dropped After Three Weeks)

I bought into the Wim Hof hype. I really did. For three weeks every morning, I ended my shower with 60 seconds of cold water. And... I hated every second. Did I feel slightly more alert? Maybe. But the stress of bracing for that cold spike didn't feel worth it for a temporary buzz. A study in the European Journal of Applied Physiology (2023) found cold exposure can reduce inflammation, but the benefits aren't magical. I quit. No regrets.

3. Walking After Meals (Underrated)

This one came from my mom, who's been doing it for decades. I started taking a 10-minute walk after dinner, and it fixed my bloating issues almost immediately. Research from Diabetes Care (2024) shows light post-meal walking lowers blood sugar spikes by up to 30%. It's not a workout – it's a stroll. But it made me feel better, and it's easy to sustain. Two thumbs up.

4. Weighted Blankets (Surprisingly Effective)

Last November, I bought a 15-pound weighted blanket from Bearaby (the cotton knotted one, $199). I was skeptical – how can a blanket do anything? But after a week, I noticed I stopped tossing and turning. A 2022 meta-analysis in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found weighted blankets improve sleep quality for people with anxiety. I'm not anxious, but the deep pressure sensation is legit calming. I still use it every night.

5. Intermittent Fasting 16:8 (Mixed Results)

I tried 16:8 fasting from March to June. I ate between noon and 8 PM. Did I lose weight? A little – about 4 pounds. But I also felt hangry by 11 AM and had trouble concentrating during morning meetings. A 2024 study from the New England Journal of Medicine showed intermittent fasting can improve insulin sensitivity, but it's not superior to simple calorie restriction. The bottom line: it worked for weight loss but made my days miserable. I stopped.

6. Strength Training Twice a Week (Non-Negotiable)

I used to be a cardio-only person. But after reading Dr. Peter Attia's book Outlive, I started lifting weights – just two sessions per week, 30 minutes each, focusing on compound movements. Six months later, my resting heart rate dropped from 72 to 64, and my posture improved. The CDC recommends strength training twice a week for adults, and I finally see why. This one's permanent.

7. Meditation with the Calm App (On and Off)

I subscribed to Calm ($69.99/year) and did 10-minute meditations for two months. I liked it when I did it, but I kept forgetting. The app's design is beautiful, but the habit didn't stick. A 2023 study in JAMA Internal Medicine found meditation reduces anxiety, but the effect size is small. It's not a magic pill. I'm trying again with shorter sessions.

8. Cutting Out Added Sugar (Drastic but Effective)

For 30 days in April, I eliminated added sugar. No desserts, no sweetened drinks, no hidden sugar in sauces. The first week was brutal – headaches, cravings, irritability. But by week two, my energy levels stabilized, and my skin cleared up. The American Heart Association recommends no more than 25g of added sugar per day for women. I now aim for that instead of zero. It's a balance.

9. Standing Desk (Meh)

I bought a standing desk converter from Flexispot ($149.99) and used it for three months. My back pain didn't improve. A 2024 study in Ergonomics found standing desks don't reduce back pain unless combined with movement breaks. Now I use it for 30 minutes per day tops. Not worth the hype.

10. Daily Fiber Supplement (The Unsung Hero)

I started taking psyllium husk (Metamucil, $24.99 for a tub) every morning. My digestion improved, my cholesterol dropped 12 points at my last checkup (per my doctor's surprise), and I feel fuller between meals. A 2023 review in The Lancet found dietary fiber reduces risk of heart disease and diabetes. This is the most boring but effective change I made. Do it.

So there's my honest list. Some trendy, some boring. The lesson? Health isn't about doing everything – it's about finding the three or four things you can actually stick with. Try one. See if it works. Ditch what doesn't. Your body will thank you.
