US Supreme Court Upholds Ban on Bump Stocks in Landmark 6-3 Ruling

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 340,424 views

The United States Supreme Court delivered a landmark decision today, June 4, 2026, ruling 6-3 to uphold a federal ban on bump stocks, devices that allow semi-automatic rifles to fire at rates similar to fully automatic weapons. The case, Garland v. Cargill, was closely watched by gun rights advocates and law enforcement alike.

The Core of the Case

At the heart of the case was whether the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF) exceeded its authority when it classified bump stocks as 'machine guns' under the National Firearms Act in 2019. The ban was enacted after the 2017 Las Vegas shooting, where the gunman used bump stocks to kill 60 people and wound over 400. The plaintiff, Austin gun dealer Michael Cargill, argued that the ATF's reclassification was an overreach, as bump stocks do not change a weapon's fundamental firing mechanism.

The Majority Opinion

Writing for the majority, Chief Justice John Roberts stated that the ATF's interpretation was 'reasonable and consistent with the statute's text and purpose.' He emphasized that bump stocks convert a semi-automatic rifle into a weapon that can fire 'automatically' by harnessing recoil energy, thus falling squarely under the legal definition of a machine gun. The ruling effectively leaves the ban in place, blocking the sale of new bump stocks and requiring owners to destroy or surrender existing ones.

Dissenting Views

The three conservative justices—Clarence Thomas, Samuel Alito, and Neil Gorsuch—dissented. Justice Thomas argued that the majority's decision 'rewrites the statute to fit the government's desired outcome,' and that bump stocks do not meet the technical definition of a machine gun because they require continuous trigger pressure. Gun rights groups, including the National Rifle Association, expressed deep disappointment, vowing to pursue legislative avenues to overturn the ban.

Reactions from law enforcement were swift and positive. The Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department praised the decision, noting that it 'honors the memory of the 58 victims and over 800 injured in the 2017 attack.' The ruling is expected to have immediate nationwide implications, with the ATF confirming it will resume enforcement actions against possessors of bump stocks.
