Trump Convicted on All 34 Counts in New York Hush Money Trial: A Historic Verdict

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 06, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 284,116 views

In a landmark verdict that has sent shockwaves through the American political landscape, a New York jury on Thursday found former President Donald Trump guilty on all 34 counts of falsifying business records in the first degree. The verdict, delivered at the Manhattan Criminal Courthouse, marks the first time a former U.S. president has been convicted of a crime.

The Charges and the Trial

The case centered on a $130,000 hush money payment made to adult film actress Stormy Daniels in the final weeks of the 2016 presidential campaign. Prosecutors argued that Trump directed his former lawyer and fixer Michael Cohen to make the payment to silence Daniels about an alleged affair, and then falsely recorded the reimbursements as legal expenses to conceal their true purpose. The trial, which lasted six weeks, featured testimony from Cohen, Daniels, and former Trump Organization employees.

Immediate Legal and Political Fallout

Judge Juan Merchan set sentencing for July 11, just days before the Republican National Convention where Trump is expected to be formally nominated as the party's presidential candidate. Each count carries a potential sentence of up to four years in prison, though legal experts anticipate a sentence of probation or a fine given Trump's lack of prior criminal record and the non-violent nature of the offenses. Trump, maintaining his innocence, called the verdict a 'disgrace' and vowed to appeal.

Reactions and Impact on the 2024 Election

The verdict has sharply divided the nation. President Joe Biden's campaign issued a statement saying the verdict 'shows that no one is above the law,' while House Speaker Mike Johnson and other Republican leaders condemned the prosecution as politically motivated. Polls conducted immediately after the verdict show a slight tightening of the race, with Trump's support among independents dipping. The conviction could complicate Trump's ability to campaign and may become a central issue in the general election.

As the nation absorbs this unprecedented development, the focus now shifts to the sentencing phase and the potential appeals process, which could stretch well beyond the November election.
