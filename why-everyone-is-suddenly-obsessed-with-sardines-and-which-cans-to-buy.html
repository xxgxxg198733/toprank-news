Why Everyone Is Suddenly Obsessed with Sardines (And Which Cans to Buy)

TR
By TopRank Team · June 06, 2026 · 5 min read

I'll be honest: I used to think sardines were cat food. The thought of eating oily little fish from a tin made me cringe. But then I watched a documentary about Mediterranean diets, read about the health benefits, and saw my favorite food influencers raving about tinned fish. So I decided to give it a shot. I bought one can of King Oscar sardines in olive oil, opened it over a bowl of rice, and took a bite. And... wow. It was actually good. Really good. The fish was tender, the oil was rich, and the flavor was briny but not fishy.

That was three weeks ago. Since then, I've become obsessed. I've tried 15 different brands, from the $1.50 cans at the grocery store to the $12 artisan tins from specialty shops. I've eaten them on crackers, in salads, on pasta, and straight from the tin. I've converted three friends and my (very skeptical) mother. Sardines are having a moment, and for good reason. They're cheap, sustainable, packed with protein and omega-3s, and delicious when you know what to buy. Here's my ranking of the best sardines on the market right now.

1. King Oscar (Classic, in Olive Oil) — The Gold Standard

If you're new to sardines, start here. King Oscar is the most widely available brand in the US, and for a reason. The fish are firm but tender, the oil is clean and flavorful, and the price is right (about $3 per tin). I've tried their versions with lemon, with chili, and plain, and they're all excellent. The key is the quality control—every tin I've opened has had consistent, boneless, skinless fillets. No scales, no grit, no weird bits. It's the Toyota Camry of sardines: reliable, affordable, and does everything well.

2. Matiz (in Olive Oil) — The Step-Up

Matiz is a Spanish brand that costs about $5 per tin, and it's worth every penny. The fish are larger and more flavorful, packed in high-quality Galician olive oil. The texture is buttery, almost like fresh fish. I ate a tin straight with a fork, and it felt like a treat. The only downside is availability—you'll need to find a specialty store or order online. But if you want to impress someone with tinned fish, this is the one.

3. Season (in Water) — The Healthy Option

Not everyone wants the oil. Season's sardines packed in water are a great option for those watching calories or just preferring a cleaner taste. The fish are still tender, and the water is lightly salted so it's not bland. I use these in salads and pasta dishes where I don't want the oil to overwhelm. They're about $2.50 per tin and widely available. Solid choice.

4. Wild Planet — The Sustainable Choice

Wild Planet is all about sustainability—their sardines are pole-caught and packed in their own natural juices, not oil. The flavor is stronger and more "fishy" than other brands, which some people love. I like it, but I find the texture slightly drier. Still, if you care about overfishing and ocean health, this is the brand to buy. About $4 per tin.

5. Bon Appétit (in Tomato Sauce) — The Comfort Food

Okay, this is a controversial one. Sardines in tomato sauce are not for everyone. The sauce can be sweet and gloppy, and the fish get soft. But when I'm in the mood for something nostalgic—like on toast with cheese—this hits the spot. It's like a weird, fishy sloppy joe. Bon Appétit is the most common brand for this style, and it costs about $1.50. Not gourmet, but fun.

6. Bela (in Piri-Piri) — The Spice Lover's Pick

Bela is a Portuguese brand that makes sardines with a kick. The piri-piri version is packed in oil with chili peppers, and it's fantastic if you like heat. The fish are meaty and the oil is spicy enough to make your lips tingle. I had these on crusty bread with some sliced onion, and it was a meal. About $6 per tin, but worth it for a treat.

7. Patagonia Provisions (in Olive Oil) — The Ethical Splurge

Patagonia is known for outdoor gear, but they also make amazing tinned fish. Their sardines are wild-caught and packed in organic olive oil. The flavor is clean and delicate, and the fish are perfectly cooked. But at $10 per tin, it's a once-in-a-while purchase. I save these for when I want to feel fancy. They're good, but not 3x better than King Oscar.

8. Tonnino (in Olive Oil) — The Fancy One

Tonnino is the Rolex of tinned fish. Their sardines come in glass jars, packed in high-quality olive oil with lemon or chili. The fish are huge—like, absurdly large—and the texture is almost like fresh-cooked fish. I bought one jar for $12, and it was delicious, but I felt a little ridiculous eating sardines that cost more than my lunch. It's a novelty, not a staple.

9. Cole's (Smoked) — The Smoky Surprise

Cole's makes smoked sardines that are unlike anything else. The smoke flavor is intense, almost like bacon. I ate them on crackers with cream cheese, and it was incredible. But the smoky flavor can be overpowering if you're not in the mood. About $4 per tin.

10. Beach Cliff (in Mustard) — The Nostalgic Weirdo

Beach Cliff is the brand my grandpa ate. Their sardines in mustard sauce are... an acquired taste. The sauce is bright yellow and tangy, and the fish are mushy. I tried them for old times' sake, and I didn't hate it, but I wouldn't buy them again. It's a curiosity, nothing more.

How to Eat Sardines Without Looking Like a Weirdo

If you're new to sardines, don't just open a can and stare at it. Here's how I've been eating them: on sourdough toast with mashed avocado and a squeeze of lemon. Or tossed into a bowl of hot pasta with garlic, chili flakes, and parsley. Or flaked over a salad with vinaigrette. The key is to treat them like canned tuna, but better. Once you get past the mental block, you'll wonder why you ever bought tuna in the first place.
