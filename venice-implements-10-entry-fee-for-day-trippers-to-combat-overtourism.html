Venice Implements €10 Entry Fee for Day-Trippers to Combat Overtourism

TR
TopRank Editorial Team June 04, 2026 ⏱ 2 min read 198,391 views

Venice has launched its controversial new entry fee system today, June 4, 2026, becoming the first city in the world to charge day-trippers for access. The €10 fee, which applies to visitors aged 14 and older who do not stay overnight, is part of a broader effort to curb overtourism and protect the UNESCO World Heritage site from damage.

How It Works

Day-trippers must book their visit online in advance through the city's official portal, receiving a QR code that they must show at checkpoints located at the city's main entry points, including the train station, bus terminal, and Piazzale Roma. Those caught entering without a valid pass face fines of up to €300. Overnight guests are exempt, as they already pay a tourist tax included in their hotel bill. The system is initially being tested for 30 peak days in 2026, including weekends and public holidays, before potentially being expanded.

Reasons Behind the Fee

Venice, with a population of just 50,000 residents, receives an estimated 30 million visitors per year, with a significant portion arriving as day-trippers. The city's narrow streets, canals, and fragile foundations are under constant strain. 'This is not a tax on tourism; it's a tool to manage numbers,' said Venice Mayor Luigi Brugnaro. 'We want to encourage longer, more sustainable visits that benefit the city and its people.' The fee is expected to raise around €50 million annually, which will be reinvested into infrastructure, waste management, and preservation projects.

Reactions and Impact

Local businesses are divided. Some hotel owners support the fee, as it may reduce the number of budget day-trippers who spend little money, while others fear it will deter all tourists. Tourist guides report that advance bookings have been slower than usual for the summer. 'It's a hassle, but I understand why they're doing it,' said Canadian tourist Emma Wilson, who purchased her pass online. 'Venice is a living museum; we have to protect it.' The system is being closely watched by other historic cities, including Barcelona, Dubrovnik, and Amsterdam, which are considering similar measures. For now, Venice is charting a new course in the global fight against overtourism.
