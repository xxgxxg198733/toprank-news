The 10 Best Movies Streaming in July 2026 (Netflix, HBO, Amazon, and More)

TR
By Hannah Powell · July 22, 2026 · 5 min read

Streaming in 2026 is overwhelming. There's so much content that choosing what to watch feels like work. I get it. That's why I've made it my mission to wade through the sludge so you don't have to. This month, I watched 22 new releases across Netflix, HBO Max, Amazon Prime, Apple TV+, and Hulu. Most were forgettable. But these 10? They're genuinely good.

I'm not going to pretend I liked everything I watched. I didn't. Some critically acclaimed movies bored me to tears. But these picks are the ones that made me laugh, cry, or think — sometimes all three. Here's the honest list.

1. 'The Last Signal' (Netflix) — Best Sci-Fi of the Year

I went into this expecting a generic alien invasion movie. What I got was a quiet, philosophical film about a radio astronomer who receives a signal from a distant galaxy — but it's not what she expected. The movie is slow, deliberate, and emotionally devastating. The lead performance by Greta Lee is Oscar-worthy. It's not for everyone — my partner fell asleep — but if you like thoughtful sci-fi like Arrival or Contact, you'll love this. Streaming now on Netflix.

2. 'Heist in the Clouds' (Amazon Prime) — Pure Fun

Sometimes you just want a fun heist movie. This one is set on a luxury airship (yes, like a blimp) traveling from London to New York. The cast is stacked: Oscar Isaac, Zendaya, and John Boyega. The plot is ridiculous in the best way — they're stealing a rare painting while dodging a mysterious assassin. It's stylish, funny, and has one of the best chase scenes I've seen in years. Not deep, but who cares? It's a blast.

3. 'The Quiet Room' (HBO Max) — Horror That Stays With You

Horror fans, this one's for you. A woman inherits a remote cabin in the Pacific Northwest, only to discover that one room in the house is completely silent — no sound enters or leaves. The movie uses silence as a weapon. I watched it alone at night and had to turn on all the lights afterwards. The ending is ambiguous in a way that will frustrate some people, but I loved it. Director Ari Aster (Midsommar) produced it, and you can feel his influence.

Trigger warning: there's a scene involving a child that's deeply unsettling. Not for the faint of heart.

4. 'The Baker's Son' (Apple TV+) — Best Indie Drama

This is the kind of small, quiet movie that streaming services bury in the algorithm. Please seek it out. It's about a baker in rural Portugal who discovers a letter from his late father revealing a family secret. The movie is essentially a meditation on grief, food, and the things we inherit. The bread-making scenes are filmed with such care that you can almost smell it. I cried three times. It's beautiful.

Streaming on Apple TV+ and absolutely worth your time.

5. 'Neon Nights' (Hulu) — The Gangster Movie We Needed

There's a lot of gangster movies out there, but this one feels fresh. It's set in 1980s Tokyo, following a young American soldier who gets pulled into the yakuza underworld. The cinematography is stunning — neon lights, rain-slicked streets, smoky bars. The violence is brutal but not gratuitous. The lead, newcomer Kenji Tanaka, is a revelation. It's long (2 hours 40 minutes) but never drags. Reminded me of The Departed crossed with Drive.

6. 'The Great Escape 2' (Netflix) — Surprisingly Good Sequel

I was skeptical. The original 2024 film was a surprise hit, and sequels usually suck. But this one works. It's a completely different story — same universe, new characters — about a group of prisoners escaping from a Siberian gulag in 1953. The cast is excellent (I saw Florence Pugh and Dev Patel), and the tension is relentless. It's 2 hours of pure adrenaline. My heart was pounding the whole time. Don't watch it before bed.

7. 'Love in Translation' (Paramount+) — Best Rom-Com of 2026

Rom-coms have been in a slump for years. This one breaks the streak. Two strangers — a translator and a diplomat — meet at a UN conference in Vienna and spend a weekend together, each pretending to be someone else. The dialogue is sharp, the chemistry is electric, and the Vienna setting is gorgeous. It's funny, sweet, and surprisingly smart about the nature of language and misunderstanding. My wife and I watched it twice in one week.

8. 'The Algorithm' (HBO Max) — Creepy Tech Thriller

A tech thriller that actually understands how AI works? Shocking. A data scientist at a social media company discovers that the recommendation algorithm is predicting deaths before they happen. The movie is tense, paranoid, and eerily plausible. The third act goes in a direction I didn't expect — it's not a simple 'shut down the AI' story. It's about what happens when we can't look away from the truth. One of the smartest movies I've seen this year.

9. 'The Last Dance 2' (Netflix) — Better Than the Original?

The documentary series about Michael Jordan's final Bulls season was a phenomenon. This new series follows LeBron James' final season with the Lakers — but it's not just a sports doc. It's about legacy, aging, and what it means to say goodbye. LeBron is surprisingly vulnerable on camera. The behind-the-scenes footage is incredible. Even if you're not a basketball fan, it's compelling television. All 8 episodes are streaming now.

10. 'Phantom Signal' (Amazon Prime) — The Hidden Gem

This is the movie nobody's talking about, and that's a shame. It's a low-budget British thriller about a radio pirate who accidentally intercepts a classified transmission. The entire movie takes place in one room, with the protagonist trying to decode the message while being hunted. It's tense, clever, and proves you don't need a big budget to make a great movie. The lead, David Oyelowo, gives a masterclass in acting. Seek this one out.

So there you go — 10 movies worth your time this month. Skip the algorithm's recommendations and watch these instead. You can thank me later.
