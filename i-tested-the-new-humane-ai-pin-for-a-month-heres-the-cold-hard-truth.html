I Tested the New Humane AI Pin for a Month – Here's the Cold, Hard Truth

TR
By Robert Martinez · July 17, 2026 · 5 min read

The Hype Machine

Remember when Humane debuted the AI Pin in late 2023? The internet went wild. A wearable that projects a laser display onto your hand, runs on AI, and promises to free you from your phone? It sounded like science fiction. Then they actually shipped it in early 2025. And it was… a mess. Reviews were mixed. Battery life was terrible. The laser display was hard to see. But Humane didn't give up. They've been iterating, and in June 2026, they released the second-generation Humane AI Pin, which they claim fixes all the issues. I decided to give it a fair shot. I wore it every day for 30 days. I used it as my primary device. Here's what happened.

I want to be clear: I'm not a hater. I genuinely want this technology to succeed. The idea of a phone-free future is appealing. I hate being glued to my screen. But the AI Pin has to actually work. And after a month, I have complicated feelings.

What's New in the 2026 Model

The new Pin is physically similar – a small square that clips to your shirt or jacket. It weighs about 34 grams, so you forget it's there. The big upgrade is the projection system. The old model had a green laser that was barely visible indoors. The new one uses a microLED projector that's brighter and supports full color. It's better, but still not great. In direct sunlight, it's invisible. Inside, it's usable but the resolution is low – about 480p equivalent. You can see simple text and icons, but don't expect to read an article or watch a video. The other upgrade is the battery. It now lasts about 8 hours of mixed use, up from 4. That's enough for a workday, but barely. You still need to charge it with a magnetic puck that's easy to lose.

The Software Experience

The AI Pin runs CosmOS, Humane's own operating system. It's entirely voice and gesture controlled. You tap the device to activate the AI, then speak your command. It can send texts, make calls, set reminders, answer questions, and control smart home devices. The AI is powered by a combination of GPT-4o and Humane's own models. In theory, it should be amazing. In practice, it's frustrating.

First, the voice recognition is good but not great. In a quiet room, it works 90% of the time. In a noisy coffee shop or walking down the street, it drops to 60%. I found myself repeating commands constantly. The AI responses are slow – there's a 2-3 second delay after you speak. That might not sound like much, but it feels like an eternity when you're used to instant responses from your phone. The gesture control (swiping on the device to navigate menus) is fiddly. The touch surface is tiny, and I'd often swipe the wrong way. The laser projection shows a simple menu with icons for messaging, music, weather, etc. You tap to select, swipe to scroll. It's not intuitive. I never got used to it.

What It Does Well

Okay, let's be fair. There are things the AI Pin does genuinely well. The voice calling is excellent. I used it to make calls while driving, and the person on the other end said I sounded clear. The AI transcription is impressive – it can transcribe your voice messages and send them as text. The notification system is also nice: it taps you on the shoulder (literally, with a haptic) to alert you to messages, and you can respond with a quick voice reply. It's less distracting than a phone. I also liked using it for quick AI queries. Asking "How many ounces in a cup?" or "What's the weather?" is faster than pulling out your phone. The camera is surprisingly good. It's a 12MP sensor that takes decent photos. The AI can also identify objects and plants – I used it to identify a mushroom in my garden. That was neat.

The Big Problems

But here's where it falls apart. The laser display is a gimmick. It's cool for showing a text message preview, but anything beyond that is painful. You can't browse the web on it. You can't see photos. You can't use maps (it gives turn-by-turn voice directions, but that's it). The AI is too limited. It can't access most apps. Want to check your Instagram? Nope. Want to use WhatsApp? Only if you set it up through a companion app. The Pin feels like a smartwatch from 2015, not a phone replacement. The battery life is still a joke. 8 hours is not enough for a full day if you use it heavily. I had to charge it at lunch. And the worst part: it's hot. After 30 minutes of use, the Pin gets uncomfortably warm against your chest. I got a mild burn after a long phone call. That's not okay.

I also have privacy concerns. The Pin is always listening for the wake word (you can turn it off, but then it's less useful). Humane says data is encrypted, but still. The Pin has a camera that faces forward – it can record your surroundings. I felt weird wearing it in public, like I was being surveilled. And the social aspect is odd. People stared at me when I talked to my chest. I had to explain what it was constantly. It's not discreet.

Who Should Buy This?

Honestly, almost nobody. The AI Pin is an interesting experiment, but it's not ready for mainstream use. It's expensive – $699 for the device plus a $30/month subscription for the AI service. That's more than a mid-range phone. If you're a tech enthusiast or a developer, it's fun to play with. If you want to reduce screen time, it might help, but you'll still need your phone for many tasks. For everyone else, stick with your phone. The AI Pin is a solution in search of a problem. I wanted to love it. I really did. But after a month, I'm putting it in a drawer and going back to my Galaxy S26. Maybe in another few years, this technology will be viable. But for now, it's a cool demo with frustrating limitations.

Here's my final take: if someone offered you a free AI Pin, take it. It's fun for a week. But don't spend your own money on it. Not yet.
